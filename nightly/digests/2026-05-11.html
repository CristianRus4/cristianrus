The Body Keeps Its Own Archive

Before the mind has named an experience, the body has already filed it. The filing system does not use language. It uses tension, posture, the particular depth of a breath.

Predictive processing theory suggests that what we experience as perception is largely a prediction being confirmed or corrected. The brain sends expectations downward and receives corrections from the senses. We do not see the world as it is; we see the world as we expect it to be, minus the errors we could not ignore.

Fatigue is not simply the depletion of a resource. It is a model the body builds of what it can safely attempt. A fatigued person does not merely have less capacity — they have a different map of what is possible.

The body holds postures that were useful once and keeps them past usefulness. A protective contraction can outlast the threat by years. The muscles remember an event the mind has classified as resolved.

What the body knows and what the mind has agreed to are not always the same document. The gap between them is not a pathology. It is a kind of archive — partial, slow, resistant to revision.

The nervous system has opinions the conscious mind has not been consulted about.

A FRAGMENT

She noticed the weight in her shoulders only when someone asked her to sit down. She had been carrying it since morning, since before morning. It had become the ordinary. She sat. The weight did not leave. But sitting made it possible to feel it as something separate from herself, rather than as herself. That was new. She filed it without a name.

A PERSON

Françoise Dastur

1942 – present — Paris, France

Françoise Dastur is a French phenomenologist whose work has centred on the relationship between time, embodiment, and mortality. Drawing on Husserl and Heidegger, she developed a detailed analysis of how the anticipation of death shapes the experience of living time — not as something morbid but as the condition that makes personal time possible at all.

Her work on the body as a site of temporal experience challenged the Cartesian assumption that the mind is the primary organ of temporal awareness. She argued that the body's rhythms, fatigues, and recoveries constitute a form of knowledge that precedes and shapes what consciousness can notice. Her influence is quiet and wide.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • What is your body telling you right now that your mind has not yet acknowledged?

    How long has it been saying it?

    Scan your body from feet to crown tonight and describe, without interpretation, what you find.

  • Which of your habitual postures or tensions were shaped by situations that are no longer current?

    What would it cost to let the posture go?

    Adopt a different posture for ten minutes and observe what changes.

  • What does the body know about a decision before the mind has finished deliberating?

    Have you learned to trust or distrust that knowledge?

    Make a small decision tonight by attending only to how each option feels in the chest or stomach.

In predictive processing theory, what is the technical term for the discrepancy between the brain's prediction and sensory input?

In predictive processing, the brain continually generates predictions about incoming sensory signals. A prediction error is the mismatch signal — the difference between what was expected and what arrived — which drives model updating and, when persistent, shapes experience.
