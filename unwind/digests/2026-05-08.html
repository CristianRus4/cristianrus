How Minimalism Became Its Own Kind of Noise

There is a version of minimalism that is simply austerity wearing aesthetic clothing. It removes things in order to perform the removal, to signal a relationship to objects that is more curated than the objects themselves. This version has been photographed extensively.

The minimalism that actually changes how a space feels operates differently. It is not the subtraction of things but the clarification of attention. When a room contains only what is necessary, the necessity itself becomes legible — you can see what the space is for, because nothing is competing with that purpose.

Restraint in design is not the same as emptiness. A composition can be sparse and still be dense with intention. The intervals in a piece by Arvo Pärt carry as much weight as the notes — the silence is not rest but the sound arriving from a different direction.

The problem with minimalism as culture is that it became an object of desire. Once it is desired, it is collected. The person who owns sixteen carefully chosen objects owns sixteen objects. The relationship to accumulation has not changed; only the objects have been replaced with objects that signal the correct relationship.

Coherence is something different. Coherence is when the parts of a thing are in honest conversation with each other — when nothing is present that contradicts the rest, and nothing is absent that the rest requires. You cannot purchase coherence. It has to be arrived at.

What the actual practice of minimalism demands is a continuous willingness to question whether what remains is still earning its place. This is not aesthetic — it is an ethical practice applied to the domain of things. And like most ethical practices, it is not finished.

THE TENSION

Subtraction is one of the few reliable ways to restore attention because excess fragments perception before thought even begins. A coherent life often has to be made by removal before it can be made by addition.

Subtraction becomes suspect the moment it is admired too much, because then emptiness hardens into style and style starts accumulating its own objects. Minimalism can become clutter in ceremonial form, with absence itself treated as a collectible.

THE NUMBER

93

The number of items John Pawson — architect of one of the most studied minimalist interior traditions — reported keeping in his entire London home at the time of a 2011 interview, including furniture.

A FRAGMENT

She moved three times in two years. Each time she left behind things she had carried from the previous flat. By the third move she owned what could be carried in a single trip. She noticed that the things she kept were not the things she had bought most carefully. They were the ones she had never thought about keeping. They had stayed without being chosen.

THE WORD TO KNOW

wabi

Japanese — the first of two components in wabi-sabi; derived from the verb wabu, meaning to feel alone or disappointed; evolved through Zen aesthetics to describe the beauty inherent in transience, incompleteness, and humble imperfection. Not sadness — the acceptance that produces a particular kind of openness.

The serene, unpretentious beauty of things that do not announce themselves; the aesthetic of imperfection accepted without apology.

Across Europe

On 8 May 1945, cities across Europe erupted in bells, crowds, sirens, singing, and relief as Victory in Europe Day was announced. Public noise can be a form of moral clarity: not clutter but release, the sound made when history finally loosens its grip.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Donald Judd

1928 – 1994 · Excelsior Springs, USA / Marfa, Texas

Donald Judd rejected the label 'minimalist' for his own work, insisting on the term 'specific objects' — things that were neither painting nor sculpture but occupied three-dimensional space on their own terms, without reference to anything beyond their own dimensions, material, and placement. His boxes of anodised aluminium and Plexiglas were not statements about reduction but about the integrity of an object that owed nothing to the tradition it declined to continue.

He eventually acquired an abandoned army fort in Marfa, Texas, where he spent decades permanently installing his own work and the work of others in converted artillery sheds — arguing that permanent installation was the only honest relationship between an object and a space. The work could not be moved without being destroyed. He died having made one of the most coherent bodies of work of the twentieth century, at a scale large enough to contain a landscape.

NOW HERE

Ryōan-ji

Kyoto, Japan

Fifteen stones in raked gravel, and from no single vantage can all fifteen be seen at once. The garden refuses completion without seeming to refuse anything at all. It demonstrates a severe kind of generosity: space arranged so that what is absent keeps doing part of the work.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    What in your environment has earned its place, and what is present simply because it was never removed?

    What would change if you applied the question to relationships, commitments, or habits rather than objects?

    Walk through one room tonight and touch each object. Ask: is this here because I chose it, or because I never chose to remove it?

  • 2

    What is the difference between coherence and control, and which one are you actually after?

    Can coherence be achieved without knowing in advance what the whole will look like?

    Identify one area of your life where you want coherence rather than simplicity. Describe what honest conversation between its parts would require.

  • 3

    What does the silence in music you love reveal about the presence of silence in other domains?

    Are there areas of your thinking where you have not allowed the interval?

    Sit with one piece of music tonight and pay attention only to the rests — the moments before the next note arrives.
