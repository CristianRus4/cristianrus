Why the Ancients Had No Word for Blue

The colour of the open sea, in the Iliad, is the colour of wine. The phrase is repeated often enough that it cannot be dismissed as a single poetic flourish: oinops pontos, the wine-faced sea. Homer's oxen are wine-coloured. Iron, too, is sometimes wine-coloured. Honey, in the same poems, is green. There is no word in the Iliad or the Odyssey for the colour we call blue. There is no word for it in the Hebrew Bible, nor in the early Vedas, nor in the Chinese classics of the same period. The most common colour in nature — the sky — appears to have been, for the people who first sang the foundational epics of Western and Eastern literature, not a colour at all.

The British prime minister William Gladstone noticed this in 1858 and dedicated a long chapter of his three-volume study of Homer to documenting it. His conclusion, which was rejected at the time and slowly accepted over the following century, was that the colour vocabulary of the Homeric Greeks was genuinely impoverished compared to that of modern Europeans, and that this impoverishment was a real feature of how they perceived the world. They saw the sea. They saw it accurately. What they did not have was the category that would have allowed them to name what they were seeing as a distinct kind of thing.

The German philologist Lazarus Geiger extended the observation through dozens of ancient languages and noticed a pattern. Languages acquire colour terms in a predictable order: first black and white, then red, then either yellow or green, and only at the end blue. Blue is the last colour to be named because it is, in nature, the rarest. Almost nothing is blue. There are no blue mammals, very few blue plants, almost no blue food. The blue of the sky is a quality of distance, not of substance. The blue of the sea is borrowed from the sky. For a culture without lapis lazuli or indigo or any other source of stable blue pigment, there was simply no occasion to develop the vocabulary, because the world around them did not insist on it.

The further claim — that the absence of the word affected the perception itself — has been tested experimentally in the last twenty years, and the answer appears to be a qualified yes. The Himba people of northern Namibia have several distinct words for shades of green and only one umbrella term that covers what English speakers would divide into blue and several greens. In sorting tasks, the Himba detect distinctions within the green spectrum more quickly than English speakers do, and have measurable difficulty isolating a blue tile from a group of green ones. The vocabulary does not determine perception, but it shapes the resolution at which the perceiver attends.

What this suggests is that the relationship between a culture and its world is not a one-way correspondence in which language names a pre-existing reality. The vocabulary determines what counts as a thing worth naming, and what counts as worth naming determines what gets noticed, and what gets noticed determines what gets refined, until eventually the categories of the language become the joints at which the world is felt to be naturally cut. The world is not the same world for a person with five words for snow as for a person with one. The difference is not merely linguistic.

The further unsettling implication is that the colours we cannot name are still there, in our own perceptual field, going unmarked. The Himba perceive distinctions we cannot. There are almost certainly distinctions other cultures, or future cultures, will name that we currently see as undifferentiated noise. The world we take to be obvious is, in part, an artefact of the words we happened to inherit. The blue we now find self-evident was, for our great-great-grandparents many times over, an absence that did not present itself as an absence — because the absence had no shape, and a shape without a name does not appear as a missing thing.

THE TENSION

The vocabulary genuinely shapes what is perceived. The Himba sorting experiments and the documented sequence in which languages acquire colour terms suggest that perception itself — not merely the report of perception — depends on the categories the perceiver has available. To say otherwise is to ignore decades of cross-linguistic experimental psychology that has confirmed Whorfian effects at the level of pre-conscious attention.

The vocabulary may shape attention without shaping perception, and the temptation to slide from one to the other is a category error that empirical work has often overstated. Homer's contemporaries saw the sky exactly as we do; they simply did not bother to name a colour with few practical implications for their daily lives. To attribute deep cognitive difference to lexical absence is to mistake the map for the territory in the opposite direction — to claim that what is unnamed is not seen.

THE NUMBER

11

The number of basic colour categories identified by Brent Berlin and Paul Kay in their 1969 cross-linguistic study — a hierarchy that appears in roughly the same order across the world's languages, with blue arriving consistently late and orange, pink, and grey arriving later still.

A FRAGMENT

He had been a translator for thirty years and still kept the question in a notebook he no longer showed anyone: what colour was the sky for the woman who wrote this sentence. The original gave no word for it. The translation he had produced gave 'pale blue' because the editor required a colour and 'no colour at all' was not English. He suspected he had made a small invisible mistake several thousand times. He suspected, also, that there was no version in which he would not have.

THE WORD TO KNOW

azzurro

/adˈdzurro/

Italian, derived from the Old French 'azur,' itself a borrowing from the Medieval Latin 'azurum' / 'lazurium,' which in turn derives from the Persian 'lāžaward,' the original name of the place — somewhere in the mountains of what is now Afghanistan — from which lapis lazuli was mined. The colour name follows the trade route of the stone: a deep, slightly violet blue that became the most precious pigment available to medieval European painters, reserved for the robes of the Virgin Mary because of its expense and its perceived sanctity. The word names a colour but also carries, in its etymology, the geography of how that colour first became economically and aesthetically available to the European eye.

The bright, mid-saturation blue of the Italian sky and sea on a clear day; in Italian usage, the standard term for the colour we would call simply 'blue' in English, but carrying connotations of clarity, warmth, and Mediterranean light that the cooler English word does not convey. Distinct in Italian from 'blu' (a darker, deeper blue) and 'celeste' (a paler, sky-toned blue) — a tripartite distinction Italian preserves and most other European languages do not.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Anna Wierzbicka

1938 – present · Warsaw, Poland / Canberra, Australia

Anna Wierzbicka is a Polish-Australian linguist whose work on the relationship between language and thought constitutes one of the most ambitious projects in the second half of the twentieth century to take seriously the claim that different languages organise the world differently. Working primarily from the Australian National University since 1973, she has developed what she calls the Natural Semantic Metalanguage — a set of approximately sixty-five 'semantic primes' that she argues are present in every human language and can be used to define every culturally-specific concept in terms a non-speaker can genuinely understand. Her early work documented the radical untranslatability of emotion words across languages, demonstrating, for instance, that the Polish 'tęsknota' is not 'longing' and the Russian 'toska' is not 'melancholy' but irreducibly different emotional categories with their own cultural histories.

Wierzbicka's complication is that her insistence on the cognitive reality of cross-cultural difference has placed her at odds with most of mainstream cognitive science and analytic philosophy, which has tended to assume that human concepts are fundamentally universal and that linguistic variation is surface decoration. Her response, accumulated across more than thirty books, has been to keep producing detailed analyses of the conceptual structures of specific languages — Russian, Japanese, English, Polish, several Aboriginal Australian languages — and to argue that the assumption of universality is itself an artefact of doing philosophy primarily in English. The work is enormous, technically demanding, and substantially less famous outside specialist circles than its scope warrants, perhaps because its conclusions are uncomfortable in a way that mainstream linguistics has not been ready to absorb.

NOW HERE

Cappella Scrovegni

Padua, Italy

A small fourteenth-century chapel commissioned by a wealthy banker as expiation for his father's usury, and decorated entirely by Giotto between 1303 and 1305 with a fresco cycle of the lives of Mary and Christ. The ceiling is one of the most saturated surviving examples of medieval ultramarine — a vault of lapis-lazuli blue dotted with eight-pointed gold stars, the pigment imported at enormous expense from the Sar-i Sang mines in what is now Badakhshan, Afghanistan. The chapel is climate-controlled and admission is timed; visitors enter in groups of twenty-five for fifteen minutes, having first spent fifteen minutes in an antechamber to acclimate the humidity. The blue overhead has not faded substantially in seven centuries. It is the colour the word 'azzurro' was made for: a blue that announces itself before anything else in the room is registered, and that, having entered the visual field, organises everything else into its weather.

ON THIS DAY

1953

Westminster Abbey, London, United Kingdom

On 2 June 1953, Elizabeth II was crowned in Westminster Abbey in the first coronation broadcast to a mass television audience — an estimated 27 million people watched in the United Kingdom alone, on screens whose colour reproduction was still more than a decade away. The Queen's robes were of crimson velvet and white ermine; the imperial state crown carried the Cullinan II diamond and the Black Prince's ruby; the entire visual logic of the event was organised around colour saturation that the medium broadcasting it could not transmit. What hundreds of millions of people saw — and what would constitute the dominant collective memory of the coronation for at least a generation — was a black-and-white version of an event built entirely around colour, a kind of negative experiment in the proposition that the world the eye perceives is the world that gets remembered.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a category in your own perception of the world that you suspect is shaped by a word you happen to have inherited — a distinction that feels natural but might dissolve if you tried to make it in a language that lacked the term?

    Could you name a thing your language does not name, and would the naming feel like recognition or like invention? What would it cost to maintain the new category against a culture that does not share it?

    Spend a day attempting to perceive a familiar scene using a language you know less well than your native one. Notice which distinctions become harder to make, and which become unexpectedly available because the borrowed language carves the world differently.

  • 2

    What in your daily perceptual experience is, like blue for the ancients, present but unmarked — visible but uncategorised, real but unnamed?

    Is there a domain in which you have, over time, acquired a new vocabulary and noticed your perception sharpen accordingly — wine, music, light, plants, faces, code? And does the new vocabulary feel like access or like confinement?

    Pick a category of thing you encounter often but cannot name precisely — types of clouds, varieties of bird call, kinds of grief. Learn five real terms for it this week. Track whether the world looks different by Friday.

  • 3

    If language genuinely shapes attention, what responsibility does that place on the words you use — and on the words your culture chooses to introduce or to retire?

    Are there terms in current circulation that you believe are reshaping perception in ways that worry you, or terms you wish existed because their absence is shaping perception by default? And how would you tell the difference between a useful new category and an artificial one that does not survive the test of use?

    Read a piece of writing — an essay, a poem, a paragraph — in a language you read with effort rather than fluency. Notice the categories the writer takes for granted that English would not, and the categories English would supply that this writer ignores. Sit with what the difference reveals.

MOVIE TIME

The Color of Pomegranates

Sergei Parajanov · 1969

Parajanov's biographical poem of the eighteenth-century Armenian troubadour Sayat-Nova, composed almost entirely of static tableaux saturated with the specific reds and blues of medieval Armenian manuscripts. The film abandons narrative for a sequence of images in which colour is the primary carrier of meaning — pomegranate red, lapis blue, the gold of liturgical objects — and a Western viewer's eye is asked to learn a colour grammar it does not already possess. Banned for years by Soviet authorities for being incomprehensible, the film is a working demonstration of how much of what looks like style in a culturally distant work is in fact a different vocabulary for the visible world.

Game answer

ultramarine

The name means 'beyond the sea' — the pigment had to be shipped across the Mediterranean from the eastern trade routes, and Europeans named the colour for the journey it required to reach them.
