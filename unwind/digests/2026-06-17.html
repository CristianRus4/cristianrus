The Word for Longing That Has No English Equivalent

Every Romanian eventually finds themselves, in a foreign-language conversation, attempting to translate a single one-syllable word from their own language and arriving, after several minutes of approximation, at a paragraph. The word is not technical. It is not philosophical. It is among the first words a Romanian child learns. It appears in folk songs, in pop songs, in the speech of grandmothers, in the speech of teenagers, in poetry written eight hundred years ago and in poetry written last week. And yet to a non-Romanian who has not lived inside the word, the explanation always seems to be missing something, and the Romanian explaining always knows that what they are saying is, at best, partial.

Dor is the Romanian noun for the longing for someone or something absent. The dictionary translations — 'longing,' 'yearning,' 'pining,' 'missing' — each catch a fragment of the word's territory and miss most of it. Dor is not the longing of a child for an absent parent, though it includes that. Dor is not the homesickness of an emigrant, though it includes that. Dor is not the romantic longing of a lover for an absent beloved, though it includes that. Dor is the larger condition of which all of these are local instances: the awareness that something one is constitutively in relation to is currently not present, and that the absence is itself a presence in the bearer's daily life.

What gives dor its specific Romanian texture is its ordinariness. The word is not a literary flourish or a folkloric special category. It is the everyday Romanian noun for a state Romanians simply assume any reasonable person will be in, off and on, for most of their adult life. The grandmother in a village in Maramureș has dor for her son who works in Italy. The Bucharest professional has dor for the village where her grandmother lives. The recent emigrant in Madrid has dor for the city where she grew up. The retired man in Cluj has dor for his wife, who died twelve years ago. The Romanian language gives all of these the same word, because Romanian has decided that they are all, at the base, the same thing.

The folk-song tradition in Romanian — the doina, the long melancholic solo song characteristic of Romanian rural music — is the artistic form most fully developed around dor. The doina is not narrative. It does not tell a story. It holds a single emotional state across the duration of the song, often through musical phrases that bend and return without resolution, and the state it holds is almost always some inflection of dor. The form has been carried by shepherds, by women working in fields, by old men at funerals, by Romanian intellectuals at urban dinner parties; it has been arranged for concert performance by Béla Bartók and Constantin Brăiloiu; it has been recorded by Maria Tănase and by Grigore Leșe and continues to be sung in versions that the performers themselves describe as inherited rather than composed. What the doina holds is the structure of dor: a longing that does not move toward resolution, that the singer does not propose to escape, and that the surrounding culture treats not as a problem but as a basic feature of being alive.

What this implies, considered from inside, is that Romanian culture has a different default expectation about the texture of a human life than the dominant Anglo-Saxon culture currently does. The Anglo-Saxon assumption — visible in self-help, in therapy, in popular psychology, in advertising — is that the appropriate steady state of a well-functioning person is a kind of resolved contentment, in which absences have been integrated, longings have been resolved or replaced, and the person is fully present in the present. The Romanian assumption is that a person who is in any way alive will continuously carry several large absences, that the absences will produce a low chronic ache called dor, and that the ache is not a sign of dysfunction but the form a developed inner life takes in a person who has loved anything.

What resists in the popular Western reception of dor is the temptation to translate the word as a piece of national melancholy in need of cheering up. The translation misses both the depth of the concept and the dignity of the surrounding culture. Dor is not depression. It is not even sadness. It is the quiet recognition that one's interior is shaped by the people, places, and conditions one has loved and that are no longer immediately available, and that the shaping is permanent and not particularly negotiable. The Romanians who have spent their lives inside the word have not been suffering in need of relief. They have been holding, with the small lexical help their language gives them, a piece of the truth about what it is to be a person who has been alive for any length of time.

THE TENSION

Dor names a basic feature of mature human experience that the dominant Anglo-Saxon vocabulary has substantially failed to articulate: that absence is a permanent texture of any developed inner life and that the appropriate response is not the elimination of the absence but the recognition that it is constitutive of who one has become. The Romanian word gives its speakers a quiet daily access to a piece of the truth about being a person that many other languages oblige their speakers to circumlocate or to ignore.

The valorisation of dor risks underwriting a cultural disposition toward melancholy that is not always benign and that has, at various periods in Romanian history, been weaponised by political movements claiming to represent the authentic national sensibility. Not every form of longing is healthy, not every absence should be carried indefinitely, and the elevation of dor as a national virtue can shade into the romanticisation of suffering in ways that have served some Romanians badly across the twentieth century.

THE NUMBER

3 million

Approximate number of Romanian citizens currently living and working outside the country's borders — primarily in Italy, Spain, Germany, and the United Kingdom — and on whose continuing transmission of dor across borders, kitchens, and intergenerational households the contemporary practical life of the word substantially depends.

A FRAGMENT

She was making sarmale for the third time that month. The recipe was her grandmother's; she had been taught it as a child in the kitchen in Bistrița, and she had not made it for the first fifteen years she had lived in Madrid because the cabbage was different and the rice was wrong and her grandmother was dead. She had finally given in and made them with what was available, and they had been almost right. Her daughter, born in Spain, had eaten them politely and asked what they were called. She had told her, in Romanian, and her daughter had repeated the word and gone back to her phone. The dor she felt at that moment was not for her grandmother. It was for the daughter she was raising, who would never quite contain what she herself contained, and whose own daughter, in turn, would inherit only the shape of the absence without its content.

THE WORD TO KNOW

dor

/dor/

Romanian, from the Latin 'dolus' (pain) via the Vulgar Latin 'dolere' (to grieve). The semantic shift from 'pain' to 'longing' is specific to the Romanian development of the inherited Latin vocabulary and is not paralleled in the other major Romance languages, which use distinct words derived from different Latin roots for the equivalent emotional territory (Italian 'nostalgia,' Spanish 'añoranza,' French 'manque'). The Romanian word is in continuous use from the earliest written attestations of the language and remains one of the most semantically rich nouns in the everyday Romanian lexicon, with cognate forms in the related Aromanian, Megleno-Romanian, and Istro-Romanian dialects spoken across the Balkans.

The longing for someone or something absent — a person, a place, a time, a state — held as a quiet permanent presence in the bearer's daily life. Distinct from any of its English approximations in the breadth of its semantic range, which encompasses parental, filial, romantic, geographic, and temporal longing within a single word, and in its cultural status as the standard Romanian term for what the language treats as a basic recurrent feature of adult life.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Maria Tănase

1913 – 1963 · Bucharest, Romania

Maria Tănase was the Romanian singer whose recordings and live performances between 1937 and her early death in 1963 substantially defined the modern reception of Romanian folk music — both within Romania and, increasingly through the second half of the twentieth century, internationally. Trained as a theatre actress and largely self-taught as a folk singer, she gathered songs from across the regional traditions of the country (the Banat, Oltenia, Moldova, Maramureș) and performed them with a voice and an interpretive intelligence that the surrounding tradition had not previously housed in a single performer. The doinas, ballads, and lyrical songs she recorded between 1937 and 1962 are the most-cited single archive of dor in twentieth-century Romanian musical culture.

Tănase's complication is that her career, which began in the inter-war period of the Romanian monarchy and continued through the early communist regime, required her to navigate a series of political environments each of which made conflicting demands on what a Romanian folk artist should represent. Briefly forbidden to record by the wartime government for her connections with Western diplomats and Jewish friends, later instrumentalised by the communist regime as an example of authentic peasant culture, she preserved through it all a personal artistic seriousness that her surrounding political authorities did not always know what to do with. She died at fifty of lung cancer, before the international interest in Romanian folk music that her recordings would substantially produce, and the continuing presence of her voice in Romanian musical consciousness has had to do most of its work in her absence.

NOW HERE

Hoia Forest near Cluj

Cluj County, Transylvania, Romania

A spruce and beech forest on the western edge of the city of Cluj-Napoca, draped across the rolling Transylvanian terrain and intersected by walking paths that the inhabitants of the city have used for at least two centuries as the nearest accessible escape into something not built. The forest is locally famous for its supposed paranormal qualities, which have given it a small reputation in international UFOlogy circles, but the more significant fact about it for most of the people who use it is that it is one of the principal sites in the city where dor for the rural Romania of the speaker's grandparents' generation can be temporarily approached. The forest stands as a continuous green presence at the edge of the post-industrial provincial capital, and the walks through it that families take on weekend afternoons are among the small ordinary practices by which an urbanised Romanian population keeps a working relationship with the older landscape from which the contemporary cities have, within a single generation, substantially separated themselves.

ON THIS DAY

1928

Bucharest, Romania

On 17 June 1928, the National Peasants' Party held the largest political rally in inter-war Romanian history on the Champ-de-Mars in Bucharest, with approximately one hundred thousand people gathered to call for the resignation of the Brătianu government and the recognition of the party's victory in the deferred elections of the previous year. The rally was, among other things, the moment in which the rural population's organised political voice reached its largest twentieth-century expression in Romania, before being progressively dismantled by the royal authoritarianism of the 1930s and the communist takeover of the 1940s. The doinas that the peasants from various regions sang during the long hours of waiting that day — preserved in a handful of contemporary accounts — were inflections of the same word the essay describes, addressed in this case to a Romania the demonstrators could feel had not yet arrived but to which they had a kind of dor whose object was located in the future rather than in the past.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a person, place, or condition for which you currently feel dor — a quiet ongoing awareness of an absence that is part of the texture of your daily life?

    Is the dor something you treat as a problem to be solved, or as a piece of who you are that asks no resolution? And what does the answer reveal about which cultural assumption you have inherited about how a developed inner life is supposed to feel?

    Spend an hour this week sitting deliberately with one ongoing absence in your life — a person, a place, a former self — without doing anything to alleviate it. Notice what shape the absence has when you do not try to resolve or to escape it. Notice whether the absence, attended to in this way, becomes heavier or lighter.

  • 2

    If the Romanian assumption is right — that any developed adult continuously carries several large absences — how many large absences are you currently carrying, and what would it be like to recognise them as constitutive rather than as problems?

    Are there absences in your life that you have been refusing to acknowledge, on the grounds that to acknowledge them would be to admit a failure of adjustment? And what would it cost to acknowledge them as simply present?

    Make a short honest list of the large absences in your current life. Do not aim to resolve any of them. Simply place the list somewhere you will see it once a day for a week, and notice what changes in your daily emotional life when the absences are explicitly named rather than only present.

  • 3

    Are there words from a language other than your native one that you have found yourself reaching for because they catch something your own language does not — and what does the reaching tell you about what you have been trying to articulate?

    If the reaching has become a regular practice, has it enlarged your own native vocabulary by importation, or has it remained a private supplement that the rest of your linguistic life has not absorbed? And what would it take for the imported word to become a working part of how you actually think?

    Pick one word from another language that catches a feature of your inner life. Use it, this week, in a piece of writing or in a conversation with someone who will not know it. Define it briefly. Notice whether the word, once placed in active circulation, begins to organise your perception of the territory it names.

TEST YOURSELF

The doina — the long melancholic solo song tradition most closely associated with the expression of dor in Romanian music — was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in which year?

The doina was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009, on the recommendation of a Romanian dossier that documented the song form's continuous transmission across the regional traditions of the country and its central role in Romanian musical and emotional life. The inscription was part of a broader Romanian cultural-policy initiative in the years after the country's 2007 accession to the European Union, aimed at securing international recognition for several specifically Romanian artistic traditions — the doina, the Călușari ritual dance, the Hațeg pottery technique — that had been substantially under-recognised in international musicological and ethnographic literature.

MOVIE TIME

Aferim!

Radu Jude · 2015

A black-and-white Romanian western, set in 1835 Wallachia, in which a constable and his son ride across the principality in search of an escaped Roma slave. Radu Jude's film recovers a period of Romanian history that the country's own self-understanding has substantially suppressed — the four centuries during which Roma people were held as chattel slaves on the great Romanian boyar estates — and conducts that recovery through a long road movie whose visual register continually shifts between the comic, the violent, and the elegiac. The dor in the film is collective and historical rather than personal: a longing addressed to a Romania that did not become, and to the people whose absence from the national consciousness has shaped the country in ways its inhabitants have not yet finished accounting for.
