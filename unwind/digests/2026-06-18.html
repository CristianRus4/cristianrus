The Staircase Reply

The conversation has ended. The other person has said what they were going to say. You have said what you were going to say. The exchange has concluded, the room has dispersed, and you are now on the staircase or in the street or in the car driving home. Halfway down the stairs, the reply you should have made arrives in your mouth fully formed. It is sharper than anything you actually said. It would have been devastating. It would have ended the argument. It would have made you look like the person you privately think you are. And it is, of course, completely useless, because the conversation in which it was needed has been over for fourteen minutes and the person to whom it was addressed is by now thinking about something else.

Yiddish has a word for this phenomenon. Trepverter: literally 'staircase words,' the reply that arrives in the brain on the staircase after the conversation in which it would have been useful has irretrievably ended. The Yiddish word is a calque from the French 'esprit de l'escalier' — the wit of the staircase — coined by Diderot in his 'Paradoxe sur le Comédien' in the 1770s. Both languages found the staircase the right metaphor for the same reason: the descent from a Parisian or a Galician apartment building, by the time the staircase had been finished, was long enough that the perfect reply would have time to arrive and the perfect reply would, with absolute certainty, have arrived too late.

What is interesting about the phenomenon, from the perspective of cognitive science, is that the staircase wit is not a random failure of timing. The brain is in a specifiable cognitive state during a charged conversation that is different from the state it enters once the charge has been removed. The cortisol and adrenaline that organise the conversational mode actively suppress the lateral, associative thinking that produces the killer comeback, on the principle that fast simple responses are more useful in a perceived social threat than slow elegant ones. Once the threat passes and the chemistry subsides, the associative apparatus comes back online, and the reply that would have required this apparatus to operate during the conversation finally becomes available. The staircase is, in this sense, the precise location at which the body's stress response has subsided enough for the mind to do the thinking the conversation required.

The cultural function of having a word for this phenomenon, as both Yiddish and French and a number of other languages have decided, is partly consolation. The reply that arrived too late was not the product of a deficient intelligence. It was the product of an intelligence operating under conditions that systematically suppress its best work. The Yiddish speaker can label the experience and proceed with their evening. The English speaker, with no equivalent word, often spends an evening or a week privately replaying the conversation and assembling, in the safety of the rerun, the replies that the live conversation did not allow them to produce. The replaying is essentially universal and is, on the staircase-words account, a small low-grade form of trauma response: the mind trying again and again to do, in the safety of after, the work that the conditions of during prevented.

What this opens up, more broadly, is the question of which forms of human capacity are systematically unavailable under conditions of perceived social pressure. The list is longer than the staircase-words case suggests. People in arguments often cannot remember the names of the people they are arguing about. Job candidates in high-stakes interviews often forget the work they have spent years doing. Public speakers facing hostile questions sometimes lose access to the very vocabulary of their own discipline. The staircase recovery is the same phenomenon visible in a small social-conversational case: the cognitive apparatus returns to full function only once the threat has passed, and the recovery is too late to be useful.

What remains is the question of what to do with the late-arriving replies. The standard advice, in the absence of a vocabulary that names the phenomenon, is to dismiss them as wasted thought and to move on. The vocabulary suggests a different possibility: that the staircase reply is information about what one actually wanted to say, freed from the conversational constraints that prevented one from saying it, and that the information is sometimes worth keeping even though the original audience has dispersed. The staircase reply can be written down. It can be addressed to the person it was meant for in another medium, with care. It can be remembered for the next time a similar conversation arises. The vocabulary that names the phenomenon is the beginning of a different relationship to one's own delayed intelligence than the dominant Anglo-Saxon framing, in which the late reply is just an embarrassment and an unprofitable rumination, has tended to encourage.

THE TENSION

Naming the staircase phenomenon as trepverter (or as esprit de l'escalier) gives the bearer a piece of useful self-knowledge: the late reply is not a deficiency of the intelligence but a feature of how cognition operates under social pressure, and the recognition allows the bearer to stop interpreting the delay as a personal failing. The recurring private replaying of charged conversations, in this light, is something to be understood and managed rather than to be ashamed of.

The naming risks legitimising a habit — the endless mental replaying of past arguments with the construction of improved comebacks — that is itself one of the most reliably documented forms of low-grade self-inflicted suffering. Once one has the word, one can be tempted to indulge the practice as a recognised cultural phenomenon rather than to do the harder psychological work of letting charged conversations actually end when they end.

THE NUMBER

1773

Year of the posthumous publication of Denis Diderot's 'Paradoxe sur le Comédien' (Paradox of the Actor), the text in which the term 'esprit de l'escalier' was first coined — composed in the 1770s but not published until 1830, half a century after Diderot's death, by which time the phrase had already become a French idiom and had begun to circulate in calques into other languages.

A FRAGMENT

He had been on the train home for an hour before the perfect sentence arrived. He sat with it for a moment. He took out his notebook. He wrote it down. He thought about whether to email it to his former colleague. He decided, finally, not to. The conversation had ended. The colleague would not now be expecting an addendum, and to send one would be to extend a thing that had completed itself, however imperfectly, in the room where it occurred. He kept the sentence in the notebook. He thought he might use a version of it the next time, if a next time arrived, in some other conversation with some other person, with the small advantage that this time he would have had the line ready before he started up the stairs.

THE WORD TO KNOW

trepverter

/ˈtʁɛpfɛʁtɐ/ — טרעפּווערטער

Yiddish; literally 'staircase words,' a compound of 'trep' (staircase, from the German 'Treppe') and 'verter' (words, from the German 'Wörter'). The compound is a direct calque from the French 'esprit de l'escalier,' coined by Denis Diderot in the 1770s and quickly absorbed into the Yiddish literary and conversational vocabulary of the central European cities — Berlin, Vienna, Warsaw, Vilnius — where Yiddish-speaking Jewish intellectual life was conducted in close contact with the French language and the French literary tradition. The Yiddish version was extended somewhat in the broader spoken use, where it can refer not only to the rapier reply but to any thought that arrives just too late to be useful — the better answer to the question on the exam, the apt quotation forgotten until afterward, the connection seen only in retrospect.

The perfect reply, comeback, or insight that arrives in the mind after the conversation in which it would have been useful has already ended — the late-arriving thought that the cognitive conditions of the original moment had systematically suppressed. Names a near-universal experience that English handles only with the imported French phrase or with awkward periphrasis, and that Yiddish (like French) lexicalises with a precision that allows the experience to be discussed and considered rather than only privately suffered.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Émile Veuillot

1813 – 1883 · Paris, France

Émile Veuillot was the French journalist, polemicist, and editor of the Catholic newspaper L'Univers in the third quarter of the nineteenth century — a figure whose career is principally remembered as a study in the practical consequences of a different kind of trepverter: the published refutation that arrived weeks or months after the original argument had been forgotten by everyone except the author of the refutation. Working alongside his more famous brother Louis Veuillot, Émile spent decades producing extended counter-arguments to liberal Catholic positions that the broader French Catholic discussion had, by the time his pieces appeared, already moved past. The accumulated body of his journalism is a curiously instructive archive of what it looks like to spend a career assembling the perfect reply on the wrong publication schedule.

Veuillot's complication is the substantial seriousness of the underlying work — his theological and political arguments were not trivial, and his command of nineteenth-century French Catholic doctrine was substantial — combined with the temporal misalignment that prevented most of the work from reaching the readers it was addressed to in the conversational moment when it could have made a difference. He is largely forgotten today, and the broader recovery of nineteenth-century French Catholic journalism in academic study has tended to concentrate on his more publicly visible brother and on the larger institutional and intellectual movements they were part of. The figure of the polemicist whose contributions arrived on the staircase is one of the recurring types in the history of intellectual journalism, and Veuillot is a particularly fully-documented instance of it.

NOW HERE

Marszałkowska Boulevard Apartment Stairwells

Central Warsaw, Poland

The long stairwells of the inter-war apartment buildings that line the central north-south boulevard of Warsaw — narrow, marble-step, often with the original cast-iron banisters surviving even where the rest of the building has been substantially rebuilt after the wartime destruction. These are the staircases on which the Yiddish word the essay describes was for many decades performed by the Jewish intellectual residents of pre-war Warsaw, who would walk down them after a charged café conversation on Krakowskie Przedmieście or Plac Trzech Krzyży and discover, somewhere between the third floor and the second, the reply they should have made at the table forty minutes earlier. The community that produced the Yiddish vocabulary the essay draws on was substantially destroyed between 1939 and 1945; the staircases that remain, in the buildings that survived or were rebuilt close to their original form, are a small architectural archive of a phenomenon whose original speakers are no longer in a position to perform it.

ON THIS DAY

1812

Washington, D.C., USA

On 18 June 1812, the United States Congress declared war on Great Britain, beginning the War of 1812 — the second and last formal military conflict between the two countries and one of the most consequential miscalculations of the early nineteenth century, undertaken on the basis of grievances that the British government had, unknown to Congress, addressed two days earlier. The repeal of the British Orders in Council, which had been the principal cause of American grievance, was passed in London on 16 June; news of the repeal would not reach Washington until weeks after the declaration. The war that followed killed approximately 20,000 people, burned the White House, produced the lyrics of the American national anthem, and could in principle have been entirely averted had transatlantic communication been three weeks faster. The whole war was, in this sense, a kind of historical staircase reply: the action that arrived too late, addressed to a conversation that had already concluded by the time the action was launched.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Do you replay charged conversations afterward, constructing the replies you wish you had made — and if so, what relation does the replay actually have to the work of having had the conversation?

    Is the replay producing useful information that you carry forward into how you handle similar conversations in the future, or has it become a closed loop that serves only to extend the original distress? And how would you tell the difference?

    The next time a charged conversation ends with you on the staircase, write the late-arriving reply down in a notebook. Do not send it to anyone. Notice whether the writing — which acknowledges and externalises the reply — reduces the urge to replay the conversation mentally, or whether the urge continues regardless.

  • 2

    Are there capacities in your own intelligence that you can feel becoming unavailable under social pressure — capacities that you know are intact but cannot reach when the room is charged?

    What would it take, in your own case, to maintain access to those capacities under pressure? And have you ever encountered someone who appears to have the capacities continuously available, and what does their continuous access actually depend on?

    Identify one situation in which you regularly experience this unavailability — meetings, conflicts, presentations. Spend a week preparing for the next instance not by rehearsing content but by attending to the bodily state in which you most often arrive at the situation. Notice whether changes in the bodily state precede changes in the cognitive availability.

  • 3

    What is your usual response to the late-arriving reply — dismissal, indulgent replay, written record, communication to the original audience — and is the response one you have chosen or one you have defaulted into?

    If the late reply is, as the essay suggests, information about what you actually wanted to say, what would it look like to receive that information without acting either on the impulse to dismiss it or on the impulse to extend the original conversation?

    Pick one significant late-arriving reply from a conversation in the past month. Write a paragraph in which you describe what the reply tells you about what you actually wanted that the original conversation prevented you from articulating. Do not send the paragraph to anyone. Notice what you have learned about yourself from writing it.

TEST YOURSELF

The French phrase 'esprit de l'escalier,' from which the Yiddish 'trepverter' is calqued, was coined by which eighteenth-century philosopher and encyclopaedist in a text on the art of acting?

The phrase 'esprit de l'escalier' was coined by Denis Diderot in his 'Paradoxe sur le Comédien' (Paradox of the Actor), composed in the 1770s and published posthumously in 1830. Diderot's larger argument in the text was that great actors do not actually feel the emotions they perform; the phrase about the staircase appears as an illustration of how the cognitive conditions of charged interaction systematically suppress the deliberative apparatus required for the best possible response, which becomes available only once the interaction has ended and the actor or interlocutor has begun the descent. The phrase entered French idiom almost immediately and was calqued into Yiddish, German ('Treppenwitz'), Polish ('koncept klatkowy'), and several other languages over the following century.

Letter by letter

The repetitive mental replaying of past conversations, decisions, or events, often accompanied by the construction of alternative versions in which the bearer behaves more skilfully than they actually did. Closely related to the staircase phenomenon: a small low-grade form of cognitive looping that arrives once the original threat has passed and the analytic apparatus has come back online but has nothing useful left to do with the situation that originally engaged it.

MOVIE TIME

Husbands and Wives

Woody Allen · 1992

An ensemble film tracing the unravelling of two New York couples after the apparently sudden announcement of one couple's separation. The film is structured as a long succession of charged conversations — at dinners, in offices, in stairwells, in cars — each of which produces in the participants the precise staircase phenomenon the essay describes, sometimes visible in the next scene as the character delivers, into the void, the line they should have used four hours earlier. The handheld cinematography, unusual for Allen at the time, places the camera close to the faces in which the late replies are being slowly formed, and the film becomes a kind of working anatomy of how the trepverter accumulates across a season of social and marital pressure.
