The Damage a Word Does When It Lands

Every word for an emotion was once coined in the absence of a better one. The coinage was not neutral. It selected from the full spectrum of what was felt and handed the rest over to silence.

To name something is to make it manageable, but management is not the same as understanding. The named thing shrinks slightly to fit inside the word.

Languages that lack a single word for a particular state are not deficient. They are forced to describe rather than label. The description is harder but more honest about complexity.

The word is a door that closes on the thing it names.

Translation is not the movement of a meaning from one house into another. It is the construction of a new house from materials that belonged to a different climate.

There is something mourned in every accurate translation and something quietly invented that was never in the original. The gap between the two is not an error. It is where languages touch without meeting.

THE NUMBER

7,168

The approximate number of living languages currently documented — each one a different architecture for sorting human experience into speakable units.

A FRAGMENT

He tried to explain the feeling to her in three languages. In the first, the word was too clinical. In the second, the word was too religious. In the third, there was no word, only a phrase that meant something like: the sound a door makes when a room has been empty for a long time. She said: yes, that one. He did not know which language she meant.

THE WORD TO KNOW

barzakh

Arabic — a threshold, interval, or barrier between two states; in Islamic cosmology, the intermediate realm between life and the afterlife, though the word's application extends to any liminal in-between.

An interval or threshold that separates two conditions without fully belonging to either.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Victor Segalen

1878 – 1919 · Brest, France

Victor Segalen was a French naval physician and writer who developed a theory of exoticism not as the consumption of the foreign but as the irreducible distance between the perceiver and the perceived. He argued that the exotic was defined by its resistance to being fully known, and that any attempt to absorb the other — aesthetically, linguistically, culturally — was a form of violence against that resistance.

He wrote in Chinese, studied Polynesian funeral chants, and died in a forest in Brittany at forty-one, possibly from a fall. His major theoretical work, Essay on Exoticism, was never finished. What survives is a notebook of fragments, each one pressing against a limit it declines to cross.
