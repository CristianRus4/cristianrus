What the Private Notebook Knows That the Published Work Does Not

In the summer of 1837, Charles Darwin opened a new notebook and wrote at the top, in pencil, 'Zoonomia' — a reference to his grandfather's speculative treatise on evolution, a work he had read and largely rejected. Within a few months, the notebook contained the first sketch of an evolutionary tree, drawn mid-sentence, with two words above it: 'I think.' The published theory that emerged twenty-two years later had the marks of a finished argument: marshaled evidence, careful qualification, the shape of a book. The notebook page has the marks of a thought in the moment of arriving, before it has been prepared for anyone to see.

There is a specific kind of knowledge in the notebook that the finished work systematically removes. The hesitations, the false starts, the paragraphs crossed out and written over, the moments where a thinker contradicts something they said three pages earlier — these are not the detritus of the real thinking but part of it. The published work shows the conclusion and the selected evidence. The notebook shows the process that produced the conclusion, including all the paths that did not lead there and the doubts that remained after the conclusion was reached.

The Stoics had a practice called hypomnemata — personal notebooks kept not for posterity but for use, in which a practitioner would copy down quotations, record reflections, transcribe conversations and observations, and return to these notes as a way of holding onto what thinking had produced before it dissolved back into daily life. The notebook was a technology of the self: a tool for converting experience into understanding, and for keeping understanding from evaporating. Michel Foucault, writing about this tradition, described it as a kind of self-writing that was preparatory to action — the private work of forming a self capable of acting consistently with what it believed.

What makes the private notebook philosophically interesting is precisely its privacy. The hypomnemata were kept for the writer's own use; Leonardo's notebooks were written in mirror-script; Darwin's B notebook was never intended for publication. The absence of the imagined reader changes the quality of thinking. When you write for no one, you write differently — less carefully in the rhetorical sense, and more carefully in the sense of fidelity to what you actually think rather than what you need to appear to think. The notebook is a space for the unedited version of the mind.

Hilma af Klint understood this. For years before she began painting the large abstract canvases that would eventually be recognized as among the first abstract works in Western art, she kept notebooks of her visual and spiritual investigations — systematic color studies, diagrammatic mappings of forces she was trying to perceive, written dialogues with figures she called the High Masters. The notebooks show a rigorous private practice that preceded and shaped the paintings; the paintings could not have been made without the notebooks. She kept the notebooks for herself. She left instructions that the paintings not be shown until twenty years after her death.

What is preserved in the notebook, ultimately, is the thinking before it becomes a position. The published work defends what has been decided; the notebook records the decision in process, with all the vulnerability and uncertainty that implies. Reading an extraordinary thinker's notebooks is often a more intimate encounter with their intelligence than reading their finished work — not because the notebooks are better written, but because the quality of thinking is more visible when it is not being performed.

THE TENSION

The notebook is the place where the mind thinks with the pen rather than for the reader — where conclusions have not yet been separated from the paths that led to them. What published works systematically hide is visible in the notebook, which is why notebooks often contain the more honest and useful thinking, the version of a person's intelligence unfiltered by the need to be persuasive.

The notebook is also a performance — a private audience imagined, a version of the self being projected into a future that might read it. Even the most private diary is written for someone, even when that someone is only the writer's future self, and the unedited version is itself edited by the self-image being maintained. There is no genuinely unperformed thinking.

THE NUMBER

13,000

Approximate number of pages in Leonardo da Vinci's surviving notebooks — representing roughly a third of what he is believed to have written, with the remainder lost; the largest surviving record of a single mind's private working process from any period before the modern era.

A FRAGMENT

She had found the notebook in the drawer where she kept things she had not decided about. It was from seven years ago, and she recognised the handwriting but not the thinking — the certainty with which a problem had been held, the confidence of the margins, the diagrams. She did not remember being that person. She was not sure she trusted them.

THE WORD TO KNOW

hypomnemata

/ˌhaɪ.pɒmˈniː.mə.tə/ — ὑπομνήματα

Ancient Greek; plural of 'hypomnematon'; from 'hypo-' (under, below) and 'mnema' (memory, memorial); personal notes, aide-mémoires, jottings; the term used in Greco-Roman antiquity for the practice of keeping personal notebooks of quotations, observations, and reflections for private use; analyzed by Michel Foucault in his 1983 essay 'Self Writing' as an ancient technology of the self.

Personal notebooks kept as tools for the formation of the self — not diaries in the autobiographical sense but working collections of what one has read, thought, observed, and wants to return to; the private record of thinking in process; a form of writing that is preparatory to living rather than documentary of it.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Hilma af Klint

1862 – 1944 · Stockholm, Sweden / Järna, Sweden

Hilma af Klint was the Swedish painter who, in 1906, began producing large abstract canvases as part of a systematic artistic and spiritual investigation she had been conducting privately for years. Her 'Paintings for the Temple' series — 193 works in total — includes abstract compositions made with a visual language of spirals, organic forms, and bold colour relationships developed through long preparation in her notebooks. She worked a decade before Kandinsky, Mondrian, and Malevich produced what Western art history would designate as the first abstract works; her own canvases were not exhibited publicly until 1986, because she left instructions that they not be shown until at least twenty years after her death. She died in 1944; the world waited four decades more.

Af Klint's notebooks are as significant as her paintings. They contain years of color studies, diagrammatic sketches, automatic writing, and documented conversations with what she called the High Masters — spiritual intelligences whose guidance she felt shaped her work. Whether understood as genuine spiritual communication or as a method for accessing unconscious material, the notebooks reveal a systematic private practice operating in parallel with a conventional public career as a botanical illustrator and portraitist. The combination of rigorous technical skill and unconventional inner life, sustained across decades, produced work that could not have been made any other way. She is still not as widely known as her male contemporaries, despite having done the thing first.

NOW HERE

Atelier Cézanne

Aix-en-Provence, Provence, France

Paul Cézanne had the studio built on a hillside above Aix in 1901, spending the last five years of his life there. It has been preserved with the objects he used: a coat on a hook, the skull he used as a still-life prop, the ladder to the loft, the high north window unchanged. The studio is not a monument to a finished achievement but to the conditions of daily work — the particular furniture of a particular practice over years. What the notebooks and letters of painters reveal, the studio makes visible in another register: this is what the work required of the person who made it, before the work became what it is.

ON THIS DAY

1907

Springdale, Pennsylvania, USA

On 27 May 1907, Rachel Carson was born in Springdale, Pennsylvania — the marine biologist and writer who would spend years observing the shore at night, filling notebooks with descriptions of tidal pools, organisms, and the quality of light on the water. Her field notebooks, kept for decades before and during the writing of her major books, contain a voice entirely different from the careful scientific prose of 'The Sea Around Us' or 'Silent Spring' — more uncertain, more immediate, more attentive to what could not yet be named. Her letters to her friend Dorothy Freeman, written in parallel with her scientific work, record the private cost of the attention her writing required.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Do you keep any form of private notebook — and if so, does the thinking in it differ from the thinking you do in conversation or in what you write for others to see?

    If the notebook is a space where you think differently because no one is watching, what does that difference reveal about how much of ordinary thinking is performance — shaped by an imagined audience even when no audience is present?

    Write, for ten minutes, in a notebook or document that you commit to never showing anyone. Write about something that matters to you without caring whether your reasoning is defensible. Afterwards, read back what you wrote and notice whether it feels more or less honest than what you would normally produce.

  • 2

    Darwin's notebook contains the first sketch of the evolutionary tree with the words 'I think' above it — a thought arriving before it is a theory. Is there a way to cultivate that quality of catching thinking in the moment of arriving, before it has been shaped into a position?

    What is lost when a thought is turned into a position? Is the loss inevitable — is the process of defending and refining what you think necessarily a departure from the rawer thing that thinking was before it was organized?

    The next time you have a thought that surprises you, write it down immediately, before you know what to do with it. Don't explain or justify it. Come back to it a week later and see whether you recognise it.

  • 3

    The Stoic hypomnemata were kept as tools for formation — notebooks not to record what you thought but to consolidate it for use, so that you would be the kind of person capable of acting on it. Do you have any practice that does this — that converts understanding into something more than information?

    Is there a difference between knowing something and having it available when you need it? What does the difference between these two states actually consist in?

    Take one idea or principle that matters to you and that you believe you understand. Write it out in your own words, without looking anything up, in enough detail to explain it to someone who has never encountered it. Notice what you can and cannot say.

TEST YOURSELF

Darwin's 1837 'B notebook' contains the first known sketch of an evolutionary tree. What two words did he write above the branching diagram?

Darwin wrote 'I think' in pencil above a rough branching diagram in Notebook B (1837), making it one of the most significant doodles in the history of science. The phrase captures the epistemic status of the thought in that moment — not yet a claim, barely a hypothesis, but the first visual representation of the idea that all life might share common ancestors and diverge through branching descent. The phrase 'natural selection' would not appear in its developed form until later, and 'common ancestor' is a retrospective label applied by later interpreters.

MOVIE TIME

The Pillow Book

Peter Greenaway · 1996

A Japanese woman in Hong Kong who has been writing on the bodies of lovers since childhood seeks a publisher who will allow her to write on his body as well as on paper. The film circles around 'The Pillow Book' of Sei Shōnagon — the 10th-century Japanese court lady whose private notebook became one of the foundational texts of Japanese literature without being written for publication. Greenaway's film is about what private writing is for, and what happens when it is seen — the same problem the notebook always poses when it outlasts its maker.

Game answer

Franz Kafka left instructions for all his diaries and manuscripts to be destroyed after his death, but personally burned the majority of his notebooks himself before he died rather than leaving the task to Max Brod

The lie is the claim about Kafka. He did ask his friend Max Brod to destroy his manuscripts, diaries, and letters after his death — a request Brod famously chose not to honour. Kafka did destroy some materials during his lifetime, but he did not personally burn the majority of his notebooks before dying; he left that instruction to Brod, who preserved them instead. Zola's documentary field research is well-documented; the Mendeleev dream story's inconsistency with his actual notebooks is a matter of historical record.
