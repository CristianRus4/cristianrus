Walking in Beauty as a Verb

There is a word in the language of the Diné, the people whom outsiders most often call the Navajo, that does not have a clean English equivalent and that organises a substantial part of how the language's speakers describe a well-lived life. The word is sometimes translated as harmony, sometimes as beauty, sometimes as balance; none of the translations is quite right. The word names neither a state of the world nor a state of the self but a quality of motion through the world — the way a person walks, attends, speaks, acts, when the various relationships in which they stand are in the proper configuration. It is closer to an adverb than to a noun. It modifies the way of doing rather than naming a thing done.

Hózhó: a quality of being in right relation. The Diné Blessingway, the foundational ceremony of the Navajo religious tradition, ends with a passage often translated into English as 'In beauty I walk. With beauty before me I walk. With beauty behind me I walk. With beauty above me I walk. With beauty below me I walk. With beauty all around me I walk. It has become beauty again.' The English translation, while it preserves the cadence, loses the verbal character of the original. What is being described is not a beautiful environment through which the speaker passes. It is a way of walking that the world participates in producing and that the walker participates in maintaining.

What is striking about hózhó, from the perspective of European philosophical vocabulary, is how completely it refuses the inside-outside division. Hózhó is not a quality of the world; it is not a quality of the self; it is a quality of the relation between them. When the relation is in good order, the world reflects it back and the walker can feel it. When the relation is disturbed — by grief, by injury, by transgression, by accident — the disturbance is in neither the world nor the walker but in the configuration of the two, and the work of restoration is the work of returning the configuration to its proper state. The Navajo healing ceremonies, including the Blessingway and the Nightway and the long body of curative ceremonies known as Chantways, are technologies for restoring hózhó when it has been disturbed. The patient is not the locus of the disorder; the relation is, and the ceremony is the relational work.

The implication, which European philosophical traditions have struggled to articulate in their own vocabulary, is that the good life is not the achievement of a stable internal state but the maintenance of a continuous correspondence with what surrounds one. The walking is the meaning. The relation has to be sustained by ongoing attention; it has no resting point. This is not the same as the European wisdom traditions that prize active engagement over passive enjoyment, because in those traditions the agent and the world are still ontologically separate and the agent's task is to act well upon the world. In the Diné formulation, the separation is what has gone wrong when things have gone wrong, and the task is not to act upon but to come back into.

What this asks of a culture is a particular density of attention to relationship. The Diné kinship system, the clan structure, the cycle of seasonal ceremonies, the spatial organisation of the hooghan — each of these is, among other things, a structure for keeping the configuration in view and for noticing when it has drifted. The European visitor to a Navajo ceremony sometimes mistakes the apparent slowness and repetitiveness for inefficiency, and misses that the slowness and the repetition are the form the attention takes. The ceremony is not getting to a point. The ceremony is the point. The point is the continued tuning.

What resists in this is the European temptation to extract hózhó from its cultural matrix and import it as a portable concept — a personal practice, a yoga, a wellness vocabulary. The temptation is understandable; the concept names something European traditions have under-articulated. But the extraction usually fails, because the concept does not survive the loss of the surrounding architecture: the clan system, the geographic place, the language in which the relevant relations are originally articulated. What can be carried over is not the word but the question the word raises — whether one's own life, in its own surrounding architecture, has any equivalent practice of continuous relational maintenance, or whether one has been pursuing the internal state and missing the configuration entirely.

THE TENSION

The Diné concept of hózhó identifies something the dominant European philosophical traditions have substantially missed: that the locus of well-being is not in the agent or in the world but in the relation, and that this changes what the work of a good life consists of. Any serious account of human flourishing has to take seriously the possibility that the inside-outside framing that has organised Western ethics since Descartes is a parochial assumption rather than a universal truth.

The use of hózhó as a critique of European thought risks both flattening the genuine internal complexity of the Diné tradition and instrumentalising it as a rhetorical move in a debate the Diné themselves are not party to. The concept is embedded in a specific religious and cultural matrix and is performed through specific ceremonies and kinship structures; lifted out as a portable philosophical category, it becomes one more piece of borrowed vocabulary in the long history of European appropriation of indigenous thought.

A FRAGMENT

The grandmother turned the wool between her fingers and watched her daughter dye the next batch in the iron pot. Her granddaughter, sitting nearby, was scrolling on her phone. The grandmother did not ask her to put it down. She knew the girl had been at the loom that morning and would be at it again before dinner. The pattern they were weaving was the same pattern her own grandmother had taught her, with the deliberate small break in the border where the spirit moved through. None of the three of them said anything for almost an hour. The fire stayed low. The wool went on becoming the colours it was being asked to become.

THE WORD TO KNOW

hózhó

/hózˈxó/

Diné Bizaad (Navajo), the language spoken by the Diné people of the American Southwest, member of the Southern Athabaskan branch of the Na-Dené language family. The word is composed of two morphemes — 'hó-' (an areal prefix indicating broad spatial or temporal extension) and a root associated with the proper or beautiful condition of relations. The compound names not a quality of an object but a quality of the configuration in which subject and surroundings together stand. It is among the most theologically and ethically central terms in the Diné language and appears throughout the prayers, songs, and ceremonies that make up the Diné religious tradition.

The state in which the relations between a person and their surroundings — physical, social, spiritual — are in proper configuration; the quality of motion through the world when that configuration is in order. Often translated as beauty, harmony, or balance, but properly understood as a relational rather than a subjective or objective property. The good life in the Diné formulation is not the achievement or possession of hózhó but the continuous practice of walking in it and of restoring it when it has been disturbed.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Annie Dodge Wauneka

1910 – 1997 · Sawmill, Arizona, USA

Annie Dodge Wauneka was the Diné public-health leader and politician who, in the 1950s and 1960s, conducted one of the most successful tuberculosis eradication campaigns of the twentieth century within the Navajo Nation, combining biomedical interventions with culturally embedded practices that respected the Diné conceptual framework rather than overriding it. She was the daughter of Henry Chee Dodge, the last chief of the Navajo people under the older political structure and the first chairman of the Navajo Tribal Council, and she was the first woman elected to that council in 1951. She served three terms. Her bilingual Navajo-English medical dictionary, produced in collaboration with the U.S. Public Health Service, remains a foundational document in the history of cross-cultural medical communication.

Wauneka's complication is the structural difficulty of her position: she was working simultaneously to bring scientifically validated medical interventions to a population that had been catastrophically harmed by previous federal interventions and to preserve a ceremonial tradition that the medical establishment of the period considered, at best, an obstacle. She refused both reductions. She insisted that the Diné ceremonial practitioners and the biomedical doctors could work together, and she conducted the patient diplomatic and translational work that made limited but real collaboration possible. President Lyndon Johnson awarded her the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1963. She is not, outside the Diné community and a small group of public-health historians, widely remembered — a fate familiar to indigenous leaders whose work was done substantially in their own language and within their own institutional structures.

NOW HERE

Canyon de Chelly

Apache County, Arizona, USA (Navajo Nation)

A canyon system in northeastern Arizona, on the Navajo Nation, with sheer red sandstone walls rising as much as three hundred metres above a flat valley floor where Diné families still live and farm. The canyon contains continuous human habitation going back at least five thousand years, with ancestral Puebloan cliff dwellings — Antelope House, the White House Ruin, Mummy Cave — built into recesses in the canyon walls between approximately 350 and 1300 CE. The Diné themselves have lived in the canyon since at least the seventeenth century; the floor of the canyon was the principal refuge during the U.S. Army's 1864 scorched-earth campaign that preceded the Long Walk. The place is administered today as a national monument by the U.S. National Park Service in cooperation with the Navajo Nation, and access to most of the canyon is permitted only with a Diné guide. To walk it with such a guide is to be told a layered geography in which the same rock face holds the marks of three or four civilisations and the relational sense the language calls hózhó becomes, for a few hours, something one can almost feel from the outside.

ON THIS DAY

1868

Fort Sumner, New Mexico Territory, USA

On 9 June 1868, the Treaty of Bosque Redondo was signed at Fort Sumner between the United States government and the Diné, formally ending the four-year internment known as the Long Walk and Hwéeldi: the forced march of more than 10,000 Diné people some 480 kilometres from their ancestral lands to a barren reservation in eastern New Mexico, where thousands died of starvation, disease, and exposure between 1864 and 1868. The treaty permitted the survivors to return to a portion of their original territory, the nucleus of what is now the Navajo Nation. The catastrophe of Bosque Redondo and the return that followed are central to Diné historical consciousness in ways comparable to the role of exile and return in other foundational national narratives, and the work of restoring hózhó after a collective rupture of that magnitude is part of what the surviving ceremonial tradition has been doing for the five generations since.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a practice in your own life that you would describe as continuous relational maintenance — an ongoing tuning of the configuration between yourself and what surrounds you — rather than as an episodic achievement?

    If there is, where in your life did the practice come from and how does it survive the absence of any surrounding cultural support for it? And if there is no such practice, is its absence something you have chosen or something you have inherited from a culture that has not offered the option?

    Spend one short walk this week — fifteen minutes — paying attention not to where you are going or to what is around you but to the quality of the relation between yourself and the path: the way the weight of your body meets the ground, the way the air around you registers, the way you are receiving the place as you move through it. Notice what kind of attention this is, and whether you have it available to you outside of this experiment.

  • 2

    Are there relationships in your life — to a place, a person, a craft, a body of work — that you have allowed to drift out of proper configuration, and for which a deliberate practice of restoration would be necessary if you wanted to be back in right relation with them?

    What has been the cost of the drift? And is the restoration something you can imagine undertaking, or has the drift gone far enough that returning would require more than you currently have to give?

    Identify one relationship in your life that you sense has drifted. Take one small action this week toward the restoration of its proper configuration — a phone call, a visit, a return to a practice you abandoned. Do not aim for resolution. Aim only for the first move.

  • 3

    The essay warns against the European temptation to extract hózhó from its cultural matrix as a portable concept. Have you, in your own life, borrowed concepts from cultures not your own in ways that you suspect, on reflection, have lost more than they have carried?

    What is the difference, in your experience, between a borrowing that genuinely enlarges your thinking and one that thins the original beyond recognition? And whose judgement do you trust on whether your borrowings have been the first or the second?

    Pick one concept you have imported into your own practice from a tradition not your own. Read, this week, one piece of writing by someone inside that tradition about what the concept actually is for them. Notice the gap between their account and your use.

TEST YOURSELF

The Diné language is a member of which broader North American language family, whose other members include the Apache languages of the American Southwest and a large group of languages spoken across Alaska and northwestern Canada?

Diné Bizaad (Navajo) belongs to the Southern Athabaskan branch of the Na-Dené language family, together with the closely related Apache languages. The Athabaskan family as a whole includes a large number of languages spoken across interior Alaska and the Yukon, Northwest Territories, British Columbia, and parts of Oregon and California, with the Southern Athabaskan group representing a southward migration that reached the present Navajo and Apache territories in the centuries before European contact. Algonquian languages are spoken further east, Siouan languages in the northern plains, and Uto-Aztecan languages from the Great Basin south through central Mexico — none of which is closely related to Navajo.

MOVIE TIME

Smoke Signals

Chris Eyre · 1998

The first feature film written, directed, and acted by Indigenous North Americans to receive wide theatrical distribution in the United States, adapted by Sherman Alexie from his short story collection 'The Lone Ranger and Tonto Fistfight in Heaven.' Two young Coeur d'Alene men travel from their reservation in Idaho to Arizona to recover the ashes of one of their fathers, in a road movie whose comic surface holds a sustained meditation on what it means to walk in right relation with one's own history. Not specifically a Diné film, but the questions it asks about restoration, ceremony, and the work of return belong to the same broader conversation.

Game answer

The traditional Navajo hooghan dwelling is built with its entrance facing west, in honour of the direction in which the sun sets and the cycle of the day completes

The lie is the third statement. The traditional Navajo hooghan is built with its entrance facing east, toward the rising sun. This orientation is central to the ceremonial and daily life of the dwelling and is observed across the Diné architectural tradition. The first two statements are accurate: the Code Talkers' use of Diné during WWII is one of the most documented episodes of the language's twentieth-century history, and the spirit line in Diné weaving is a widely-attested feature of the tradition.
