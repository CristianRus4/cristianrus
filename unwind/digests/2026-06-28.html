Travelling Without Arrival as the Point

There is a class of trip the dominant Western vocabulary of travel handles poorly: the trip whose purpose is the travelling itself rather than any destination toward which the travelling is directed. The hiker who walks for the day rather than to anywhere in particular, the long aimless drive on the back roads, the wandering through an unfamiliar neighbourhood on no errand at all, the slow circular ramble whose only end is the eventual return to the starting point. These activities are recognised in English by various awkward periphrases — 'a walk for its own sake,' 'a drive with no destination,' 'wandering around' — none of which is grammatically a single noun and all of which carry a slight register of the unproductive, the leisure-class, the unaccountable use of time.

Spanish has a noun for this kind of going. Vacilar, in its standard sense, means to hesitate or to waver; the present participle 'vacilando' was borrowed into English by John Steinbeck in 'Travels with Charley' (1962) in a more specialised sense he claimed to have picked up in Mexico: travelling without a particular destination, the journey being more important than the arrival. The Mexican Spanish use Steinbeck reported is not the standard dictionary sense of the verb and has been questioned by some scholars of Mexican Spanish as possibly a translator's invention or a regional usage that did not survive into broader contemporary speech. Whatever its actual provenance, the word has had a small but persistent afterlife in English-language travel writing as a term for a kind of travelling the rest of the vocabulary has not handled well.

What the concept names, regardless of how Mexican its origin actually was, is real. There is a category of travelling whose entire purpose is to be in motion rather than to arrive anywhere, and the category has been substantially evacuated from contemporary tourist culture by the structural pressures of efficient transportation, time-limited holidays, and the social expectation that a trip should produce results in the form of photographs, stories, and verifiable destinations reached. The vacilando-traveller does not produce these results. The vacilando-traveller often cannot even describe coherently where they have been, because the destination was not the point and the route was not optimised for memorability. The category of activity persists, but the cultural infrastructure for recognising it has thinned.

What is interesting about vacilando, considered carefully, is its relationship to attention. The journey directed at a destination organises attention around the route toward the destination: the next turn, the remaining distance, the estimated arrival. The journey without a destination organises attention around the present quality of the going: the light at this particular point, the texture of this particular surface underfoot, the sound of this particular street. The two modes of attention are not different intensities of the same thing; they are different things, and the second is substantially less practised in contemporary urban life than the first. A person who has not done much vacilando-travelling has often lost the capacity for the second mode of attention almost entirely, and has to learn it again deliberately, like a skill that has atrophied through disuse.

There is a long literary and philosophical tradition of vacilando-travelling, distributed across cultures that have given it various names. The French 'flâneur' tradition that runs from Baudelaire through Walter Benjamin is one regional version: the urban wanderer who reads the city as a text by walking it slowly without errand. The Japanese tradition of the haiku poet's seasonal journey — Bashō's 'Narrow Road to the Deep North' is the most-cited example — is another. The English-language ramble, the German Wanderung, the Australian walkabout, each in different ways names some version of the same activity. The vocabulary is plural and the underlying practice is widely distributed across human cultures, which suggests that the activity is meeting a need that the contemporary destination-driven travel culture has substantially failed to meet.

What remains is the practical question of how to recover the practice in contemporary urban life, where the structural pressures against it are substantial. The deliberate cultivation of vacilando is not the same as the natural practice that emerges from a culture in which it is the default; the deliberate version is self-conscious, often slightly awkward, and easily collapses back into goal-directed activity at the first opportunity. The recovery, if it is to take place at all, requires both small individual practices — the walk with no destination, the drive on the back road, the willingness to be temporarily and deliberately unproductive — and some larger acknowledgment by the surrounding culture that the practice is valuable enough to make space for. The first part is available to anyone. The second part is the cultural work of a generation, and it has not yet been done.

THE TENSION

The recovery of vacilando-travelling — or whatever name one gives the activity — is one of the more important small cultural projects of the present moment, because the destination-driven travel culture that has displaced it has substantially atrophied the capacity for the present-tense attention that the activity uniquely produces. The reduction of travel to verifiable destinations reached has been part of a broader reduction of experience to its documentable products, and the resistance to this reduction requires the deliberate practice of unproductive going.

The valorisation of aimless wandering as a recovered ancient practice can become a particularly leisured-class form of self-congratulation, available primarily to people whose economic position allows them to be unproductive without consequence. The flâneur, the Bashō pilgrim, the contemporary Western 'slow traveller' all share the substantial privilege of being able to choose not to optimise for destination, and the celebration of the practice as a universal recovery overlooks the conditions that make it available to some and not to others.

THE NUMBER

10,000

Approximate number of miles John Steinbeck and his standard poodle Charley drove together across the United States between September and December 1960, in the trip that produced 'Travels with Charley' and that Steinbeck described, in much of the resulting book, as conducted in the vacilando mode — although subsequent scholarship has questioned how much of the actual journey was as aimless as the book presented it as being.

A FRAGMENT

He had no plan for the afternoon. He had walked to the edge of the small park and had then walked past it and had then turned left at a street he had not noticed before and had then continued for an hour through neighbourhoods he had never had reason to visit. The walking had its own logic, which was not his logic. He had let it have its way. He returned to his apartment four hours later with no photographs, no purchases, and no story he could tell at dinner about where he had been. The afternoon had nonetheless been, he understood as he climbed the stairs, one of the better afternoons of the month.

THE WORD TO KNOW

vacilando

/bɑːsiˈlɑːndoʊ/

Spanish, the present participle of the verb 'vacilar' (to hesitate, to waver, to vacillate). The standard Spanish meaning of the verb refers to indecision or wavering; the specialised sense — travelling without a particular destination, with the journey itself being the point — was reported by John Steinbeck in 'Travels with Charley' (1962) as a Mexican Spanish usage he had encountered, though subsequent scholarship has questioned whether the specialised sense was as widely-used in Mexican Spanish as Steinbeck implied or whether the usage was partly his own elaboration of the verb's broader semantic possibilities. Regardless of its disputed provenance, the word has had a small but persistent afterlife in English-language writing about travel as the most precise available term for a kind of activity the language does not otherwise lexicalise.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Rebecca Solnit

1961 – present · Bridgeport, Connecticut, USA / San Francisco, California, USA

Rebecca Solnit is the American essayist whose 2000 book 'Wanderlust: A History of Walking' is the most fully developed contemporary English-language work on the cultural history of walking as an activity in its own right rather than as a means of transportation. Solnit's broader body of essays across the subsequent two decades — 'A Field Guide to Getting Lost' (2005), 'The Faraway Nearby' (2013), 'Hope in the Dark' (2004), the 'Men Explain Things to Me' essays that gave currency to the term 'mansplaining' — has placed her among the most widely-read American essayists of her generation, with a particular ability to combine close cultural observation with substantial political and ethical seriousness in prose that has remained accessible without sacrificing intellectual range.

Solnit's complication is the recurring difficulty of being a public essayist whose serious scholarly work is often eclipsed in the broader culture by the much greater visibility of the few pieces of her work that have entered general conversation as memes or shorthand references. 'Men Explain Things to Me' has been read by orders of magnitude more people than 'Wanderlust' or 'A Field Guide to Getting Lost,' and the resulting public picture of Solnit as primarily a feminist polemicist has substantially overshadowed the careful long-form work on landscape, environment, art history, and political memory that constitutes the larger part of her actual output. The pattern is familiar from many essayists who have produced one piece of breakthrough cultural commentary; the breakthrough work becomes the public identity, and the broader achievement has to wait for the slower scholarly assessment that has not yet caught up.

NOW HERE

El Camino de Santiago at León

León, Castilla y León, Spain

A section of the medieval pilgrimage route from the French border to Santiago de Compostela on the Atlantic coast of Galicia, as it passes through the small Castilian cathedral city of León — the route's approximate midpoint and traditionally the place where the pilgrim, having walked for several weeks, begins to reckon with the question of what the remaining several weeks of walking are actually for. The pilgrims who walk the Camino in any of its various contemporary forms — the religious pilgrimage, the secular long walk, the partial section completed in vacation time — substantially perform a contemporary version of vacilando: the route is fixed but the experience of it is largely the present-tense going, and the destination at Santiago is for most modern pilgrims less the point than the long approach. The cathedral plaza in León, in the late afternoon, has a steady distribution of pilgrims arriving from the east and others setting out for the west, and the small city has organised its commercial life around the population of walkers in transit for several centuries.

ON THIS DAY

1969

Greenwich Village, New York City, USA

On 28 June 1969, in the early hours of the morning, a police raid on the Stonewall Inn — a gay bar on Christopher Street in Greenwich Village — was met with sustained resistance from the bar's patrons and the gathered crowd outside, in a series of confrontations that continued across the following several days and that the subsequent historiography of the gay rights movement has identified as the inaugural event of the modern public LGBTQ political struggle. The Stonewall events were not vacilando — the participants knew very well where they were and what was at stake — but the broader cultural geography of which the Stonewall Inn was part, the network of bars, baths, neighbourhoods, and walking routes that constituted the gay urban geography of mid-century American cities, was substantially organised around the kind of unsupervised aimless going that the surrounding straight society was at the time actively attempting to suppress. The recovery of the right to walk a city without surveillance, which the Stonewall riots advanced for one specific community, is part of the larger history of the conditions under which vacilando is possible at all.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    When was the last time you went somewhere — walked, drove, travelled — with no destination as the explicit purpose, the going itself being the point?

    How did you feel during the going, and what did the absence of a destination free up that goal-directed travelling does not? And what does it say about your current pattern of life that the answer is what it is?

    Spend at least an hour this week travelling — by foot, by bicycle, by car if necessary — with no destination. Do not take a phone or camera. Let the going go where it goes. Notice both what becomes available in the absence of direction and what feels uncomfortable about the unstructured time, and let both observations inform what you do next.

  • 2

    The essay distinguishes two modes of attention — the route-directed and the present-quality — and suggests that contemporary urban life has substantially atrophied the second. In your own experience, can you reliably enter the second mode, or do you find yourself slipping back into the first even when the conditions for the second are available?

    What conditions, in your own case, most reliably produce the present-quality mode of attention? And are those conditions available to you often enough to maintain the capacity, or have you reached the point where the capacity has begun to atrophy from disuse?

    Pick one activity you regularly perform in the route-directed mode and try, this week, to perform it in the present-quality mode instead. Notice both the difficulty of the shift and what the present-quality mode reveals that the route-directed mode had been suppressing.

  • 3

    The essay warns that the celebration of aimless wandering can become a leisured-class self-congratulation available primarily to people whose economic position permits unproductive time. Have you noticed this pattern in the contemporary slow-travel literature you have encountered, and have you participated in it?

    How would you distinguish, in your own life, between the practice of unproductive going as a genuine recovery of an undervalued mode of experience and the practice as a status performance that depends on economic privilege for its conditions of possibility?

    If you have the resources to undertake unproductive going at all, consider this week whether your practice of it includes any acknowledgment of the conditions that make it available to you — and whether you are doing anything, in your own life, to make some version of the practice available to people whose conditions do not include the same privilege.

TEST YOURSELF

John Steinbeck reported that he had encountered the specialised sense of 'vacilando' — travelling without a destination — during which earlier period of his life, in a location he would return to as a touchstone for several of his subsequent works?

Steinbeck reported in 'Travels with Charley' (1962) that he had picked up the specialised sense of 'vacilando' during his travels through Mexico in the late 1940s, the same period during which he was working on 'The Pearl' and 'The Wayward Bus' and in which his engagement with Mexican culture was at its most intense. The reported attribution has been questioned by subsequent scholarship, and some Mexican Spanish linguists have not been able to confirm the specialised sense as a current usage in Mexican Spanish, but Steinbeck himself consistently attributed the word's specialised meaning to his Mexican experience and the attribution has continued to circulate in the English-language secondary literature regardless of its disputed accuracy.

MOVIE TIME

Wendy and Lucy

Kelly Reichardt · 2008

A young woman drives from Indiana toward Alaska, hoping to find seasonal work in a cannery, accompanied by her dog Lucy. Her car breaks down in a small Oregon town. The film, barely eighty minutes long, follows the slow disintegration of her plans across the few days she spends stranded. Reichardt's filmmaking is patient with the small textures of unstructured going — the walking through the town, the waiting at the auto shop, the long shots of the road that does not lead to where the protagonist was trying to go — and the film operates as a kind of dark mirror image of the vacilando ideal: the protagonist did not choose the aimless going and cannot afford it, and the film is in part about what unstructured travel looks like when the conditions that would make it pleasurable are not available.

Game answer

tornado

Tornado is a Spanish-derived English word for a violent rotating column of air, not a word for any kind of human travelling. The other four are all words for versions of unstructured going: 'vacilando' (Spanish, travelling without destination), 'flâneur' (French, the urban wanderer), 'Wanderung' (German, a long ramble or hike), and 'dérive' (French, the Situationist practice of unplanned drift through urban space). Each names a slightly different cultural inflection of the same underlying practice.
