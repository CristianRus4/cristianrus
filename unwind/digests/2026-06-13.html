The Energy to Bother

Between the recognition that something needs to be done and the doing of it, there is a small undocumented stretch of psychological territory that contains most of the daily life of an adult human being. The dishwasher needs unloading. The form needs filling in. The friend who has been worrying needs the call. The thing on the desk has been on the desk for three days. The decision was made yesterday. The reasoning was sound. The will is intact. What is missing is a small intermediate substance, neither motivation nor discipline, that the rest of the architecture cannot manufacture and whose absence renders the entire chain unactionable.

Estonian has a verb for the having of this substance. Viitsima: to be in a state of inner willingness to bother doing something. The verb is in everyday use across Estonian speech, deployed dozens of times a day in conversation about what one viitsib (is up to doing) and what one ei viitsi (cannot be bothered to do). The distinction the verb makes is precisely the one English fails to make. English handles the territory with awkward compound expressions — 'can be bothered,' 'feel up to,' 'have it in me' — none of which is grammatically a single verb and all of which carry a slight apologetic register, as if having or not having this substance were a moral failing rather than a basic feature of human psychology.

The Estonian construction treats viitsima as morally neutral. To say 'ma ei viitsi' — 'I cannot be bothered' — is in Estonian a simple report on one's inner state, not a confession of laziness. The Estonian assumption, which the English-speaking culture has not fully made, is that the substance the verb names is a finite renewable resource whose availability fluctuates and whose absence is one of the basic data of daily life. A culture that has named the substance can talk about its presence or absence with the same matter-of-factness with which it can talk about being hungry or tired, and can adjust the day's expectations accordingly without dramatising the adjustment.

The English-language productivity literature has tended to treat the absence of viitsima as a problem to be solved through better systems, better habits, better self-management. The Estonian framing suggests something different: that the substance the verb names is not a malfunction when it is absent but a real state with its own causes, and that the appropriate response is sometimes to find what the body and mind require for its return rather than to push through its absence by force. A person whose viitsima is depleted and who pushes through anyway can produce the work, but at a cost that the productivity literature consistently underestimates and that the longer-term well-being literature has begun to document with increasing clarity.

What is interesting about the depletion of viitsima is how poorly it correlates with the apparent difficulty of the task at hand. A person can spend an entire Saturday afternoon unable to summon the will to send a single two-line email and produce, the following Monday, a complex report in three hours. The variable is not the difficulty. The variable is the state of the substance the Estonian verb names, and the substance has its own weather, partially explained by sleep and food and social load and the cumulative residue of recent demands, but never fully reducible to any of these. The honest account of one's own viitsima requires the recognition that the substance is real, partly understood, and not fully controllable.

What this opens up is a different relation to the activity of getting things done. The Anglo-Saxon model treats the will as the engine, the task as the cargo, and the obstacle as the moral category in which 'cannot be bothered' belongs. The Estonian model treats viitsima as a separate variable whose state is one of the basic data of the day, and treats the practical question — what can be done now, given the state of this variable — as a question whose answer changes hour by hour and whose accurate reading is itself a skill. The reading is the precondition for any of the subsequent self-management. The cultures that have a word for the substance can read it more accurately. The cultures that do not have the word have spent the past century treating the absence as a character flaw.

THE TENSION

Naming viitsima as a discrete psychological variable is a substantial gain in self-understanding for cultures that have lacked the vocabulary. The Anglo-Saxon productivity literature has done real damage by treating the substance's absence as a willpower failure to be overcome by better technique, when in fact the absence is data about the person's actual state and treating it as such — rather than as a problem — produces more sustainable engagement with what actually has to be done.

The valorisation of viitsima risks becoming a sophisticated permission structure for avoidance, dressed up as a more honest reading of one's inner state. There are plenty of cases in which what is needed is precisely to do the thing despite the absence of the inner willingness — the difficult conversation, the deferred task, the obligation to others — and a vocabulary that legitimates not doing on the grounds of inner unwillingness can erode the capacities that the older Anglo-Saxon framing, for all its harshness, was trying to maintain.

THE NUMBER

1.1 million

Approximate number of native speakers of Estonian — one of the smallest of the Finno-Ugric languages, spoken primarily within the borders of one of the smallest member states of the European Union, and the source of a number of lexical items that handle aspects of inner life for which the larger European languages have no equivalent.

A FRAGMENT

She had cleared her morning specifically to write the letter. She sat down at the desk. She opened the laptop. She made the coffee. She drank the coffee. She moved a small stack of books from one corner of the desk to the other. She opened the email program and stared at it. She closed it. She opened the document. The cursor blinked. She got up and went to the window. She came back. After an hour she wrote one sentence. After an hour and forty minutes she deleted it. She thought, in the Estonian phrase she had recently learned, 'ma ei viitsi,' and she stopped trying. The letter would be written tomorrow or the day after. Today the substance was not available, and the substance not being available was a fact about today, not a verdict on her.

THE WORD TO KNOW

viitsima

/ˈʋiːtsʲˌima/

Estonian, a Finno-Ugric language of the Baltic region most closely related to Finnish and to the smaller Livonian and Votic languages of the eastern Baltic coast. The verb 'viitsima' is in active everyday use, conjugated regularly across persons and tenses, and is one of a small set of Estonian verbs that name aspects of inner volitional state with a precision the surrounding European languages do not quite match. The verb is part of what Estonian linguists sometimes describe as the inner-state register of the language, which includes a number of distinctions between modes of willingness, energy, and inclination that English handles only through periphrasis.

To be in a state of inner willingness to bother doing something; to have the small intermediate substance, neither motivation nor discipline, whose presence makes an intended action actually occur and whose absence renders an entirely intended action impossible despite intact reasoning and intact will. Used in Estonian as a simple morally neutral report on one's current state, distinct from any account of laziness or moral failing.

A QUOTE

On vaja olnud teha, aga ma ei ole viitsinud. Ja mitte sellepärast, et ma oleksin laisk, vaid sellepärast, et inimene ei viitsi alati kõike, mida ta tegema peaks.

Jaan Kaplinski · Päevaraamat (Daybook), 1989

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Lennart Meri

1929 – 2006 · Tallinn, Estonia

Lennart Meri was the Estonian writer, ethnographer, documentary filmmaker, and first President of the restored Republic of Estonia, serving from 1992 to 2001 during the country's reorientation from Soviet republic to member of NATO and the European Union. Before his political career, Meri had been deported with his family to Siberia in 1941 at the age of twelve, returning to Estonia after the war and conducting, over the following four decades, a remarkable series of ethnographic expeditions to the Finno-Ugric peoples of the Russian north — the Khanty, the Mansi, the Nenets, the Mari — producing both documentary films and the books, including 'Hõbevalge' (Silver White, 1976), that recovered for Estonian readers a sense of their place within a broader Finno-Ugric cultural geography.

Meri's complication is that the presidency he assumed at sixty-three required him to set aside, substantially, the cultural-intellectual life that had been his vocation, and to conduct in its place the deeply unglamorous diplomatic work of integrating a small post-Soviet republic into the institutional architecture of the West. He did the work, and did it well, and Estonia's transition during the 1990s was among the most successful of any post-Soviet state. But the books and films Meri did not make during the presidential years are part of the cost his country owed him after his term ended, and which his health, declining through his last years, did not allow him to make. He died in 2006 with several of those projects unwritten. The figure who became the public face of the restored republic is more widely remembered than the ethnographer who would, in a different timeline, have produced a more substantial body of work on the cultural geography he had spent his earlier life mapping.

NOW HERE

Lahemaa National Park

Northern coast, Lääne-Viru and Harju counties, Estonia

Estonia's oldest national park, established in 1971 along the rocky northern coast east of Tallinn, encompassing a complex landscape of bog, forest, glacial erratics, manor houses, and small fishing villages along the limestone cliffs of the Baltic. The park preserves both the natural geography — the alvar grasslands on thin limestone soils, the spruce and pine forests, the kettle bogs slowly accreting peat — and the older Estonian architectural and cultural landscape of the coastal villages, where the wooden buildings, the boat-houses, and the small fishing harbours still bear the structure of pre-Soviet rural life. The park is one of the places where the cultural pattern of which the verb viitsima is one small lexical item is still recognisable as a way of life: the slow, weather-attuned, energy-conserving rhythm of small-population life on a difficult coast, where the inner state of willingness is one of the basic variables on which the day's work has to be planned.

ON THIS DAY

1991

Tallinn, Estonia

On 13 June 1991, two months before Estonia's full restoration of independence, the country's Supreme Council adopted a resolution authorising the language law that would make Estonian the sole official language of the country and require the use of Estonian for all administrative business of the state. The law, passed in its full form earlier the same year, was one of the central instruments by which the small Baltic republic recovered its public life after fifty years of Soviet occupation during which Russian had been the dominant language of administration, education, and large parts of urban Estonia. The resolution was, among other things, a decision to maintain a language with one and a half million speakers as a working modern administrative language in a global environment that has made such a maintenance progressively more demanding. The viitsima the project has required is not the small daily one the essay describes but its collective sibling — the sustained civic willingness to keep doing the work that a smaller language requires of its community to remain a working medium of public life.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    How accurately, on a typical day, can you read the state of your own viitsima — the small substance the Estonian verb names — and adjust your expectations and plans accordingly?

    When you cannot summon the substance, what is your default response: to push through, to defer, to switch tasks, to investigate what the absence might be signalling? And which of these responses, on reflection, serves you best?

    Spend three days noticing, at hourly intervals, the state of your willingness to bother with the next thing on your list. Without judging the state, simply note it: high, medium, low. Notice patterns. Notice also what activities, foods, or people the substance correlates with.

  • 2

    Are there obligations in your life that you have been failing to meet not because of any defect of intention or reasoning but because the substance the Estonian verb names has been absent — and what would honest acknowledgment of this require of you?

    If you acknowledged the absence honestly to the people affected, would they be able to receive the acknowledgment, or has the surrounding culture trained them to hear it only as evasion? And how would you signal the difference?

    Identify one deferred obligation in your life. Write, but do not necessarily send, a short note that explains honestly that the inner willingness has not been available, that you are not unwilling but unable in this small specific sense, and that you are working on the return of the substance rather than on the discipline to override its absence. Notice what writing the note clarifies, whether or not it ever gets sent.

  • 3

    What conditions, in your experience, are the most reliable producers of viitsima — what consistently restores the substance when it has been depleted?

    Are those conditions ones you have access to often, or are they structurally rare in your current life? And if they are rare, what would have to change for them to be more available?

    Pick one condition you have identified as a reliable restorer of the substance — a particular walk, a particular kind of meal, a particular kind of conversation — and arrange this week for it to occur at least three times. Track whether the substance becomes more available, and whether the things you have been deferring begin to be done.

TEST YOURSELF

Estonian belongs to which language family, whose other members include Finnish, Hungarian, and a number of smaller languages spoken across northern Eurasia?

Estonian belongs to the Finnic branch of the Finno-Ugric (or Uralic) language family, together with Finnish, Karelian, Veps, Ingrian, Votic, and the now nearly extinct Livonian. The broader Uralic family also includes Hungarian (in the Ugric branch), Sámi (in its own branch), and a number of smaller languages spoken by indigenous peoples across northern Russia and Siberia. Estonian is not Indo-European; despite being surrounded by Indo-European languages (Latvian, Russian, Swedish across the Baltic, German historically), it shares its deep grammatical and lexical structure with Finnish and the other Finnic languages, with which it remains partially mutually intelligible at the level of basic vocabulary.

MOVIE TIME

In the Crosswind

Martti Helde · 2014

An Estonian feature about the 1941 mass deportations to Siberia, based on the letters of a young woman separated from her husband and sent to a labour camp. The film is composed almost entirely of long static tableaux — actors held in motionless poses while the camera moves through them — that turn each scene into a kind of stilled photograph. The form embodies a particular question the essay raises: what does the substance the Estonian verb names look like under conditions of long sustained deprivation, and what becomes possible (and impossible) when an entire population's reserves of the substance have been systematically exhausted by external violence rather than by ordinary depletion.

Game answer

õun

Õun is Estonian for 'apple' — a concrete noun for a fruit, not a verb of inner state. The other four are verbs that name distinct dimensions of willingness, capacity, endurance, or wanting: viitsima (to be willing to bother), jaksama (to have the strength or stamina for), kannama (to bear, to be able to carry), tahtma (to want, to will). Estonian's vocabulary of inner state is unusually finely distinguished, with each of these verbs occupying a specific semantic territory that English handles only with periphrasis.
