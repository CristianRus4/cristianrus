The Form That Resistance Makes Possible

In 1969, Georges Perec published a novel of 300 pages in French without using the letter 'e' — the most common letter in the language. 'La Disparition' was not a stunt or a parlour trick. It was a work of genuine structural originality, in which the very absence of the letter generated meaning: the word 'père' (father) cannot be written, nor 'mère' (mother), nor 'famille.' The constraint was not arbitrary decoration around otherwise free composition; it was the compositional principle itself. The form made the book possible. Without the wall to push against, there would have been nothing to push.

This is what the Oulipo group — founded in Paris in 1960 by the mathematician François Le Lionnais and the writer Raymond Queneau — was proposing as a systematic theory of literary creation: that constraint is not the enemy of invention but its engine. The group's founding insight was that the apparently free poem or novel is not free at all; it merely runs on inherited conventions whose constraining force has been made invisible by familiarity. The Oulipian made the constraint explicit and deliberate, which was a way of making the generative act visible.

Igor Stravinsky articulated the same principle from the position of a composer who had spent decades working within and against inherited forms: the more constraints one imposes, the more one frees oneself. The logic is counterintuitive but structurally precise. A constraint closes off the infinite, which is paralyzing, and produces instead a finite set of genuinely interesting problems. The sonnet's fourteen lines and required turn do not limit what can be said in a sonnet; they produce the specific pressure that makes the specific thinking the form enables. The constraint is the generator.

Viktor Shklovsky, writing in Russia in 1917, described the mechanism at a different level: art's function is ostranenie — defamiliarisation — making the ordinary strange enough to be seen again. Habitual perception is not perception; it is recognition, which is cheaper and faster than actual looking. The formal constraint, at its best, performs a version of this: by forcing language or image or sound into an unexpected configuration, it disrupts the automatic and requires genuine attention. The reader or listener is returned to the position of someone who does not yet know what they are reading, which is the only position from which the thing can be experienced rather than merely processed.

This is why the constraint tradition in art is not a tradition of difficulty for its own sake but a tradition of recovered attention. The Ryōan-ji rock garden in Kyoto — fifteen rocks in raked gravel, unchanged for five hundred years — cannot be exhausted. Not because its maker was incomprehensibly skilled, though they were skilled, but because the formal system they created continues to produce new spatial relationships and new figures of attention in every viewer and in every light. The constraint makes the inexhaustibility.

What resists in all of this is the association of freedom with absence of limit. The blank page is not freedom; it is the place where nothing has happened yet. The constraint is not the opposite of freedom but its specific instantiation: the shape freedom takes when it has found the wall it needed to become force. This does not apply universally — constraint without internal logic is merely arbitrary difficulty, and the distinction between a generative limit and a merely punishing one requires genuine judgment. What it proposes is a different question for any creative act: not 'what should I do?' but 'what am I working against?'

THE TENSION

The Oulipian argument is that constraint is not a limitation on creation but its engine: the explicit formal rule closes off the infinite, which is paralyzing, and generates instead a finite set of genuinely productive problems. If this is right, the history of artistic originality is not a history of breaking free from form but of finding the right form to push against — which means that what we call creative freedom is always the product of a specific resistance.

The constraint argument risks becoming a doctrine that aestheticises difficulty for its own sake and mistakes procedural ingenuity for depth. Not every formally constrained work is inexhaustible: most are clever at best, and the intellectual interest in how the constraint was met does not automatically translate into the kind of meaning that outlasts the novelty. The Oulipo's most successful works succeed despite their constraints as much as because of them, which suggests that the constraint is a necessary but not sufficient condition for what actually matters.

THE NUMBER

Possible poems in Raymond Queneau's 'Cent Mille Milliards de Poèmes' (1961) — a book of ten sonnets whose fourteen lines can each be independently combined, producing a combinatorial library that would take more than 190 million years to read completely at one sonnet per minute.

A FRAGMENT

She had given herself the rule when she started: one constraint, strictly observed, for every project. She had not expected it to work. She had expected to produce the kind of thing that demonstrates a rule. Instead the constraint had done something she had not planned for: it had found the part of the problem she hadn't known was there, which the constraint had no reason to find but had found anyway, as if the wall knew something about the room she was trying to build.

THE WORD TO KNOW

ostranenie

/ˌɒstrəˈnɛniə/ — остранение

Russian; coined by Viktor Shklovsky in his 1917 essay 'Art as Technique' (Iskusstvo kak priyom), the founding document of Russian Formalist literary theory; from 'stranit' (to make strange) or 'strannyi' (strange, odd); the deliberate technique of presenting familiar things in unfamiliar ways to restore the sensation of seeing them rather than merely recognizing them; translated variously as 'defamiliarisation,' 'estrangement,' or 'making strange.'

Defamiliarisation; the artistic technique of presenting what is familiar in such a way that it is perceived as strange — restoring the sensation of genuine experience to things that habitual perception has made automatic. In Shklovsky's formulation, art exists to overcome the automatism of perception; the successful work makes the ordinary visible again by making it strange enough to require real looking.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Raymond Queneau

1903 – 1976 · Le Havre, France / Paris, France

Raymond Queneau was the French novelist, poet, and mathematician who, in 1960, co-founded the Ouvroir de Littérature Potentielle — Oulipo — with the mathematician François Le Lionnais. The group's project was the systematic study of formal constraint as a generative literary method: not the accidental constraints of received form but the deliberate, explicit, mathematically rigorous constraint chosen for its productive properties. Queneau's own contributions included 'Exercices de Style' (1947), a single trivial anecdote retold in ninety-nine different stylistic registers, and 'Cent Mille Milliards de Poèmes' (1961), a book of ten sonnets designed so that each of the fourteen lines could be freely exchanged across all ten poems, generating 10 to the power of 14 — one hundred trillion — valid sonnets from a book that physically fits in one hand.

Queneau held a doctorate in mathematics and worked for decades as an editor and reader at Gallimard, the most prestigious literary publisher in France, where he was instrumental in recognising and supporting major postwar writers. He was simultaneously working on a popular history of mathematics intended for general readers, and his literary work consistently returned to the border between numerical structure and literary form. His novel 'Zazie dans le Métro' (1959) — about a provincial girl's anarchic day in Paris — was a popular success while its linguistic playfulness anticipated by a decade the techniques of the nouveau roman. He is substantially better known in France than outside it, and substantially better known by writers than by general readers — a figure who changed the possibility space of literature without ever becoming a name most people can place.

NOW HERE

Ryōan-ji Rock Garden

Kyoto, Japan

Fifteen rocks arranged in raked white gravel, enclosed by a low oil-soaked clay wall, in a garden approximately the size of a tennis court. The garden was created in the late fifteenth century at a Rinzai Zen temple in the northwest of Kyoto. No one knows who designed it or what it represents, if it represents anything. The system it creates is formally simple and apparently inexhaustible: from any position within the garden, only fourteen of the fifteen rocks are visible — the fifteenth is always hidden behind another. The garden has been visited and contemplated for five hundred years without producing an agreed account of what it contains. Its constraint is absolute and its output is open. The wall holds the whole thing together by limiting the world to just this.

ON THIS DAY

1819

West Hills, New York, USA

On 31 May 1819, Walt Whitman was born in West Hills, Long Island. He would spend the next three decades developing a form of verse so different from its predecessors that it appeared to have no form at all — the long catalytic lines, the grammatical accumulations, the use of the catalogue as a structural principle. What Whitman had done was not abandon formal constraint but invent a new one: a measure built from the spoken phrase rather than the metrical foot, from the rhythms of the King James Bible and the Italian opera he loved rather than from inherited English prosody. 'Leaves of Grass' is among the most formally deliberate books in English poetry, which is visible only to anyone who tries to write like it and finds they cannot.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a constraint you have worked under — a formal limit, a material restriction, a deadline, a rule you gave yourself — that generated something you would not have found without it?

    Was the generative force of the constraint visible to you while you were working under it, or only in retrospect? And if it was only visible in retrospect, what does that suggest about whether you could choose constraints deliberately and produce the same effect?

    Give yourself an arbitrary formal constraint for the next creative or intellectual task you undertake — a word limit, a structural rule, a prohibited element — and observe whether the constraint makes the problem easier or harder to solve. Notice what it forces you to find that you were not looking for.

  • 2

    Shklovsky argued that habitual perception is not perception — that we recognise rather than see. Is there something in your daily experience that you stopped genuinely perceiving at some point and now only recognise?

    Is there a technique you have found — deliberately or accidentally — for returning familiar things to genuine perception? And is this a skill that can be practiced, or does the technique itself become habitual and eventually cease to defamiliarise?

    Describe, in writing, something you see every day — a street, a room, a face — as if you were encountering it for the first time and had no prior category to place it in. Notice what the ordinary description omits and what the unfamiliar description makes visible.

  • 3

    The Oulipo group argued that received literary forms are also constraints — just invisible ones. Do you experience the conventions you work within (in any domain) as constraints, or have they become so familiar that they feel like the natural shape of the thing?

    What would it look like to make the invisible constraints in your own practice explicit — to name them as constraints rather than as the way things are? And would naming them change your relationship to them, or would the naming itself become another form of habituation?

    Identify one domain in which you work regularly and list the unspoken rules that govern how you do it — things you do without deciding to do them, defaults you have never questioned. Pick one and try working without it for a day. Notice what the absence reveals about what the rule was doing.

TEST YOURSELF

Georges Perec's 'La Disparition' (1969) is a 300-page novel written without the most common letter in French. What formal constraint governs its English translation, 'A Void' (1994), by Gilbert Adair?

Gilbert Adair's English translation 'A Void' (1994) maintained the exact same constraint as the original: no letter 'e' appears anywhere in the 300-page text, despite 'e' being the most common letter in English as well as in French. This required Adair to produce not merely a translation but a parallel constrained composition — finding English equivalents for Perec's French while simultaneously observing the same formal rule. The translation is widely considered one of the most virtuosic achievements in the history of literary translation.

MOVIE TIME

The Lighthouse

Robert Eggers · 2019

Two lighthouse keepers stranded on a remote New England island in the 1890s, shot in black and white in a near-square aspect ratio (1.19:1) that reproduces the format of nineteenth-century maritime photography. Eggers chose the format for historical reasons but it functions as a constraint in the Oulipian sense: the compressed frame removes the panoramic, forces attention to faces and confined space, and makes the island feel genuinely inescapable. The film's formal limits — monochrome, near-square, single location, two characters — produce an intensity that wider, more freely composed cinema forecloses. The constraints are visible as constraints, and the meaning they generate could not have arrived any other way.
