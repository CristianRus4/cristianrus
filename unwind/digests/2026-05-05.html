What the Exile Carries That the Citizen Does Not

To lose a place is not the same as losing it from memory. The exile often knows the country left behind with a precision that the person still living there cannot achieve — because the one who stays does not need to reconstruct it. The country of departure becomes, for the person who leaves, an object of sustained interior labour.

This is one of the minor cruelties of displacement: that the place intensifies in the mind at the same rate it recedes in practice. A street corner in a city you will likely never return to can become more known — in a particular way — than any street you walk daily. The daily street requires no effort of holding. The other one must be held or it will dissolve.

Writers who produced their major work in a language not their first, or in a country not their origin, often describe this dual inhabitation not as a loss but as a clarification. The home culture, seen from outside, gives up its naturalisations. What had seemed inevitable about it becomes contingent. What had seemed universal becomes local. The loss of unselfconsciousness is the condition of this particular kind of seeing.

The country of departure does not stay still while you are gone. But neither do you.

There is a phrase that appears in many forms across exile literatures: the idea that exile teaches you what you actually are, because it strips away the ambient confirmation that you previously mistook for identity. When the city no longer reflects you back, you are forced to ask what there is of you that does not depend on reflection.

But the clarification is not clean. The person the exile becomes cannot return to the person who left. The home country, if re-entered, is inhabited differently — as a place that was once known and is now also remembered, which is a stranger relation than simply knowing it. The two layers never fully coincide.

What the exile carries that the citizen does not is a permanent awareness of contingency — that to live in a place is always a choice among possible placements, and that identity is less a possession than an ongoing negotiation with a context that could always be otherwise.

A FRAGMENT

He carried the name of the street but not the smell. He could tell you the exact sequence of facades, the slight incline, where the baker had been before the pharmacy replaced it. But when he tried to imagine standing there he arrived at something diagrammatic. A plan, not a place. He did not know when the smell had gone. He thought it might have been the third year. He thought it might have been earlier, the night he understood that going back would mean going back to a version that no longer existed, and that he was the reason it no longer existed.

THE WORD TO KNOW

fremde

German — from Old High German fremidi, meaning belonging to another, estranged, foreign. Related to the English word 'frame' through a shared Proto-Germanic root, but carrying a weight of subjective estrangement not present in the English cognate. The word can mean both the foreign place and the feeling of foreignness as a state of the self.

The condition of being in a place — or being a self — that does not feel like one's own; the felt quality of not-belonging, experienced as a feature of a location, a language, or one's own reflection.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Hélène Cixous

1937 – present · Oran, Algeria / Paris, France

Hélène Cixous was born in colonial Algeria to a German-Jewish father and a Sephardic mother, grew up speaking French and German in a city whose culture was Arabic, and arrived in France as an already displaced person. Her early theoretical work, particularly the 1975 essay 'The Laugh of the Medusa', argued that language itself encodes the structures of exclusion, and that writing from the margin — from the body, from the position of one who has never been assumed to be the speaker — was not a disadvantage but a different kind of access to what language could do.

Her later work moved away from manifestos and toward what she called 'fictional criticism' — texts that dissolved the boundary between autobiography, theory, and prose poetry. She was a close collaborator of Jacques Derrida for decades, though her work retained a warmth and embodied specificity that contrasted with his more architecturally abstract mode. She has described her own writing practice as one of not-knowing: she does not write toward conclusions but follows language into territory she could not have mapped in advance.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    What does it mean to know a place — and is the kind of knowing that comes from leaving sharper or just different from the kind that comes from staying?

    What have you understood about a place or a period of your life only once it was behind you?

    Write for ten minutes about a place you no longer live. Write only what you can still reconstruct in precise detail — and notice where the reconstruction breaks down.

  • 2

    How much of what you call your identity depends on a context that happens to surround you, rather than on something you carry independently of context?

    If you were removed from your current environment entirely, which parts of yourself would persist and which would dissolve?

    Spend one hour today in a completely unfamiliar environment — a neighbourhood you never visit, a park you don't know — and pay attention to how you present yourself to yourself without your usual context.

  • 3

    Is the dual inhabitation of exile — belonging nowhere fully — a poverty or a form of freedom that single-placed people cannot access?

    Are there ways in which you already live in more than one place simultaneously, without having physically left?

    Identify the place in your life you feel most ambivalent about — neither fully yours nor fully foreign — and write one paragraph about what that ambivalence actually consists of.
