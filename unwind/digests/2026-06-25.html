Harmonious Resonance Between People, Things, Situations

There is a phenomenon in social life that everyone has experienced and that the dominant European vocabularies have generally failed to name with precision. A dinner party reaches a state in which the conversation, the food, the wine, the light, the music, the particular configuration of who is sitting next to whom, all begin to operate together as a single thing rather than as a collection of separate elements. The room has come into tune with itself. The participants can feel it. None of them caused it; each of them is now contributing to maintaining it. The state has its own logic, its own pacing, its own reluctance to be interrupted by extraneous demands. The state has, while it lasts, a quality of rightness that the participants will remember for years.

Javanese has a word for this. Tjotjog (in the older Dutch-influenced spelling) or cocok (in the modernised Indonesian-influenced spelling) names the quality of harmonious mutual resonance between elements — between people, between people and food, between a person and a place, between an idea and the moment it is offered. The anthropologist Clifford Geertz, in his 1960 monograph 'The Religion of Java,' devoted substantial attention to the concept as one of the central organising terms of Javanese social and aesthetic experience. The word names not the achievement of a harmony imposed from outside, but the discovery that certain particular combinations of elements simply are in harmony — that the resonance is there to be found rather than constructed.

What is distinctive about the Javanese concept is its inclusiveness across categories that the European vocabulary tends to keep separate. A particular dish can be tjotjog with a particular guest. A particular kind of music can be tjotjog with a particular kind of weather. A particular profession can be tjotjog with a particular kind of person, in a way that has nothing to do with talent or training and everything to do with the alignment between the person's inner constitution and the demands of the work. A marriage can be tjotjog, or fail to be. A piece of clothing can be tjotjog with the wearer, in a way the wearer feels immediately on putting it on and that does not depend on conventional standards of style. The concept proposes that there are correct matches between elements in many more domains than European thought has typically acknowledged and that the recognition of these matches is one of the basic skills of a well-conducted life.

What is also distinctive is the assumption embedded in the concept that not everything can be made tjotjog through effort. The harmony is discovered rather than produced. A combination that is not tjotjog can sometimes be made workable through accommodation and patience, but it will not become tjotjog merely because the participants have decided that it should be. The recognition of when a combination is tjotjog, and when it is not, is one of the skills the surrounding culture works to develop in its members, and the absence of the skill is treated as a real deficiency. A person who consistently makes non-tjotjog choices — wrong marriages, wrong professions, wrong friendships — is regarded not as unfortunate but as missing one of the basic capacities for navigating a life.

The implication, which European vocabularies have not generally articulated, is that the right combinations of elements have a kind of objective character that careful attention can detect. The Javanese assumption is not that beauty is in the eye of the beholder. The Javanese assumption is that the world contains many possible alignments and many possible misalignments, that the alignments produce a recognisable quality the surrounding culture has named, and that the cultivation of the perception of this quality is one of the principal projects of education. The dinner party that has come into tune with itself is not in tune because the participants have agreed it is; it is in tune because the elements have, in fact, found their proper relation, and the participants have been skilful enough — or fortunate enough — to recognise the relation and to leave it undisturbed.

What this opens up, considered seriously, is a different relation to the planning of one's life. The dominant Western vocabulary of self-improvement assumes that the right life is the life one constructs through deliberate choice — the right career chosen for instrumental reasons, the right partner chosen through systematic compatibility analysis, the right city selected by data. The Javanese concept proposes a complementary skill: the ability to recognise tjotjog when one encounters it, and to refuse the combinations that are not tjotjog even when they would be more advantageous on the constructed criteria. The two skills are not opposites; the person who can both deliberately choose and accurately recognise is better equipped than the person with only one or the other. But the recognition skill has been substantially neglected in the dominant Anglo-Saxon vocabulary, and the consequences of the neglect are visible in the high proportion of contemporary lives that look good on paper and feel, to the person living them, like a sustained mismatch.

THE TENSION

The Javanese concept of tjotjog identifies a real and underexamined skill — the recognition of when elements of a life are in harmonious mutual resonance — that the dominant Western vocabulary of deliberate construction has substantially neglected. A person whose life is built only on the constructed criteria, without the cultivation of this recognition skill, is more likely to assemble a life that looks correct from the outside and feels persistently wrong from the inside.

The valorisation of tjotjog as a recognition skill risks providing a sophisticated alibi for refusing to do the difficult constructive work of building a life. The participant can dismiss the difficult marriage, the difficult job, the difficult friendship as 'not tjotjog,' and the diagnosis can become a permission to evade exactly the work that the situation actually required. The Javanese concept assumes a cultural matrix in which the recognition skill is taught from childhood and balanced against substantial commitments to community and family; lifted out of that matrix into Western individualism, it can become a justification for serial abandonment dressed up as careful attunement.

THE NUMBER

55

Approximate number of slendro and pelog scales — the two principal tuning systems of Javanese gamelan music — that traditional Javanese theory recognises as carrying distinct emotional and ceremonial associations, with the appropriate scale for a particular occasion understood as a matter of tjotjog: the recognition that this music, at this time, in this place, is in correct resonance with the surrounding conditions.

A FRAGMENT

She had attended seven dinner parties at the same hostess's apartment over the previous year. Six of them had been pleasant. The seventh, on a Friday evening in October with no obvious special occasion, had been the kind of evening at which time stopped behaving normally. The same hostess. Approximately the same menu. Three of the same eight guests. Something different in the configuration of the rest had produced an evening that the eight participants would each remember separately for the next decade. None of them, asked afterward, could account for it. The hostess had not done anything different. The wine was the same. Something had simply come into tune that had not come into tune the previous times, and there was no procedure by which any of them could now reproduce it.

THE WORD TO KNOW

tjotjog

/ˈtʃɔtʃɔɡ/

Javanese, with the spelling 'tjotjog' reflecting the older Dutch-influenced romanisation used during the colonial period and in the foundational anthropological literature (notably Clifford Geertz's 'The Religion of Java,' 1960). The modern Indonesian-language spelling is 'cocok,' which is the form most commonly encountered in present-day Indonesian writing. The word is in everyday use across Javanese and Indonesian speech, and is one of a small set of Javanese terms that has been carried into anthropological and philosophical English vocabulary as a term of art for a phenomenon other languages do not lexically isolate. The semantic range covers both the specific sense of harmonious resonance and the more general sense of correct fit or appropriate match.

The quality of harmonious mutual resonance between elements — between people, between people and food, between a person and a place, between an idea and the moment it is offered, between any two things whose particular conjunction has produced a state of correct fit. Names not the harmony imposed from outside but the harmony discovered as already present in certain combinations and as already absent in others, and the recognition of which combinations are which is one of the principal social and aesthetic skills the Javanese cultural tradition seeks to develop in its members.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Ki Hadjar Dewantara

1889 – 1959 · Pakualaman, Yogyakarta, Dutch East Indies / Yogyakarta, Indonesia

Ki Hadjar Dewantara — born Raden Mas Soewardi Soerjaningrat into the minor Javanese aristocracy of the Pakualaman court — was the Indonesian educator, journalist, and independence activist whose 1922 founding of the Taman Siswa schools established what became one of the most influential indigenous education systems in colonial Southeast Asia. The Taman Siswa philosophy combined Western pedagogical methods with a deliberate recovery and revaluation of Javanese cultural traditions, including the aesthetic and philosophical vocabulary of which tjotjog is one element. Dewantara was repeatedly imprisoned and exiled by the Dutch colonial authorities for his political activities, and after Indonesian independence in 1945 he served briefly as the first Minister of Education in the new republican government.

Dewantara's complication is the tension between the universalist humanism of his pedagogical writings and the specifically Javanese cultural vocabulary in which he conducted much of his thinking — a tension that has produced contested receptions of his work across the multilingual and multicultural Indonesian state his generation founded. His educational philosophy has been embraced by Javanese cultural conservatives as a defence of regional tradition, by Indonesian nationalists as a foundation for a unified national education, and by international comparative-education scholars as an Asian contribution to the global progressive-education movement. The actual texture of his thinking, which sits somewhat differently from each of these receptions, has been substantially less examined than its various political deployments would suggest.

NOW HERE

Borobudur at Dawn

Magelang Regency, Central Java, Indonesia

The largest Buddhist temple in the world, built in the ninth century in central Java by the Sailendra dynasty, consisting of a stepped pyramid of nine stacked platforms — six square below and three circular above — surmounted by a central stupa, with more than two thousand relief panels and five hundred Buddha statues distributed across the structure. The temple was abandoned in the fourteenth century, slowly buried by jungle and volcanic ash, and rediscovered by the Dutch colonial administration in 1814. In the half-hour before sunrise, the eastern face of the temple receives the first horizontal light of the day across the Kedu Plain, and the Borobudur fits into its landscape with a precision that visitors who have come from many continents have, for two hundred years, recognised as one of the most fully tjotjog conjunctions of built structure and natural setting anywhere in the world. The site is administered by the Indonesian government and is one of the principal pilgrimage destinations for Buddhists across Southeast Asia, in addition to its UNESCO World Heritage status.

ON THIS DAY

1950

Pyongyang and Seoul, divided Korea

On 25 June 1950, units of the Korean People's Army crossed the 38th parallel and invaded the territory of the Republic of Korea, beginning the Korean War. The war that followed would last three years and would kill approximately 2.5 million people, most of them Korean civilians. It would also, more lastingly, install the partition of the Korean peninsula that has now persisted for more than seventy years and shaped the cultural, economic, and political trajectories of both states. The Korean concept of han, which the digest of 15 June described, is substantially the long emotional residue of this war and of the partition that followed it. The Javanese concept of tjotjog and the Korean concept of han are not, in any direct sense, related — but they belong to the larger family of Asian cultural terms that name precisely the conditions whose disturbance produces the deep social wounds that the second half of the twentieth century made widely visible across the continent.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Can you recall a specific occasion on which a particular combination of people, place, and conditions came into the kind of mutual resonance the Javanese word names — and what allowed you to recognise it as such?

    Did the occasion teach you something about what tjotjog feels like that you could now use to recognise it more reliably in future, or did it pass through you without leaving a portable lesson? And what does the answer suggest about how the recognition skill is acquired?

    Spend a week paying particular attention, at every meal, gathering, or conversation, to the moments at which the configuration comes briefly into mutual resonance. Notice the conditions that obtained when it did, and the small signs that signal the state to a careful observer. Notice also what you do, habitually, that interferes with the recognition.

  • 2

    The Javanese tradition treats the consistent inability to recognise tjotjog as a real deficiency. Where in your own life have you made choices that the constructed criteria endorsed and that your perception of fit would have warned against, had you been listening to it?

    Were you genuinely unable to perceive the misfit at the time, or were you choosing not to attend to a perception that was available? And which of the two diagnoses is more uncomfortable to accept?

    Identify one current decision in your life — about work, about a relationship, about a commitment — where the constructed criteria endorse one choice and your felt sense of fit endorses another. Sit with the discrepancy for a week without resolving it. Notice which voice grows louder under sustained attention, and which one was louder only because you had been training yourself to hear it.

  • 3

    How would you teach the recognition skill — the perception of when elements are in tjotjog and when they are not — to a person who had not been raised in a culture that taught it from childhood?

    Is the skill teachable at all in adulthood, or does it require the kind of formative cultural immersion that no adult intervention can substitute for? And if it is teachable, what would the curriculum consist of?

    If you have access to a person — a child, a friend, a younger colleague — who has been making choices that consistently produce non-tjotjog combinations, attempt this week to describe the recognition skill to them in language that does not assume they have already acquired it. Notice what the description requires and what it cannot quite reach, and let the difficulty teach you about what the skill actually consists of.

TEST YOURSELF

Which anthropologist's 1960 monograph 'The Religion of Java' devoted substantial attention to the concept of tjotjog and substantially introduced it into English-language anthropological vocabulary?

Clifford Geertz's 'The Religion of Java' (1960), based on fieldwork conducted in the small town of Modjokuto (a pseudonym for Pare, in East Java) in the mid-1950s, was the principal early ethnographic work to treat tjotjog as a central organising concept of Javanese social and aesthetic life. Geertz's subsequent work — particularly the essays collected in 'The Interpretation of Cultures' (1973) — would develop the 'thick description' methodology for which he is most widely remembered, but 'The Religion of Java' remains the most-cited single English-language ethnographic treatment of the Javanese conceptual vocabulary, including its terms for harmony, fit, and the recognition of correct combination.

MOVIE TIME

Opera Jawa

Garin Nugroho · 2006

An Indonesian operatic film commissioned by Peter Sellars as part of the 2006 Vienna Mozart Year, freely adapting the Ramayana story into a contemporary Javanese village setting in which the entire dialogue is sung in Javanese gamelan and the choreography draws on classical Javanese dance traditions. Garin Nugroho's film is a sustained working through, in cinematic form, of the question of tjotjog at every level — the alignment of the actors with their roles, the alignment of the gamelan with the drama, the alignment of the contemporary social anxieties with the inherited mythological material — and the film is most affecting in the moments at which these alignments lock into place across the visual, musical, and emotional registers simultaneously.

rasa — The Sanskrit (and broader South and Southeast Asian) aesthetic concept of the distilled emotional flavour of a work of art or an experience — the essence that careful attention extracts

ma — The Japanese concept of significant negative space, pause, or interval — the empty between things as itself a presence in design, music, and conversation
