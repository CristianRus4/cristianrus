A Person Becomes a Person Through Other Persons

There is a Bantu word, in regular use across much of southern Africa, that proposes a definition of personhood radically different from the one inherited from European philosophy. The European tradition, since at least Descartes, has tended to locate the person in the individual self — a discrete locus of consciousness whose existence is established by its own internal capacity to think. The Bantu word proposes instead that personhood is not a property an individual possesses but a quality that emerges through standing in proper relation to other persons. A person is not a person alone. A person becomes a person through other persons. The famous formulation, in Nguni: umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu.

Ubuntu is the noun, drawn from this formulation, that names the relational ground from which personhood arises. The word entered the broader English-language conversation in the 1990s through the writings of Desmond Tutu and through the post-apartheid Truth and Reconciliation Commission, which used the concept as a partial framework for understanding what it would mean for a society to repair itself. The word has since been popularised, commercialised, used as the name of a Linux distribution, printed on motivational posters in airports, and generally subjected to the fate of any indigenous concept that travels too quickly into the global marketplace of ideas. The original sense, which is the philosophical claim, deserves a more careful hearing than the popularised version has usually allowed.

What the philosophical claim entails, if taken seriously, is that the self-sufficient individual of European modernity is not a basic unit of reality but an abstraction — a useful fiction for certain legal and economic purposes that has been mistaken for a description of how human beings actually are. The Bantu account proposes that the human being is irreducibly relational, that the qualities we usually attribute to the individual (intelligence, character, virtue) are not properties of the individual but properties of the network of relations the individual stands in, and that to be a complete person is to be in functioning relation with other complete persons who are made complete by their relation to you. The implications for ethics are substantial. The implications for psychology are substantial. The implications for political theory are very substantial.

What is often missed in Western discussions of ubuntu is that the concept is not a romantic claim about community as opposed to individuality but a metaphysical claim about what kinds of things human beings are. The contrast with European individualism is not a contrast between two stylistic preferences but between two incompatible ontologies. The European individual is logically prior to the community; the community is what individuals choose to form for various practical reasons. The ubuntu person is logically posterior to the community; the individual is what the community produces in particular nodes of itself, and what continues to exist only as long as the relations are sustained.

There is an obvious objection to be made: a great many people in southern Africa and elsewhere live in conditions of severe social rupture — incarceration, refugee status, mass urban anonymity — and they continue, by any ordinary observation, to be persons. The ubuntu tradition has its responses to this, which are not always the ones a Western reader might expect. One response is that severely ruptured persons are precisely what the surrounding society's failure has produced, and that the apparent persistence of personhood under conditions of rupture is at the cost of qualities the tradition would identify as essential. Another response is that the relations that constitute a person can be relations to ancestors, to spiritual beings, to absent kin — that the network is not limited to the living and the present. The metaphysics is more flexible than its caricature suggests.

What resists in all of this, for a reader trained in the European individualist tradition, is the discomfort of an account that does not begin with the autonomous self as the basic given. The autonomous self is so deeply built into European political vocabulary — rights, freedoms, choices, contracts, consents — that the alternative initially appears either utopian or vaguely menacing. The ubuntu tradition is neither. It is a different starting place, with different consequences, developed by people who have lived inside it long enough to know its strengths and its failure modes. The question it puts to a Western reader is not whether to adopt it but whether the universalist assumption that European individualism is the natural state of human beings has been earned, or merely inherited.

THE TENSION

Ubuntu is a serious metaphysical proposal that European philosophy has substantially failed to engage with on its own terms, treating it instead as either a piece of African folk wisdom or a slogan. The proposal is that personhood is constituted relationally rather than individually, and the proposal is incompatible with the standard European starting point in a way that cannot be reconciled by minor amendments to the latter. A genuine engagement requires letting the proposal challenge the European framework rather than absorbing it as a colourful supplement.

The valorisation of ubuntu as the alternative to European individualism risks both flattening the actual philosophical diversity within African thought and obscuring the political uses to which the concept has been put. The post-apartheid invocation of ubuntu sometimes functioned to ask material concessions from the dispossessed in the name of communal harmony rather than to demand them from the beneficiaries of dispossession, and any serious account of the concept has to reckon with how it has actually operated when deployed by powerful institutions.

A FRAGMENT

When the elders gathered, they did not begin with the matter that had brought them together. They began by greeting each person present, naming them, naming their parents and the village their parents had come from, asking after the absent kin. The young man who had been waiting for the meeting to begin had thought this was a delay. He understood, halfway through, that this was the meeting beginning. By the time the named matter was raised, the room contained not a collection of individuals each with a position but a configuration of relations in which the matter could be properly placed.

THE WORD TO KNOW

ubuntu

/ùˈɓúntʼù/

Nguni Bantu (specifically isiZulu, isiXhosa, and related languages of southern Africa). The word is composed of the noun-class prefix 'ubu-' (denoting an abstract or general quality) and the root '-ntu' (related to personhood, humanity). Cognates appear across the Bantu language family — 'botho' in Sesotho and Setswana, 'utu' in Swahili — with broadly similar philosophical contents. The fullest articulation in the southern African tradition is in the Nguni proverb 'umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu' — 'a person is a person through other persons' — which functions as both a definition and an ethical injunction.

The quality of being a person, understood as something that emerges through and is sustained by one's relations to other persons; the relational ground from which individual personhood arises and to which it remains constitutively dependent. Names both a metaphysical claim about what human beings are and an ethical claim about what a well-conducted life consists of. Distinguished from European individualism in proposing that the community is logically prior to the individual rather than the reverse.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Mogobe Ramose

1947 – present · Soweto, South Africa

Mogobe B. Ramose is the South African philosopher whose 1999 book 'African Philosophy Through Ubuntu' is the most fully developed contemporary academic treatment of the concept as a piece of formal philosophy rather than as a political slogan or a popular ethics. Ramose was trained in the European philosophical tradition — he holds doctorates from Leuven and from the University of South Africa — and has spent his career arguing that the dominant Western academic philosophy has been systematically deficient in its refusal to engage with African philosophical traditions on equal terms. His work locates ubuntu within a broader Bantu metaphysics of being as motion, in which existence is fundamentally relational and processual rather than substantive and individual.

Ramose's complication is that his work has had a substantial influence within African philosophical departments and a small but growing reception in continental European philosophy, while remaining largely invisible to the analytic philosophical mainstream of the English-speaking world. He has continued writing into his late seventies, including substantial work on the relationship between Bantu thought and the ecological crisis — a connection he sees as following directly from the ubuntu account of relational personhood, extended to include the non-human elements of the relational network. His career is an instance of the broader pattern in which Southern philosophical work has had to argue for the status of being philosophy at all before it can be argued with on its content.

NOW HERE

Constitution Hill

Hillbrow, Johannesburg, South Africa

The site of the former Old Fort Prison Complex in central Johannesburg, where Gandhi, Mandela, and a long succession of South African political prisoners were held during the colonial and apartheid eras; since 2004, the location of the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the highest court on constitutional matters in the country. The court was deliberately built on the site of the prisons to embed the new constitutional order in the place that had embodied its opposite, and the architecture of the court — designed by a collaboration of South African architects — incorporates bricks from the demolished sections of the prison into the walls of the courtroom. The South African Constitution of 1996, which the court interprets, was the first major national constitution to refer explicitly to ubuntu, in the postamble to the interim constitution of 1993, in language that has continued to inform South African jurisprudence on questions of restorative justice and dignity.

ON THIS DAY

1980

Johannesburg, South Africa

On 10 June 1980, the African National Congress published in London the text of a statement smuggled out of Pollsmoor Prison the previous month, in which the imprisoned Nelson Mandela rejected the conditional offer of release made by the apartheid government, on the grounds that he could not separately accept a freedom that was not also extended to his people. The statement, read in his absence by his daughter Zindzi at a rally in Soweto, contained the line that has been quoted in almost every subsequent account: 'Your freedom and mine cannot be separated.' The formulation belongs to the same philosophical tradition the essay describes — the proposition that the individual person's condition cannot be considered in isolation from the conditions of the persons through whom that person has come to be a person at all.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    If you took seriously the proposition that personhood is constituted by relation rather than residing in the individual, what about your own self-understanding would have to change?

    Which of your current relationships would be revealed, on that account, as more constitutive than you currently treat them — and which would be revealed as more peripheral than your sense of yourself has assumed? And what would it cost you to act on the rebalanced picture?

    Make a list this week of the five people (alive or dead) without whom you would not be the person you currently are. Notice whether your daily attention reflects the constitutive weight of those particular relationships, or whether your attention has drifted toward relationships that are easier to maintain but less constitutive.

  • 2

    Where in your own life do you treat the autonomous individual as the basic unit — your own and others' — and where do you treat the relation as the basic unit?

    What determines which framing you reach for in a given situation? And whose interests does each framing tend to serve?

    Take one decision you are currently facing and ask yourself how it would look reframed: not as 'what should I do?' but as 'what does the network of relations of which I am a node need from me here?' Notice whether the reframe changes the answer or only the texture of arriving at it.

  • 3

    Is there a tradition of thought outside the one you were trained in that you have noticed yourself dismissing too quickly — and what would it require of you to engage with it on its own terms rather than as a contrast to your own?

    What has kept you from the engagement — time, the absence of trusted guides, the discomfort of having your starting assumptions challenged? And which of these would be addressable if you decided to address it?

    Identify one tradition of thought you have largely written off. Read, this week, one essay or chapter by a serious practitioner of it, chosen because the practitioner is trying to do philosophy rather than to present the tradition to outsiders. Notice what you learn that the outsider summaries had missed.

MOVIE TIME

Tsotsi

Gavin Hood · 2005

A young gang leader in a Johannesburg township, after a violent carjacking, discovers an infant in the back seat and takes the child home with him. The film follows the slow involuntary disruption of his self-conception as he is drawn back into the relational structures he has spent the previous decade refusing. Adapted from a novel by Athol Fugard set in apartheid-era Soweto and updated to the post-apartheid present, the film works within a moral framework that the ubuntu tradition would recognise — a person is not a person alone, and the route back to personhood runs through the relations the person has tried to sever.
