Inherited Sorrow That Becomes Endurance

There is a Korean word that does not translate cleanly because the experience it names was, until quite recently, a collective condition of being Korean rather than a discrete emotion that a Korean person might or might not feel. The word names a sorrow accumulated across generations — a sorrow whose origins lie in specific historical wounds (colonisation, partition, war, dictatorship, displacement) but whose presence in any individual life is not directly traceable to any single one of those wounds. The wounds are in the parents, in the grandparents, in the great-grandparents, in the village the family came from before there was a village, and the inherited sorrow is the present-tense form they take in the descendants who did not experience them firsthand.

Han is the Korean noun. It is sometimes translated as resentment, sometimes as bitterness, sometimes as melancholy; none of the translations carries what the Korean word carries. Han is not exactly grief, because grief implies a specific recent loss; han is the long settling residue of losses no single person fully remembers. Han is not exactly bitterness, because bitterness implies an active animosity toward a specific agent; han is the diffuse weight of injuries whose perpetrators are largely dead or unreachable. The Korean cultural tradition has spent the past century elaborating the concept in poetry, music, film, and political discourse, and the elaboration has produced a vocabulary for the inheritance of historical pain that European languages have not equivalently developed.

What is distinctive about han is its relationship to endurance. The concept does not propose that the accumulated sorrow should be expressed and discharged through some therapeutic process. It proposes instead that the sorrow becomes, in time, a kind of substance that the bearer learns to live with and that, in the living with, produces a particular quality of strength. The endurance is not the suppression of the sorrow. The endurance is what the sorrow becomes when it has been carried long enough that the carrying has shaped the person who is doing it. There is a moral seriousness in the Korean concept that the popular Western therapeutic vocabulary, with its assumption that all suffering should be processed and released, has tended to find difficult to register.

The musical tradition of pansori — the long solo narrative songs that have been performed in Korea for at least three centuries — provides the most fully developed artistic vocabulary for han. A great pansori singer is judged not on technical perfection but on the depth of han they can express through the voice, and the depth of han is understood to be unavailable to performers who have not lived enough to have accumulated it. There are passages in the major pansori narratives whose performance, by the recognised masters of the form, has been described by audiences as producing in the room a shared sensation of historical weight that the listener carries home and feels as their own inheritance even when their family has no direct historical connection to the events the song relates.

What this suggests, considered carefully, is that some forms of human suffering are not soluble at the level of the individual life and require to be held by something larger than the individual — a community, a tradition, an art form, a vocabulary — in order to be borne at all. The Western therapeutic tradition has tended to assume that all suffering can in principle be processed by the sufferer, given sufficient skill and time, and that what remains unprocessed is a failure of the work. The Korean tradition has tended to assume that some suffering is structurally beyond the individual's capacity and that the appropriate response is the development of cultural forms strong enough to carry what individuals cannot.

What resists in the popular Western reception of han is the temptation to treat it as a form of national depression in need of treatment, or as a romanticised piece of cultural identity that should be celebrated as authenticity. Both readings miss the structure. Han is neither a pathology nor an aesthetic. It is a piece of practical philosophy developed by a people who have had a great deal to bear and who have come to recognise that the bearing requires institutions — songs, words, ceremonies, gestures — that the surrounding culture has to maintain on behalf of the individuals whose lives the surrounding culture has, often involuntarily, asked too much of.

THE TENSION

Han names a real and underexamined dimension of human suffering: the inheritance of historical pain across generations, whose presence in any individual life cannot be fully accounted for by that individual's own experience. The Western therapeutic tradition, with its assumption that all suffering is processable by the sufferer, has substantially failed to engage with this dimension, and the Korean concept is one of the few well-developed vocabularies for it in any of the world's living languages.

The valorisation of han risks essentialising what is in fact a contested concept within Korean culture itself, often invoked by political projects with their own agendas. A number of Korean scholars have argued that the modern emphasis on han as a defining Korean characteristic is itself a relatively recent construction, shaped by colonial-era Japanese ethnographers and by post-war South Korean cultural nationalism, and that the foundational claim that Koreans are particularly burdened by historical sorrow has been used to justify a range of conservative cultural and political positions that the concept does not in itself require.

THE NUMBER

1910

The year of the Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty that formally placed the Korean peninsula under Japanese imperial rule, the most-cited historical event in twentieth-century articulations of han, and the beginning of a thirty-five-year colonial period whose specific cultural and economic injuries remain a substantial part of Korean political memory.

A FRAGMENT

The grandmother had not spoken about the war. The mother had spoken about it once, briefly, and then not again. The daughter, born in a country her family had not been born in, had never heard a complete account of what had happened to the great-aunts and the great-uncles in the months following the partition. What she had inherited, instead, was the texture of how her family carried itself: the carefulness of certain conversations, the particular quality of silence that fell when certain names were mentioned, the long unstated baseline of caution about whether the next decade would be one in which a family could keep what it had. She had inherited a posture toward the world. She had inherited it without knowing she was being given it. The substance of it was han, though her family did not use the word.

THE WORD TO KNOW

han

/hɑːn/ — 한 (恨)

Korean; written with the hangul syllable 한 and corresponding to the Sino-Korean hanja 恨 (the Chinese character for resentment or regret, pronounced 'hèn' in Mandarin). The Korean concept developed substantially beyond the Chinese cognate during the twentieth century, particularly in the context of Japanese colonial rule (1910–1945), the Korean War (1950–1953), and the long subsequent processes of partition, military dictatorship, and economic transformation. The contemporary cultural elaboration of han as a specifically Korean concept has been led by literary critics, musicologists, and theologians (notably in the minjung theology of the 1970s and 1980s) who treated the concept as a key to understanding the modern Korean experience.

A long-accumulated collective sorrow whose origins lie in specific historical wounds but whose presence in any individual life cannot be traced directly to any single event; an inherited residue of generational pain that, in being borne over time, becomes a quality of endurance and a particular kind of strength. Distinct from grief, bitterness, or melancholy in its multi-generational temporal structure and in its relationship to a cultural tradition that has developed specific artistic and ceremonial forms for its expression and containment.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Park Wan-suh

1931 – 2011 · Kaepung, present-day Democratic People's Republic of Korea / Seoul, Republic of Korea

Park Wan-suh was the Korean novelist whose 1970 debut, 'Namok' (The Naked Tree), began a literary career that would span four decades and produce some of the most widely-read Korean fiction of the late twentieth century. Born in 1931 in what is now North Korea, she lived through the Japanese colonial period as a child, survived the Korean War in Seoul as a young woman, and lost her elder brother and her uncle in the war under circumstances she would return to repeatedly in her fiction. Her novels — 'Mother's Stake,' 'Who Ate Up All the Shinga?,' 'A Sketch of the Fading Sun' — combine close domestic observation with the long historical weight of the Korean twentieth century, written in a prose that is plainspoken on the surface and accumulates substantial weight across the duration of the books.

Park's complication is that she began her career as a writer at forty, after twenty years as a housewife and mother during which she had not published, and that the loss in her family that drove much of her fiction was the kind of loss the South Korean political environment of her active writing years did not always permit her to articulate fully. The censorship she worked under was sometimes formal and sometimes the informal pressure of a society that had not collectively reckoned with what it had done to itself during and after the war. She produced the body of work she produced in the spaces that were available to her, and was recognised in her last decades as one of the major Korean writers of her century, but the books she might have written under different conditions are among the costs the surrounding period exacted from the literature she contributed to making.

NOW HERE

Imjingak

Paju, Gyeonggi Province, Republic of Korea

A complex of memorials, viewing platforms, and a railway bridge on the southern bank of the Imjin River, three kilometres south of the Korean Demilitarised Zone, built in 1972 to allow South Koreans who had been separated from family members in the north to commemorate the absent kin. The site includes the Bridge of Freedom, across which approximately 13,000 prisoners of war were repatriated to the south at the close of the Korean War in 1953, and the long fence on which separated family members have for fifty years tied prayer ribbons to the wire — tens of thousands of them, weather-faded, the older ones now barely legible — addressed to specific named individuals on the other side of the line whose continued existence the writers cannot confirm. The site is one of the principal physical locations at which the historical substance the word han names takes the form of an architectural and ritual practice in which the visiting public still actively participates.

ON THIS DAY

2000

Pyongyang, Democratic People's Republic of Korea

On 15 June 2000, the South Korean President Kim Dae-jung and the North Korean leader Kim Jong-il signed the June 15th North-South Joint Declaration at the conclusion of the first-ever inter-Korean summit, in a meeting held in Pyongyang fifty years after the beginning of the Korean War and forty-seven years after the armistice that ended its active hostilities without producing a peace treaty. The declaration committed the two states to pursue reunification by their own efforts, to address the humanitarian issues of separated families, and to promote balanced economic cooperation. The substance of the agreement was substantially undone in the following decades by political shifts on both sides; what survives is the date itself, observed as a public commemoration in South Korea and used in the diaspora as a reference point for the specifically political form that han has taken among Koreans whose family members were separated from them by the line on the map that the war did not erase.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a form of inherited sorrow in your own family or community — a residue of historical events you did not personally experience but that you carry as a baseline texture of your life?

    If there is, do you have a vocabulary for it, or have you absorbed it without ever being given a name for what you have been given? And does the absence of a name make it easier to live with, or harder?

    Spend an hour this week thinking about what you carry from generations before your own. Try, in writing, to name the texture of it as precisely as you can — not the events but the present-tense way they have shaped how you stand in the world. Do not try to resolve anything. The naming itself is the work.

  • 2

    The Korean tradition assumes that some suffering is not soluble at the level of the individual and requires cultural forms — songs, ceremonies, words — to be borne. Have you experienced, in your own life, a sorrow that proved too large for any individual processing and that was made bearable by participation in something larger?

    What was the cultural form that carried what your individual capacities could not? And do you have access to such forms in your current life, or have you been attempting to bear collectively-sized sorrows individually?

    Identify one sorrow in your life that has resisted individual processing. Look for a cultural form — a music, a literature, a ceremony, a community of fellow-bearers — that has historically been used to carry that kind of sorrow. Spend an hour with it this week. Notice whether the participation changes the weight of what you are carrying.

  • 3

    The Western therapeutic tradition tends to assume that all suffering should be processed and released; the Korean tradition assumes that some suffering becomes endurance through the long carrying. Which of the two assumptions has shaped your own approach to your harder experiences, and on what evidence has the choice been made?

    Have you encountered cases in your own life where the therapeutic assumption proved inadequate — where the suffering did not yield to processing and required a different relation? And what did the encounter teach you that the dominant cultural vocabulary had not?

    Pick one persistent sorrow you have been treating as a problem to be solved. Spend a week relating to it instead as something to be carried — not avoided, not minimised, but accepted as a permanent feature of your inner landscape with its own weight and its own slow effects on the kind of person you are becoming. Notice what shifts in your relation to the sorrow when the goal is no longer its disappearance.

TEST YOURSELF

Pansori, the Korean musical tradition most closely associated with the expression of han, is a solo vocal genre in which a single singer performs a long narrative accompanied by which kind of musical accompaniment?

Pansori is traditionally performed by a single singer (sorikkun) accompanied only by a single drummer (gosu) playing the barrel-shaped buk drum. The drummer is not merely a rhythmic accompanist but a full participant in the performance, providing both rhythmic structure and the spoken or sung interjections (chuimsae) that punctuate the narrative and that the audience also contributes to. The minimal instrumentation places the entire weight of the performance on the voice, and the depth of han a great singer can convey through extended unaccompanied passages is a primary criterion by which the tradition judges its masters. The genre was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2003.

Concept match

MOVIE TIME

Peppermint Candy

Lee Chang-dong · 1999

A South Korean film that traces, in reverse chronological order, the life of a man who throws himself in front of a train at the opening — moving backward through his disintegrated marriage, his career as a police interrogator under the dictatorship, his service in the army during the Gwangju Massacre of 1980, and back to the moment of innocent first love that the subsequent decades have systematically dismantled. Lee Chang-dong's film is among the most fully developed cinematic expressions of han in its specifically Korean political-historical sense: the protagonist's deterioration is not the result of his individual choices alone but of the historical weight a particular generation of Korean men have been asked to carry, and the film's reverse structure refuses the consolation of any narrative arc toward redemption.
