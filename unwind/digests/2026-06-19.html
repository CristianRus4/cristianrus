The Restless Heart in the Days Before a Journey

There is a specific quality of inner weather that arrives in the days before a planned departure and that has nothing to do with the practical anxieties of the trip itself. The packing has been done or has been deferred without alarm. The tickets are confirmed. The arrangements at the other end are in place. And yet, several days out, something in the chest has begun to lean forward, the sleep has acquired a small unfamiliar restlessness, and the ordinary business of the days before has become harder to attend to than its content warrants. The mind is already partly on the road. The body has begun to prepare for the displacement before the displacement has begun.

Swedish has a noun for this state. Resfeber: literally 'journey-fever,' the restless mixture of anticipation and low-grade anxiety that precedes a departure. The word names the condition itself rather than any particular trip-related concern, and it makes the same distinction that the Norwegian forelsket makes with regard to romantic love: it isolates a specific phase, here pre-travel rather than pre-relational, as a discrete inner state with its own characteristic texture. The Swedish speaker can describe the state to others, can recognise it in themselves with the precision the lexical isolation allows, and can therefore manage it more deliberately than the English speaker who has no word for the territory.

What distinguishes resfeber from ordinary anxiety is its specifically anticipatory structure. The state is not about anything currently happening. The state is about the body's recognition that something is about to begin, and the recognition is producing physiological effects that precede any actual cause. The autonomic nervous system, which evolved to prepare the organism for imminent challenges, does not always wait for the challenge to arrive before beginning the preparation. The week before a long journey, the system is already partly in journey mode — adrenal slightly elevated, sleep slightly compressed, appetite slightly altered — and the resulting state is a kind of preview of the trip experienced inside the still-familiar walls of the home one is about to leave.

The Swedish noun has a literary register that the English equivalents do not. Travel writers in Swedish have used the word for two centuries to name something they take to be one of the basic pleasures of a traveller's life. The fever before a departure is not, in the Swedish framing, a problem to be alleviated. It is part of the gift of taking the journey at all — the body's involuntary registration that something is about to be different, and the small somatic intensification that the anticipation produces. The Swedish traveller who knows the word can savour the state in the way an English-language traveller, without the word, often struggles to do.

There is, in this, a small piece of cultural philosophy worth attending to. The Anglo-Saxon vocabulary tends to treat the period before an event as a kind of dead time — preparation, logistics, waiting — that should be passed through efficiently in order to arrive at the event itself. The Swedish noun proposes a different relation: that the time before the event is itself one of the experiences the event makes possible, that the anticipation is part of the trip rather than a prelude to it, and that the inner weather of the days leading up belongs to the same continuous experience that the days of the trip will eventually contain. The traveller who has the word travels for longer than the calendar dates of the trip suggest, because the resfeber has begun three or four days early.

What this opens up, more broadly, is the question of how much of the texture of a life is conducted in the anticipations and the residues of named events rather than in the named events themselves. The actual journey, when it arrives, will be a few specific days of moving through specific places. The resfeber will have begun a week earlier and will have prepared the body and the mind in ways the journey itself could not have produced if it had arrived without warning. The residue — the memory work, the photograph-sorting, the slow integration of what was seen — will continue for weeks after the return. The Swedish word names the leading edge of a temporal halo that the dominant Anglo-Saxon framing has tended to compress or to overlook, and the recognition of the halo as part of the experience changes the calculus of which experiences are worth having at all.

THE TENSION

Recognising resfeber as a discrete and meaningful state expands what it means to take a journey, by acknowledging that the experience begins in the days of anticipation and continues in the weeks of residue. The Anglo-Saxon framing that treats only the named days of the trip as the trip itself underestimates how much of a journey's value is constituted by its temporal halo, and the Swedish word provides the lexical tool for making the halo visible.

Romanticising the pre-journey fever risks turning a normal mild stress response into a piece of branded experience that the traveller is now obliged to cultivate and to enjoy. Not every restless night before a trip is a meaningful inner state worth attending to; some of them are just sleep disrupted by the calendar's pressure, and the elevation of every flutter into a named phenomenon can produce a kind of compulsory mindfulness that is its own form of overwork.

4-6 days

Typical onset window of detectable resfeber-like physiological changes — measurable increases in evening cortisol and small decreases in sleep efficiency — before a major planned displacement, according to studies of travel-related stress responses in working adults preparing for international trips of one week or longer.

A FRAGMENT

It was Tuesday evening and the flight was on Friday. The bags had been packed since Sunday. There was nothing left to organise. She lay in bed with the window open and listened to the city outside and could feel the trip already beginning under the surface of the still-familiar room. She had stopped fighting it years ago. The fever was reliable. It would carry her through the practical work of Wednesday and Thursday and would arrive at the airport already half-spent, the body's preparation having done most of its work in advance. The flight itself, when it came, would be the easy part.

THE WORD TO KNOW

resfeber

/ˈreːsˌfeːbər/

Swedish, a compound of 'resa' (journey, travel) and 'feber' (fever), literally 'journey-fever.' The word is in everyday use in Swedish and has cognates in the closely related Norwegian ('reisefeber') and Danish ('rejsefeber'). The construction is attested in Swedish literary sources from the nineteenth century and is documented as a folk-medical category in older Scandinavian writing on the conditions of travelling. The compound is straightforward in its constituents but isolates as a discrete category a state that the larger European languages handle only through periphrasis.

The restless mixture of anticipation and low-grade anxiety that arrives in the days before a planned journey, producing a specific quality of inner weather distinct from any particular practical concern about the trip itself. Names the body's involuntary pre-departure preparation — the slightly altered sleep, the leaning-forward in the chest, the diffuse low-grade intensification of awareness — as a state in its own right, with its own characteristic texture and duration.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Sven Hedin

1865 – 1952 · Stockholm, Sweden

Sven Hedin was the Swedish geographer, topographer, and explorer whose multi-year expeditions across Central Asia between 1893 and 1935 produced the most detailed European maps yet made of large portions of Tibet, Xinjiang, and the Tarim Basin, and substantially fixed in European cartography a region whose interior had previously been blank on Western maps. His expeditions, often privately funded and conducted with small parties under conditions of considerable difficulty, included the first European traverse of the Trans-Himalayan range and the rediscovery of the lost Silk Road city of Loulan in the Lop desert. His books — 'Through Asia,' 'Trans-Himalaya,' 'The Silk Road' — were major popular successes in their time and established Hedin as one of the most internationally famous Swedes of his generation.

Hedin's complication is the political position he occupied in the 1930s and 1940s, when his openly expressed sympathy for Nazi Germany — including meetings with Hitler and the dedication of one of his last books to a senior Nazi cultural functionary — substantially destroyed his international standing and complicated his posthumous Swedish reception. The geographical work itself has not been seriously challenged, and the maps and ethnographic observations he produced remain valuable scholarly resources, but the figure has become difficult to discuss without the political shadow. Sweden has not entirely resolved what to do with Hedin: his archive remains an important resource, his books are still in print, and his presence in the history of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences remains, but the unambiguous public celebration that the geographical work might have warranted has not been possible for the seventy years since his death.

NOW HERE

Stockholm Central Station Platform 16

Norrmalm, Stockholm, Sweden

The northernmost platform of Stockholm's Central Station, from which the night trains depart for the Arctic Circle — most notably the long-distance service to Narvik in northern Norway and to Boden and Luleå in Swedish Lapland, journeys of fifteen to twenty hours that traverse most of the length of the Scandinavian Peninsula. The station itself, opened in 1871 and expanded across the subsequent century, is one of the busiest public spaces in Sweden, and platform 16 in the evening — particularly in the long-light weeks around the summer solstice — is a small concentrated archive of resfeber: travellers loading the heavy luggage of the multi-week summer trips north, the families about to begin the annual visit to relatives in the small inland villages, the solitary hikers headed for the high-fell country with their packs in the early stages of the inner weather the Swedish word names. The platform is what it has been for a century and a half: the moment at which the anticipation of the journey transitions into the journey itself.

ON THIS DAY

1846

Hoboken, New Jersey, USA

On 19 June 1846, the first organised game of baseball under codified rules — the Knickerbocker Rules drafted by Alexander Cartwright the previous year — was played at the Elysian Fields in Hoboken, between the New York Knickerbockers and the New York Nine. The game lost 23–1, by the Knickerbockers, was largely uncommemorated at the time and would become canonical only decades later as the sport's origin myth was being assembled. The journey from a small game in a New Jersey field to a national institution would take fifty years and would substantially involve the long-distance railway travel that the second half of the nineteenth century was making available to American working-class audiences for the first time. The resfeber the early travelling baseball teams would have experienced — assembling in railway stations at dawn for trips of a week or more — was the small predecessor of the larger national circulation of attention the sport would eventually produce.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Do you experience resfeber in the days before a significant trip — the specific quality of inner weather the Swedish word describes — and have you generally treated it as part of the trip or as a problem to be managed away?

    What changes in your sense of the experience if you treat the pre-journey state as one of the things the trip itself is for, rather than as preliminary noise? And which of the pre-trip days do you most want to recover from the dismissive framing?

    Before your next planned displacement of any significance, deliberately attend to the inner weather in the days leading up. Make a brief note each evening about what you notice in the body, the mind, the sleep, the appetite. Notice what shifts in the eventual experience of the trip when the pre-trip days have been included in it.

  • 2

    The essay suggests that the texture of a life is conducted substantially in the anticipations and residues of named events. What proportion of your daily emotional life is currently being shaped by events that have not yet happened, or that have already ended?

    Is the proportion higher or lower than you would have predicted before you tried to measure it? And what are the implications for how you think about presence — the dominant cultural injunction to attend only to what is happening now?

    Spend one day deliberately tracking how much of your inner weather is determined by past or future events rather than by the present moment. Do not try to redirect attention to the present. Simply observe the actual distribution, and notice what the observation teaches you about how your interior is constituted.

  • 3

    Are there other small specific inner weathers in your life that you would benefit from having a more precise vocabulary for — pre-event states, post-event states, transitional moments that English does not lexically isolate?

    Could you assemble, this week, a short personal lexicon — three to five terms drawn from other languages or coined yourself — for the most recurrent of these states? And would the lexicon, once assembled, change anything about how you live the states themselves?

    Pick one transitional or anticipatory state from your own life — the Sunday-evening feeling, the long-flight return-home feeling, the morning before a hard conversation — and find or coin a word for it. Use the word with one person who will understand the exercise. Notice whether the shared name allows you both to discuss the state with a precision that the previous lack of a word had prevented.

TEST YOURSELF

Which of the following is the Norwegian cognate of the Swedish word 'resfeber,' constructed from the same Germanic compound elements?

The Norwegian cognate of the Swedish 'resfeber' is 'reisefeber,' composed of 'reise' (journey) and 'feber' (fever) — the direct equivalent of the Swedish components. Danish 'rejsefeber' completes the pattern across the three principal North Germanic languages of Scandinavia. The other options either combine the wrong elements ('ferdfeber' is not a standard Norwegian compound; 'vandrefeber' would be 'wander-fever,' a related but distinct concept) or name something else entirely ('veisykdom' would mean 'road-sickness' in the sense of motion sickness, not anticipation).

MOVIE TIME

Old Joy

Kelly Reichardt · 2006

Two old friends in their thirties take a weekend camping trip into the Cascade mountains of Oregon, ostensibly to visit a remote hot spring one of them has heard of. The film, barely seventy-six minutes long, devotes most of its running time to the pre-arrival texture of the trip — the drive out, the wrong turns, the small accumulations of unsaid distance between the two friends — and treats the journey itself as the experience the film is about. Reichardt's camera is patient with the resfeber that hangs over the early sequences, and the film becomes a working demonstration that the trip's value was substantially constituted by the days of anticipation and the hours of getting lost rather than by anything that occurred at the eventual destination.
