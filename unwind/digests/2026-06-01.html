The Second Life of What We Leave Behind

There is a fundamental optimism in the act of putting something in a jar and trusting it to time. We tend to think of preservation as stopping the clock—freezing the world in its current state, defending it against decay. But fermentation is entirely different. It is a surrender to biological momentum. It does not stop time; it harnesses it. You are not building a wall against bacteria; you are inviting the right ones in and letting them do the work.

Consider the cabbage. Left alone, it rots. It becomes an unpleasant, structureless puddle. But chopped, salted, and submerged, it undergoes a radical transformation. The salt draws out the water, creating an environment where lactic acid bacteria thrive. They consume the sugars, producing acid that protects the cabbage from the organisms that would otherwise destroy it. Decay is averted not by sterility, but by guided proliferation. The system protects itself by becoming something else.

This is a profound metaphor for how we might approach the fragile structures of our own lives. We spend so much energy trying to keep things exactly as they are—our institutions, our relationships, our habits. We try to seal them off from the air. But rigid things break when the environment changes. The things that endure are the ones that can metabolize the change. They take the ambient chaos and turn it into structure. They sour, they complexify, they gain depth.

To ferment is to have agency through subtraction. You prepare the conditions, you set the boundaries, and then you step back. It is a domestic ritual that asks you to be a curator of microscopic ecosystems. You are trusting the invisible world to be on your side. And usually, it is. The microbial logic of the world leans toward life, toward new forms of order emerging from the breakdown of the old.

We should look at more of our failures this way—not as things that have spoiled, but as substrates waiting for the right conditions to transform. A collapsed project, a broken routine, a period of stagnation; these are the raw materials. If you apply the right pressure and wait, they do not just disappear. They become the nutrient base for whatever happens next. The breakdown is the beginning.

THE TENSION

We control the outcome by carefully managing the environment and excluding what we deem harmful. Safety and longevity come from the sterile, the rigid, and the tightly bounded.

We survive only by cooperating with the breakdown, letting go of original forms to find new ones. Resilience is found in the permeable, the shifting, and the uncontrolled.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Eduard Buchner

1860 – 1917 · Munich, Germany

For centuries, science believed fermentation required living yeast cells—that the mysterious transformation of sugar into alcohol was an act of vital force. Buchner shattered this by grinding yeast with sand, extracting the liquid, and showing it could ferment sugar entirely on its own. He proved that it was not the living organism, but the enzymes it produced, that did the work, fundamentally altering our understanding of biochemistry.

The man who unlocked the chemical secret of life’s invisible processes met a painfully blunt end. Having revolutionised how we see the microscopic world, he volunteered as a major during World War I and was killed by a shrapnel wound in Romania, his profound grasp of chemical vitality offering no protection against the crude mechanics of artillery.

NOW HERE

The Qvevri Cellars

Kakheti, Georgia

Large clay vessels remain buried in the cool earth beneath a house, holding crushed grapes, skins, and stems. They are sealed with beeswax and left to the darkness. There is no intervention, no stirring, only the slow, invisible heat of transformation beneath the floorboards. The ground itself acts as the regulator, cradling the vessels as the violent chemistry of fermentation settles into profound stillness.

THE ANSWERS

Trivia answer

Maple syrup

Maple syrup is made by boiling down the sap of maple trees to evaporate the water and concentrate the sugars; no fermentation is involved. Chocolate, vanilla, and coffee all undergo crucial fermentation steps during processing to develop their flavors.

Game answer

Lactic acid bacteria, which are responsible for fermenting vegetables like cabbage into sauerkraut, are only found naturally in the soil and must be introduced manually

Lactic acid bacteria are naturally present on the surface of all plants growing in the ground, including cabbage. No external starter culture is needed; the bacteria are already there, waiting for the right conditions to activate.
