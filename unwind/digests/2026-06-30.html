Nothing Arises Alone

The early Buddhist tradition, particularly the strand that took shape in the centuries after the Buddha's death in northern India in the fifth century BCE, returned repeatedly to a single doctrine that the surrounding philosophical world found difficult and that the subsequent two and a half thousand years of Buddhist thought have continued to elaborate, defend, and dispute. The doctrine is at once cosmological, ethical, psychological, and ontological. It proposes that nothing in the universe arises by itself, that every event and every entity depends for its existence on a particular configuration of conditions, and that the apparent independence and substantiality of any single thing вАФ including the self вАФ is an artefact of a certain way of looking, not a feature of how things actually are.

PratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda is the Sanskrit name for the doctrine; in Pali, the language of the earliest preserved Buddhist canon, it is paбє≠iccasamuppƒБda. The compound is often translated as 'dependent origination' or 'dependent arising,' though both translations leave considerable work to the reader. The doctrine has, in different schools of Buddhist thought, been formalised as a twelve-link chain of causation, as a more general principle of co-arising without strict temporal ordering, and as an analytic tool for revealing the absence of independent self-nature in any phenomenon whatsoever. The differences among these formulations are substantial and have produced serious philosophical debate across the entire history of Buddhist thought, but the underlying intuition is shared: things do not exist alone, and to understand any single thing requires understanding the conditions in which it arises.

What is distinctive about pratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda, considered against the European philosophical tradition, is its refusal of the substantial individual as the basic unit of analysis. The dominant Western metaphysical tradition since at least Aristotle has tended to treat individual substances as primary and relations between them as secondary; the Buddhist tradition has tended to treat the relations as primary and the individuals as derivative вАФ not unreal, exactly, but real only as nodes in the network of conditions that produced them. The self, on this account, is not a soul or a substance but a pattern, continuously re-arising on the basis of the conditions that sustain it, and dissolving when the conditions change. The implications for ethics, for psychology, and for political theory are substantial and have been worked out in different ways across the Buddhist intellectual traditions of South, Southeast, Central, and East Asia.

The contemporary scientific understanding of complex systems вАФ ecology, neuroscience, ecology, evolutionary biology, network theory вАФ has, in different vocabulary, been working out something structurally similar to the Buddhist insight across the past century. The organism does not exist independently of its ecological niche; the ecological niche does not exist independently of the organisms that constitute it; the brain does not exist independently of the body it is embedded in or the social environment that organises its inputs; the gene does not exist independently of the cellular machinery that reads it. Each of these is a small contemporary scientific version of the older Buddhist insight, conducted in the laboratory rather than in the meditation hall, and arriving at conclusions that the philosophical tradition of substances and properties has substantially struggled to accommodate.

What pratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda asks of a person who takes it seriously, in any of its formulations, is a particular kind of attention to the conditions that have produced the present moment. The person, the room, the conversation, the feeling, the obligation, the opportunity вАФ each is not a fact that simply is, but a node in a network of conditions whose visibility depends on the depth and breadth of the attention brought to it. The practice of attending to the conditions, rather than to the apparent solidity of the resulting entities, is one of the central exercises of Buddhist meditation across its many schools, and is offered not as a metaphysical exercise for its own sake but as a practical training in seeing the world more accurately than the default mode of perception permits.

What remains, considered at the close of a month in which a substantial portion of the world's languages have been asked to give up small fragments of their unique vocabulary, is the recognition that the words themselves were never independent things. Each word in this month's series arose in particular conditions вАФ historical, cultural, linguistic, individual вАФ and exists in its full sense only as a node in the network of those conditions. The borrowed word lifted out of its surroundings is not quite the word in its native life; the curated collection of borrowed words assembled here is not the lived experience of any of the cultures the words came from. What such a collection can do is point at the territory the words named, in the cultures where they did their original work, and invite the reader who has been borrowing them to attend more carefully to the conditions of their own life that the borrowed words have made briefly visible. The work of pratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda is to keep that attention going.

THE TENSION

PratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda offers a substantially more accurate metaphysics than the European tradition of individual substances and properties, and the convergence between the Buddhist insight and contemporary complex-systems science suggests that the doctrine is not a piece of religious mysticism but a piece of careful descriptive philosophy that the European tradition has spent two and a half thousand years catching up with. To take the doctrine seriously is to recover a philosophical resource that has been substantially neglected in the Western philosophical mainstream.

The contemporary Western reception of pratƒЂtyasamutpƒБda, particularly in its popularised mindfulness and 'interconnectedness' forms, has substantially flattened the doctrine's actual philosophical demandingness into a vague feeling of relational openness that the original Buddhist analytic tradition would have found insufficient. The doctrine's full working through requires sustained engagement with technical philosophical questions вАФ about causation, about identity over time, about the relationship between conventional and ultimate truth вАФ that the popular use rarely undertakes, and the appropriation of the slogan without the analytic substance has done both the doctrine and its appropriators less service than it appears to have done.

A FRAGMENT

He had been keeping the notebook for thirty-six years. The early entries were in handwriting he no longer entirely recognised as his own; the later ones, on the laptop, in a voice he could still hear in his head when he read them back. The continuity between the boy who had begun the notebook and the man who was now closing it had been, on careful examination, less a continuity of self than a continuity of attention: the same patient looking, conducted across decades by a series of slightly different people each of whom had inherited the notebook from the previous one and added a few entries of their own. The notebook had been the network. The selves had been the nodes. He set it down on the desk and turned out the light.

NOW HERE

Nalanda Mahavihara Ruins

Nalanda District, Bihar, India

The ruins of what was, between the fifth and the twelfth centuries CE, one of the largest residential universities in the pre-modern world вАФ a Buddhist monastic complex of perhaps ten thousand monks and a thousand teachers at its peak, drawing students from across South, Southeast, and East Asia and serving as one of the principal centres of Buddhist philosophical scholarship during the long period when the Madhyamaka, YogƒБcƒБra, and other MahƒБyƒБna schools were being developed and elaborated. The site was sacked in approximately 1193 by the Ghurid general Bakhtiyar Khilji, and the surviving library вАФ said to have contained hundreds of thousands of manuscripts вАФ burned for months. The ruins, excavated since the late nineteenth century and now administered as an archaeological park, preserve the foundations of the monasteries, lecture halls, and temple complexes; the modern Nalanda University, founded in 2014 on a nearby site, is an attempt by the Indian government and a consortium of Asian states to revive, in a contemporary form, the tradition of cross-cultural Asian scholarship that the original Nalanda represented across seven centuries.

ON THIS DAY

1908

Tunguska region, Krasnoyarsk Krai, Russian Empire (present-day Siberia, Russian Federation)

On 30 June 1908, at approximately 7:17 in the morning local time, an atmospheric explosion of energy equivalent to approximately 10 megatons of TNT occurred over the Stony Tunguska River in central Siberia, flattening an estimated 80 million trees across an area of more than 2,000 square kilometres but causing no documented human casualties because of the extreme remoteness of the location. The Tunguska event is the largest impact event in recorded human history, almost certainly caused by the atmospheric airburst of a small asteroid or comet fragment, and the absence of an impact crater on the ground has continued to fuel investigation across the following century. The event illustrates, in unusually pure form, the doctrine the essay describes: a small piece of solar-system debris, present in the inner solar system through the conditioning history of stellar nucleosynthesis and planetary formation, intersected the orbit of the Earth at the precise conjunction of conditions that produced the airburst, which itself produced the forest destruction, which itself produced the long subsequent history of investigation. Nothing in the chain was independent of any other link, and the whole event is unintelligible without the full network of conditions that produced it.
