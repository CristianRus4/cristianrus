The River's Argument

A river encounters a boulder. It does not deliberate. It does not strain against the boulder. It does not summon the will to overcome the obstacle. It simply finds, by the geometry of water in motion, the path around the boulder, and within seconds the boulder is behind the river and the river is continuing to do what rivers do. The boulder, given a great deal of time, will eventually erode. The river will not have addressed itself to the eroding. The river will only have continued to flow, and the continued flowing will have done, over centuries, what no single act of pushing could have accomplished in any reasonable time.

Classical Chinese Daoism has a phrase for the kind of action the river is performing. Wú wéi: literally 'non-action,' though the translation is misleading. The phrase does not advocate doing nothing. It advocates a specific kind of doing — action that does not force itself against the grain of the situation, action that finds the path the situation already affords rather than constructing the path through effort. The Daoist classics, the Dao De Jing and the Zhuangzi, return repeatedly to images of water, of carpenters who let the wood tell them where to cut, of cooks who let the knife find the joints between the bones, of rulers who govern by not actively governing. The principle being illustrated is the same across the various images: the most effective action is the action that does not strain against what would have happened anyway.

What this is not, despite the popularised Western reception of Daoism, is passivity. The river continues to move. The carpenter continues to carve. The ruler continues to govern. The principle is not the absence of action but the absence of forcing. The Western philosophical traditions that have most fully developed the concept of agency have tended to associate effective action with the deliberate imposition of the agent's will on a resistant world; the Daoist tradition proposes that this association is one of the most consequential errors a culture can build itself around, and that the imposed action almost always produces side-effects sufficient to negate the apparent gain.

The contemporary literature on second-order effects has been, without generally acknowledging the source, working out a partial restatement of the wú wéi principle in the empirical vocabulary of complex systems theory. The intervention that addresses a visible problem directly often produces, through the system's response, exactly the conditions the intervention was meant to prevent. The river of the situation, having been blocked, finds a new path that the intervener did not anticipate, and the new path is often worse than the original problem. The Daoist insight is that the appropriate response to a complex situation is rarely the direct intervention; it is more often the patient attention to which path the situation is already taking and the small adjustment that allows the underlying tendency to express itself in its less destructive form.

What this requires, in practice, is a kind of patience that contemporary Western culture is structurally bad at producing. The carpenter who lets the wood tell him where to cut is operating on a temporal horizon that the carpenter who hammers the wood into the desired shape cannot afford. The political reformer who waits for the conditions of social change to ripen is, in the meantime, watched daily by an electoral system that demands visible action and that punishes the absence of obvious deliverables. The wú wéi principle is, on this account, not merely a philosophical alternative to Western activism but a practical impossibility in many of the institutional environments contemporary Western societies have built. The Daoist insight is correct; the conditions for acting on it have been substantially eliminated.

What remains is the question of how to recover, in one's own life and in the institutions one participates in, even a small portion of the relation to action that the principle describes. The full Daoist programme requires cultural conditions that the present moment does not supply. The partial programme — the small daily refusals to force what would have happened of its own accord, the cultivation of attention to where the situation is already going, the willingness to let some interventions be small enough that the surrounding system can absorb them without producing a counter-response — is available to anyone who can hold the discipline. The river does not advise this practice. The river simply performs it, and the patient observer who watches the river for long enough can recover something of the philosophy that watching produced in the original Daoist sages who walked the same rivers two and a half thousand years ago.

THE TENSION

The wú wéi principle correctly identifies a class of situations — complex systems with their own internal dynamics — in which direct intervention is structurally counterproductive, and Western activist culture has spent the past two centuries learning expensively, case by case, what the Daoist classics articulated at the beginning. The recovery of the principle is one of the more urgent intellectual tasks of the present moment, not as an exotic philosophy but as a more accurate description of how complex situations actually respond to attempted control.

The wú wéi principle, lifted from its original cultural setting and deployed as a piece of general wisdom, can become a sophisticated alibi for inaction in situations that genuinely require deliberate human intervention. Not all complex systems respond well to non-forcing; some require active reshaping, and the celebration of patient non-action can become a way of avoiding the harder political and personal work that the situation actually demands. The classical Daoist tradition itself was not naive about this and contained substantial debates about when wú wéi was appropriate and when it was not.

THE NUMBER

5,000

Approximate number of characters in the Dao De Jing, the foundational Daoist text traditionally attributed to Lao Tzu and dated by most contemporary scholars to the fourth century BCE — a text whose extreme compression of language is itself a kind of wú wéi: each character placed only where the meaning could not have arrived without it.

A FRAGMENT

The mediator had been doing the work for forty years and had developed a particular reputation for the cases that had defeated other practitioners. Her secret, when she had occasion to describe it, was that she did almost nothing. She sat in the room. She asked one or two questions. She allowed the silence between answers to extend longer than the participants were comfortable with. She did not propose solutions. She did not summarise positions. She simply attended, and the parties to the disputes, after a sufficient interval, generally did the work themselves. She had been criticised for many years for not earning her fee. The cases she had resolved had a way of staying resolved.

THE WORD TO KNOW

wú wéi

/wǔ weǐ/ — 無為

Classical Chinese, from the Daoist philosophical tradition. The compound consists of two characters: 無 (wú, 'without' or 'non-') and 為 (wéi, 'doing' or 'making'), literally meaning 'non-doing' or 'non-action.' The phrase is central to the Dao De Jing and the Zhuangzi, the two foundational Daoist texts of the late Warring States period (fifth to third centuries BCE). The phrase has been the subject of substantial commentarial and translation debate across two and a half thousand years: 'non-action' is the literal translation but is misleading, since the phrase advocates a specific kind of action rather than the absence of it. Alternative translations include 'effortless action,' 'action through non-action,' and 'non-coercive action.' The Mandarin pronunciation 'wú wéi' reflects the modern Standard Chinese reading; the Classical Chinese pronunciation would have been substantially different.

Action that does not force itself against the grain of the situation — the deliberate finding of the path the situation already affords rather than the construction of a path through imposed effort. The Daoist principle that the most effective action is the action that does not strain against what would have happened of its own accord, and that direct intervention in a complex situation typically produces side-effects sufficient to negate the apparent gain. Distinct from passivity in that it specifies a way of doing rather than the absence of doing.

A QUOTE

上善若水。水善利萬物而不爭，處眾人之所惡，故幾於道。

Lao Tzu · Dao De Jing, Chapter 8, c. 4th century BCE

The highest good is like water. Water benefits the ten thousand things and does not contend; it dwells in places that all disdain, and so it is close to the Way.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

A.C. Graham

1919 – 1991 · Penarth, Wales / Oakland, California, USA

Angus Charles Graham was the British sinologist whose translations and philosophical analyses of the classical Chinese tradition — including the Mohist canon, the Zhuangzi, the later Mohist 'Disputers of the Dao,' and the Lieh-tzu — substantially transformed English-language scholarship on ancient Chinese philosophy in the second half of the twentieth century. His 1981 translation of the inner chapters of the Zhuangzi remains the standard English version, distinguished by its philological care and its philosophical sensitivity to the ways in which classical Chinese conceptual vocabulary does not map neatly onto Western philosophical categories. His 1989 'Disputers of the Tao' is the most comprehensive single-volume English-language account of the philosophical debates of the Warring States period.

Graham's complication is the gap between the rigour of his scholarly work and the relatively small audience it has reached outside specialist circles. The translations are demanding; the philosophical arguments require sustained patience; and the implicit critique they advance of the assumption that Chinese philosophy can be made comprehensible to Western readers by being made to resemble Western philosophy has limited the work's uptake in introductory and survey contexts. The picture of classical Chinese thought that emerges from Graham's lifetime of work is substantially more sophisticated and substantially more alien than the popularised account that has continued to dominate, and the gap between the available scholarly understanding and the prevailing public reception of Chinese philosophy is one of the small ongoing scandals of contemporary intercultural intellectual life.

NOW HERE

Mount Wudang

Hubei Province, central China

A mountain range in northwestern Hubei, rising to approximately 1,600 metres at its highest peak (Tianzhu Feng), with a continuous tradition of Daoist religious practice and monastic settlement going back at least to the third century CE and substantially intensified during the Ming dynasty in the early fifteenth century, when the Yongle Emperor sponsored a major construction programme that produced more than thirty monasteries and temple complexes across the range. The mountain is one of the principal centres of religious Daoism in China and the legendary birthplace of the internal martial arts tradition that includes tai chi chuan and bagua zhang. The monasteries are still active, though substantially smaller than at their Ming peak, and the morning practice of slow movement on the temple platforms — the same patient flowing movement that classical Daoism took as one of its principal physical metaphors for wú wéi — has been continuously performed on these mountains for somewhere between fifteen hundred and two thousand years.

ON THIS DAY

1284

Hamelin, Lower Saxony, Holy Roman Empire (present-day Germany)

On 26 June 1284, according to a series of inscriptions and chronicles whose details vary but whose core date is consistent, 130 children of the town of Hamelin disappeared in circumstances that the surviving sources do not satisfactorily explain. The legend of the Pied Piper, which would later attach itself to the event, was a subsequent narrative attempt to give the disappearance a comprehensible cause; the most credible contemporary historical research suggests that the children may have been part of an organised emigration to the colonised lands of Eastern Europe, possibly under the leadership of a recruiter or 'lokator' who later became, in the folklore, the figure with the pipe. The actual cause remains uncertain; what is certain is that an event sufficiently disruptive to be commemorated by an inscription on the wall of the town's church for the following seven centuries did, in fact, occur. The story has continued to circulate through European culture in part because it presents in compressed form a question that wú wéi addresses obliquely: what kinds of interventions in a community's life can be undertaken in apparent good faith and produce outcomes the community will spend centuries trying to make sense of.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Where in your own life are you currently forcing an outcome through deliberate intervention when the situation would, on careful attention, find its own path with less effort and possibly better result?

    Is the forcing motivated by impatience, by external pressure to produce visible action, or by a genuine assessment that the situation will not move without your intervention? And which of the three is most often the actual driver, on honest examination?

    Identify one situation in your life in which you have been actively pushing. Stop pushing for one week. Watch what the situation does without your intervention. Notice whether the situation worsens, stays the same, or finds a path you had not been making space for, and let the observation inform whether you resume the intervention.

  • 2

    The essay distinguishes wú wéi from passivity. In your own practice, can you reliably tell the difference between the patient non-forcing the principle describes and the simple avoidance the surrounding culture would also call inaction?

    What signs in your own conduct, or in the situation's response, would allow you to distinguish the two? And have you ever mistaken one for the other, in either direction?

    Pick one instance from your past in which you took no action and look back honestly at whether the non-action was wú wéi in the philosophical sense or avoidance in the everyday sense. Use the distinction you arrive at to calibrate your reading of one current non-action in your life, and make a deliberate decision based on the calibration.

  • 3

    The essay suggests that the contemporary institutional environment has been substantially configured against the possibility of wú wéi. Where in your professional life have you experienced this — situations in which the appropriate response was patient non-intervention, but the surrounding system demanded visible action?

    How did you handle the conflict, and what did the handling cost? And are there ways to negotiate with the surrounding system to create space for the patient response, or is the conflict structural in ways no individual negotiation can resolve?

    If you currently have authority over even a small part of an institutional process, choose one decision this week to defer in a deliberate wú wéi mode — to let the situation declare itself for one more cycle before you intervene — and notice both what the situation does in the additional time and what the surrounding institution does in response to your visible non-action.

TEST YOURSELF

The Dao De Jing, the foundational text of Daoism, is traditionally attributed to which figure, who according to legend served as an archivist at the royal court of the Zhou dynasty before withdrawing from public life?

The Dao De Jing is traditionally attributed to Lao Tzu (also written Laozi or Lao Tze), a figure said to have served as an archivist at the royal court of the Zhou dynasty before withdrawing westward in his old age. Legend has it that the border guard at the pass through which Lao Tzu intended to leave detained him until he had written down the substance of his teaching, producing the text in five thousand characters. The historicity of Lao Tzu is contested, and contemporary scholarship generally treats the text as a composite work assembled over several generations in the fourth and third centuries BCE rather than the work of a single author. Zhuang Zhou, also a major Daoist figure, is associated with the other foundational Daoist text, the Zhuangzi.

MOVIE TIME

Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter... and Spring

Kim Ki-duk · 2003

A South Korean film set almost entirely on a small floating Buddhist hermitage in the middle of a remote lake, following the relationship between an old monk and his young apprentice across the five sections of the title. The film operates on a temporal register few contemporary works attempt: long held shots, minimal dialogue, the seasons as the principal structural device. Kim Ki-duk's filmmaking is not Daoist in any specific cultural sense, but the film's relation to action and consequence — the way it allows the situations to develop without forcing them toward resolution — performs a working version of the principle the essay describes, and the relationship at its centre is one of the most patient masters' studies in modern East Asian cinema.
