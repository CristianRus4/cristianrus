The Look Two People Exchange That Neither Will Act On

Two people, across a room, register each other. Something passes between them that they both notice. They both notice that the other has noticed. They both notice that the other has noticed that they have noticed. Then they look away. Nothing further happens. They do not exchange names. They do not approach each other. They do not, in any externally observable way, do anything at all. And yet what passed in the look was, by both of their later accounts, one of the more vivid intersubjective experiences of the week — a moment whose specificity neither of them will be able to reproduce in any of the conversations they will have during the rest of the day.

There is a word for this in a language spoken by perhaps fifteen people in the world. Mamihlapinatapai is from Yagán, the language of the Yaghan people of Tierra del Fuego, at the southern tip of South America. The Guinness Book of Records once cited it as the most succinct word in the world, though the citation has been challenged on linguistic grounds. The word is sometimes loosely translated as 'a look between two people, each of whom wishes the other would begin something they both desire but neither wants to initiate.' The translation is approximate. The word's precise sense is contested even among Yagán speakers, in part because the speaker population has fallen below the number at which detailed lexicographic argument can be sustained.

What matters about the word, regardless of how exactly it is glossed, is that it names a specific intersubjective phenomenon that other languages — English certainly, and most European languages — have not lexically isolated. The phenomenon is one of the basic experiences of being a social mammal: mutual recognition that both parties register and that neither acts on. It happens in romantic contexts, the context in which the word is usually cited, but it happens in many others. Two strangers at a funeral catch each other's eye over an absurdity neither of them will mention. Two colleagues at a meeting register simultaneously that the speaker is bluffing. Two children in the back of separate cars at a stoplight see each other and decide, wordlessly, to be allies for the duration of the red light. The structure is the same. The look is mutual, the recognition is mutual, and the recognition of the recognition is mutual, and the action that follows is none.

Why this particular structure should be so common, and so vivid, is a question that game theory and the philosophy of mind have approached from different sides. The game theorists point out that mutual recognition of a possible coordination, without the means to communicate explicitly, is a deeply old problem for social organisms — the same problem the famous Schelling focal point was designed to model. The philosophers of mind point out that the recursive structure (I know that you know that I know) is among the most cognitively demanding feats human consciousness performs, and that the experience of having performed it is something the perceiver of a successful instance can feel directly.

What is striking about the Yagán word, considered against this background, is that it names not the successful coordination but the failed one — or rather, the one in which the failure is itself the meaning. The look that does not produce an action is not a precursor to an action that did not happen. It is its own complete phenomenon. The participants have arrived together at a state of acknowledged shared awareness that neither will breach into the world of acts. The look is the act. The not-acting is part of the structure.

There is a kind of consolation in the existence of the word that is hard to specify. Languages with millions of speakers have not bothered to name this very common experience, perhaps because the experience is composed of nothing that produces a record. A language spoken by very few has named it precisely. The word survives the speaker population. It will be in the dictionaries long after the last fluent speaker has died. What this means for the relationship between language and experience is uncertain, but the fact of it — that a phenomenon as universal as the shared look has been most exactly named by one of the smallest language communities in the world — is one of those facts about human language that resists explanation while declining to disappear.

THE TENSION

The Yagán word identifies a real and universal phenomenon and the larger languages of the world should incorporate it, or some equivalent, because the absence of a word has kept the experience invisible as a category despite its near-universality. To name something is to make it discussable, and the look between two people who will not act has been part of the social experience of every culture in human history.

The temptation to treat the word as a universal key risks both flattening the specific Yagán cultural context in which it developed and overgeneralising from a single contested gloss. The phenomenon may be universal; the word, in its full sense, may not be. To import it into English as a self-help category is to do to it what English does to many borrowed words, which is to keep the syllables and discard most of what made them meaningful in the original language.

A FRAGMENT

They had been in the same train carriage for forty minutes and had looked at each other twice. The second time had been longer than the first. He was getting off at the next stop. She was going on to the end of the line. The train slowed and he stood, picked up his bag, and walked past her seat without looking down. She did not look up. The doors hissed open and closed. The train moved off. Both of them spent the rest of the afternoon composing, in their separate heads, the conversation they had not had, and both of them, in different ways, were grateful for it.

THE WORD TO KNOW

mamihlapinatapai

/ˌmamiɬapinaˈtapai/

Yagán (also Yámana), a language isolate spoken by the Yaghan people of the Tierra del Fuego archipelago at the southern tip of South America. The language was extensively documented in the late nineteenth century by the missionary-linguist Thomas Bridges, whose Yagán-English dictionary remains the principal source for the vocabulary. The word is morphologically complex, combining a reciprocal prefix with verbal roots related to looking and to indecision; the precise compositional analysis varies among the small number of linguists who have worked on the language. The Guinness Book of Records' designation of the word as the world's most 'succinct' was a popularisation rather than a technical claim.

The shared, knowing look between two people that registers a mutual recognition — often of a possibility neither will pursue, or of an awareness neither will speak. Sometimes translated as the look between two people each of whom wishes the other would initiate something that both desire but neither wants to begin, though the word's precise sense is broader and contested; what is common to all uses is the structure of recursive mutual awareness that produces a complete intersubjective event without producing any external action.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Cristina Calderón

1928 – 2022 · Robalo, Navarino Island, Chile

Cristina Calderón was, until her death at the age of 93 in February 2022, the last living native speaker of the Yagán language. Born on Navarino Island in the Chilean part of Tierra del Fuego in 1928, she lived nearly her entire life on the southern coast of the island, in a Yaghan community whose numbers had already collapsed by the early twentieth century to a few dozen households. She raised her children and grandchildren in Spanish — the language of school, of work, of survival in Chilean society — but kept Yagán as the language of her own daily thought and was, from the 1980s onward, the principal teacher and source for the linguists, ethnographers, and cultural workers attempting to document and partially revive the language before her death.

Calderón's complication is one she shared with most of the world's last speakers of small languages: she became, in old age, simultaneously a living archive, a national symbol (the Chilean government declared her a Living Human Treasure), and a single person carrying the weight of being asked to represent an entire culture whose conditions of life had been destroyed long before she could have done anything about it. She produced, with her granddaughter Cristina Zárraga, a book of Yaghan stories ('Hai kur mamashu chis' — 'I Want to Tell You a Story') in 2005, and she taught Yagán to a small number of second-language learners, including her own descendants. Whether the language she spoke will become extinct or will find a sufficient community of new speakers to survive in some form is a question her death has placed at the centre of the next generation's work.

NOW HERE

Bahía Wulaia

Isla Navarino, Magallanes Region, Chile

A wide bay on the western coast of Isla Navarino, at the extreme southern end of the Tierra del Fuego archipelago, sheltered by the surrounding hills and accessed by a small dirt track from Puerto Williams, the most southerly civilian settlement on Earth. The bay was a major Yaghan settlement for centuries, with shell-mound deposits along the shore that archaeologists have dated to several thousand years of continuous habitation. It was here that the HMS Beagle attempted to leave behind, in 1833, the three young Yaghan it had taken to England and returned 'civilised'; the experiment ended within weeks, the missionaries who had been left with them dispersing or dying and the Yaghan returning to their own kin. The bay is silent now, with the wind off the Beagle Channel and a small lichen-covered chapel on the shore where the missionary station once stood. The wider history the place holds is one of those silences whose specificity language is barely adequate to.

ON THIS DAY

1834

Cape Horn, Tierra del Fuego, Chile

On 8 June 1834, the HMS Beagle, with the young Charles Darwin aboard as a naturalist, was sailing in the waters off Tierra del Fuego on the second voyage that would, over the following four years, take her around the world. Darwin's encounter with the Yaghan people, which he recorded in his journal with a mixture of fascination, scientific curiosity, and the casual prejudice of his class and era, would shape his thinking about the unity of the human species and about the conditions under which it had taken on its various forms. His descriptions of the Yaghan are now read with as much attention to what they reveal about Darwin and about the Victorian gaze as to what they reveal about the people he was attempting to describe — a reading that the Yaghan, whose population at that point was already in the early phase of the catastrophic decline that would follow European contact, were not in a position to influence.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Has there been a mamihlapinatapai in your life — a look exchanged with another person, in any context, that you have remembered with disproportionate vividness even though nothing further happened?

    What was it about that particular look that has kept its place in memory? And does the fact that nothing followed it preserve the experience, or has the not-acting become, over time, a small regret?

    Without searching, simply notice this week the looks you exchange with strangers — the brief moments of mutual registration on streets, in shops, on transport. Pay attention to which of them carry the recursive quality the word names, and which are merely glances. Notice what determines the difference.

  • 2

    The word names a non-action that is its own complete event. Is there a domain of your life in which you regularly mistake non-action for failure, when in fact the non-action is the right answer?

    How do you distinguish, in your own practice, between the non-action that is restraint and the non-action that is cowardice? And whose judgement do you trust most when you cannot make the distinction yourself?

    Identify one situation in your life this week in which you have been pressuring yourself to act and ask honestly whether the not-acting might be the complete and appropriate response. Consider not acting deliberately, and notice what happens both inside you and in the situation.

  • 3

    What does it mean that a language spoken by very few people has most precisely named an experience common to almost everyone — and that the larger languages have left it unnamed?

    Is the absence of a word in a large language evidence that the experience is genuinely common but unmarked, or evidence that the apparent commonality across cultures is itself an artefact of how we are reading the experience back through the borrowed word?

    Pick a small experience from your life that English does not name well. Search for a word in another language — any language — that comes closer to naming it. If you find one, sit with it for a week. Notice whether the new word changes how often you notice the experience.

TEST YOURSELF

The Yagán language is a language isolate, meaning it has no demonstrated genetic relationship to any other known language. In which region of the world is it indigenous?

Yagán (also called Yámana) is indigenous to the Tierra del Fuego archipelago, the cluster of islands at the southern tip of South America, divided today between Argentina and Chile. The Yaghan people lived for several thousand years across the southern coast and the Beagle Channel, with material culture adapted to one of the coldest climates inhabited by any pre-industrial human group. The language has no demonstrated relationship to any other South American language family — including Mapudungun, the Selk'nam language, or the Andean Quechuan and Aymaran families — and is classified as a language isolate.

Wong's camera is patient with the unsaid in a way few other films are. The entire emotional architecture of the film is built around the structure the Yagán word names: the look that registers a mutual recognition, the recognition that neither party will breach into action, and the small joint completeness of the not-acting that follows.

mamihlapinatapai — The mutual look between two people that registers shared awareness without producing any external action — the look is the act

Schelling point — A solution that people tend to choose by default in the absence of communication, because each expects the other to expect it

common knowledge — In game theory, a fact that all parties know, that all parties know that all parties know, and so on without limit — the recursive structure that makes coordination possible

joint attention — The cognitive achievement, emerging in human infancy around nine months, of sharing focus on a third object with another person while both register that the focus is being shared
