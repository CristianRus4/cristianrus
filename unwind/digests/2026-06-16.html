The First Weeks of Love as a Chemical Event

The state arrives, when it arrives, with a specific physical signature that anyone who has been in it can recognise and that no one who has not been in it can quite credit by description. The chest is differently arranged. Sleep becomes optional. Food tastes like food again. Music in the supermarket is unbearably moving. The conversation with the person whose existence has reorganised the inside of the brain extends for hours in a way that violates the laws of how time normally passes. The person in this state is functionally not the same person they were three weeks ago. They are operating on different chemistry, and the chemistry is so distinctive that the neuroscience of recent decades has begun to map it with considerable precision.

Norwegian has a noun for this specific period and this specific state. Forelsket: the early condition of being newly in love, before the relationship has acquired its own gravity and its own ordinary problems. The word is in everyday Norwegian use and is one of the small group of emotional terms that the broader European languages have not lexically isolated. English handles the territory with the awkward phrase 'falling in love,' which carries the right activity but the wrong tense — the falling is continuous, but English treats it as either a verb (the act of falling) or a state (having fallen), and has no word for the specific phase the Norwegian noun names: the suspended moment between the falling and the having fallen, in which the new chemistry is at its peak and the relationship has not yet settled into the form it will take.

The neuroscience of the state has been mapped most fully by Helen Fisher and her collaborators at Rutgers, who have spent two decades putting newly-in-love subjects into fMRI scanners and watching their brain activity. What they found, repeatedly, is a pattern of activation that is essentially indistinguishable from a moderate cocaine high: the ventral tegmental area lights up, dopamine flows through the same reward pathways, and the brain regions associated with social judgement and risk assessment quiet down. The forelsket brain is, by the measurable criteria of neural activity, a brain on a drug. The drug is endogenous. The supply is approximately three to eighteen months. The eventual decline of the state, which feels to the experiencer like a betrayal of what was real, is a chemical inevitability written into the system.

What is interesting about the existence of a word for this specific phase, and not for its successor, is what it implies about the temporal granularity at which a language has chosen to operate. Norwegian has chosen to distinguish the early condition lexically from the longer condition that follows. English has chosen not to. The cultural consequences are not trivial. A Norwegian who is forelsket can describe their state to others, can compare notes with friends who have been forelsket, can know that the condition has a name and a typical duration and is not a permanent identity. An English speaker in the same condition can only say that they are 'falling in love' or 'in love,' both of which conflate the temporary chemistry with the longer thing it sometimes becomes.

The conflation has practical costs. The decision to make a major life commitment — to marry, to move country, to have a child — while in the forelsket state is documented to produce significantly worse outcomes than the same decision made after the state has stabilised, and the absence of a clear lexical distinction between the two states makes the warning harder to articulate. The popular wisdom that one should not propose marriage in the first six months is, in part, a folk attempt to make the distinction the vocabulary fails to make. The Norwegian, with the word available, can at least notice that the decision being made is being made by a person operating on temporary neurochemistry. The English speaker, with no word for the difference, has to work harder to keep the distinction in view.

What remains, regardless of which language one happens to think in, is the strangeness of the state itself. To be forelsket is to be visited by a chemistry that one did not choose and that will eventually subside without consultation. The state is universally experienced as the most real thing in the person's life while it is occurring and is widely reported as having been a kind of beautiful illness in retrospect. The Norwegian word does not solve the strangeness. It only names it. But the naming is what allows the strangeness to be examined rather than merely undergone, and the difference between the two relations is much of what culture has historically been for.

THE TENSION

Recognising forelsket as a temporary chemical state — distinct from whatever longer relationship may follow it — is one of the more useful pieces of practical knowledge a culture can equip its members with, and the absence of a clear English word for this phase has measurable costs in the form of decisions made under the influence of a neurochemistry the decision-maker does not know is operating. The Norwegian lexical distinction is not a curiosity; it is a piece of public health infrastructure encoded in vocabulary.

The chemical reduction of forelsket to a temporary brain state risks deflating something the experiencers themselves often regard as the most meaningful event of their lives. The fact that the state has a neural correlate does not mean it is reducible to that correlate, any more than the fact that grief has a neural correlate means grief is nothing more than a pattern of neural activation. The Norwegian word gains its dignity from the fact that it names a state Norwegians take seriously, and the popularised neuroscience often comes packaged with a deflationary attitude that the experience itself does not invite.

THE NUMBER

12-18 months

Approximate duration of the forelsket state in the standard findings of Helen Fisher's two-decade research programme on the neuroscience of romantic love — the window during which the characteristic dopamine-driven brain activation pattern is reliably observable, and after which the relationship, if it continues, transitions to a different neurochemical regime associated with longer-term pair bonding.

A FRAGMENT

He had not slept properly in eleven nights. He had lost five pounds without trying. He had taken to walking past her building on his way to anywhere else, adding twenty minutes to ordinary errands. His friends, who were patient with him, had noticed that he laughed more often and that he was a worse listener. His sister, who knew the signs, had asked him gently not to make any large decisions for at least three months. He had nodded. He had then, that same week, looked at apartments together with her. The condition that had taken him was older than he was. He was the latest of several thousand generations to undergo it, and he was certain, like all of them before him, that what was happening to him was unprecedented in human history.

THE WORD TO KNOW

forelsket

/ˈfɔːrɛlskət/

Norwegian; the past participle of the verb 'forelske seg' (to fall in love), used as both a participle and an adjective to describe the early state of being newly in love. The construction is closely paralleled in Danish ('forelsket') and Swedish ('förälskad'), all three deriving from a common North Germanic root related to the verb 'elske' (to love), with the prefix 'for-' indicating the inchoative aspect — the entry into the state. The word is in everyday use across the Scandinavian languages and carries a slightly different register than the comparable English periphrasis 'falling in love,' isolating the early phase as a discrete condition with its own characteristic duration and texture.

The early condition of being newly in love, before the relationship has acquired its own gravity and its ordinary problems — the suspended moment between the falling and the having fallen, in which the new neurochemistry is at its peak and the relationship has not yet stabilised into the form it will eventually take. THE NOT SO KNOWN

Helen Fisher

1945 – 2024 · New York, USA

Helen Fisher was the American biological anthropologist whose three-decade research programme on the neuroscience of romantic love and pair bonding produced the most extensive empirical mapping of the forelsket state in any of the world's languages. Working primarily at Rutgers University and at the Kinsey Institute, Fisher conducted a series of fMRI studies in which she placed newly-in-love subjects, long-married subjects, recently-rejected subjects, and grieving subjects into the scanner while showing them photographs of the persons in question, producing the first systematic neural maps of the different romantic conditions. Her books — 'Anatomy of Love' (1992), 'Why We Love' (2004), 'Why Him? Why Her?' (2009) — translated the research for general readers and became the principal popular reference for the neuroscience of romantic relationships.

Fisher's complication is that her academic work was conducted in parallel with a substantial commercial role as Chief Scientific Adviser to the dating service Match.com, a position that brought her research considerable visibility and the financial resources that supported much of the underlying scholarship, but that also exposed her to the recurring critique that the line between science and product marketing had not been kept as clean as the dignity of either required. The substantive findings of her brain-imaging work have, on the whole, held up to subsequent replication; the broader popular framework she built around them has been more contested, with critics arguing that her categories of romantic temperament were too schematic to do the explanatory work she asked of them. The pattern is familiar from many scientist-popularisers: the careful research produces results that the popular synthesis amplifies past the point the data support.

NOW HERE

Vigeland Sculpture Park

Frogner, Oslo, Norway

The largest sculpture park in the world devoted entirely to the work of a single artist, comprising more than two hundred bronze, granite, and wrought-iron sculptures by Gustav Vigeland, installed across thirty hectares of central Oslo between 1924 and Vigeland's death in 1943. The sculptures depict human figures across the full arc of life — infants, children, lovers, parents, the elderly, the dead — at every scale from miniature to monumental. The central monolith, carved from a single block of granite and rising seventeen metres, is composed of 121 entwined human figures climbing toward the sky. The park is one of the principal Oslo civic spaces and is heavily visited at all seasons; the late spring and early summer, when the long northern light extends through the evening and the city's young people gather along the bridges and the lawn, is its forelsket season, in which the sculptures and the living figures around them perform a single continuous study of what it looks like to be a human being newly addressing another human being.

ON THIS DAY

1904

Dublin, Ireland

On 16 June 1904, the young James Joyce, then twenty-two, went walking through Dublin with Nora Barnacle on what is generally taken to have been their first date — an encounter Joyce would memorialise two decades later by setting the entire action of 'Ulysses' on the same calendar date. The day, now commemorated annually in Dublin and in cities of the Joycean diaspora as Bloomsday, is the most precisely-dated forelsket in the history of literature: a single afternoon walk that began a relationship lasting thirty-seven years, that produced two children and a substantial portion of the most influential novel of the twentieth century, and that Joyce continued for the rest of his life to credit as the moment from which everything he wrote took its bearing. The chemistry that visited him on that walk was not different in kind from the chemistry that has visited any of his readers; it was different only in what he eventually did with it.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Have you been forelsket, in the specific sense the Norwegian word names — and looking back, can you distinguish in your memory between that early phase and whatever followed it?

    Did the absence of a clear English word for the early phase make the distinction harder to hold while you were in it? And what decisions did you make during the early phase that you would not have made later, for better or worse?

    If you are currently in such a state, name it to yourself — using the Norwegian word or any other — and notice what changes in your inner relation to it once it has been named as a temporary chemical condition rather than as the permanent shape of your life. If you are not currently in such a state, think of a past instance and try to recover what the state actually felt like from inside it, with the distance of time.

  • 2

    Are there other temporary states in your life — grief, exhaustion, fear, exhilaration — for which you do not have a precise vocabulary, and that you have therefore tended to mistake for permanent features of who you are?

    What would it cost to develop a more accurate vocabulary for your own temporary states? And what would the more accurate vocabulary make possible that the current vagueness does not?

    Pick one temporary state that you experience repeatedly. Find or coin a precise word for it this week. Use the word when the state arrives, even if only privately, and notice whether the precision of the naming changes your relation to the state.

  • 3

    The essay suggests that the conflation of forelsket with long-term love has practical costs in decisions made under the influence of temporary neurochemistry. Have you made — or watched someone make — a significant decision under the influence of a state you did not realise was temporary?

    What would it have taken, in retrospect, to keep the distinction in view at the time? And does the experience inform how you now hold the difference between current intensity and longer-term consequence?

    Identify one decision you are currently considering. Ask honestly which inner states are currently most active in your reasoning about it, and whether any of those states have a temporary character you have not been fully accounting for. Defer the decision, if you can, for as long as it would take for the state to stabilise.

TEST YOURSELF

Which neurotransmitter is most heavily implicated in the brain-activation patterns observed in fMRI studies of subjects in the early phase of romantic love — the chemical signature that distinguishes the forelsket state from later stages of pair bonding?

The fMRI studies pioneered by Helen Fisher and others have repeatedly identified the ventral tegmental area — the dopamine-producing region of the midbrain whose projections drive the brain's reward and motivation systems — as the central locus of activation in subjects in the early phase of romantic love. The dopaminergic activation pattern is essentially indistinguishable from the pattern observed in cocaine-induced euphoria. Oxytocin and serotonin both play substantial roles in later phases of pair bonding (oxytocin in the longer-term attachment that may follow forelsket, serotonin in the obsessive thinking that characterises the early phase), but the defining neurochemical signature of forelsket itself is the dopaminergic reward-circuit activation.

MOVIE TIME

Reprise

Joachim Trier · 2006

Joachim Trier's debut feature, set in contemporary Oslo, about two young writers in their early twenties — close friends, both ambitious — whose careers diverge after their first novels are submitted to publishers. The film is in part about literary aspiration but its central energy is forelsket in its most specifically Norwegian sense: the early reckless beauty of romantic and intellectual attachments before the slower realities of work, depression, family, and time have begun to sort them. Trier shoots Oslo as a city in the same condition — late-spring light, long evenings, the particular northern quality of the air — and the city and the characters perform the same brief illuminated suspended state together.

Game answer

false_cognate

Despite the apparent similarity, the words are not cognate in any useful sense. The Norwegian 'forelsket' derives from the verb 'elske' (to love) with the inchoative prefix 'for-'. The English 'forsaken' derives from the Old English verb 'forsacan' (to renounce, give up), with a completely unrelated root from a Proto-Germanic verb meaning 'to deny' or 'to refuse.' The prefix 'for-' is genuinely common to both Germanic languages but functions as a productive prefix in many independent compounds and is not evidence of shared root.
