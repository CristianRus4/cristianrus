The Obligation to Repair What Is Broken in the World

In the Kabbalistic tradition that developed in the small Galilean town of Safed in the sixteenth century, the creation of the world is described in terms that other religious cosmologies have not generally proposed. Before the creation, the divine fullness occupied the entirety of being; in order to make space for a world distinct from itself, the divine withdrew into itself, leaving a void. Into the void, divine light was poured. But the vessels that were to contain the light were not strong enough; they shattered, and the divine light scattered as sparks across the broken fragments of the world. The world we inhabit is, on this account, a world made of broken vessels and scattered light. The work of human life is to gather the sparks, repair the vessels, and return the world to its intended wholeness.

Tikkun olam is the Hebrew phrase for this work: literally, 'repair of the world.' The phrase is older than the Lurianic Kabbalah that gave it its most fully developed cosmological setting — it appears in the Mishnah, the early rabbinic legal compilation completed around 200 CE, in the more limited sense of ordinances enacted for the general welfare of the social order. The Kabbalistic recasting in the sixteenth century made the phrase the central organising concept of a complete spiritual cosmology, and the twentieth-century recasting, particularly within the Reform and Conservative branches of American Judaism, made it the central organising concept of a programme of Jewish social-justice ethics. The same two words have done very different work in each of these contexts.

What is interesting about the concept, considered in its full historical range, is that it proposes a particular relation between the cosmic and the ordinary. The shattered vessels are everywhere. The scattered sparks are in every encounter. The work of tikkun is not reserved for great occasions; it is performed in the small daily acts by which a person attends to the broken aspects of the immediate world they inhabit. The Kabbalistic tradition went so far as to say that each blessing recited over food, each act of kindness to a stranger, each refusal of the easy unkindness, contributes a measurable quantity to the repair of the cosmos. The metaphysics is large; the practical application is small and continuous.

What this opens up, considered from outside the Jewish tradition, is a different relation between the individual moral act and the larger condition of the world. The dominant Western secular ethical framework, particularly in its consequentialist forms, has tended to treat individual moral acts as units to be summed and to evaluate the resulting sum against some standard of overall welfare. The tikkun framework treats individual moral acts as participants in a larger ongoing work — the repair of a world that no single person and no single generation can complete — and the question is not whether a given act produces a measurable improvement but whether the act contributes to the larger project that the act would be insufficient to complete on its own.

There is, in this, a particular kind of consolation for the person engaged in difficult work whose visible effects are small. The participant in tikkun olam does not have to demonstrate that their individual acts have repaired the world. The participant has to demonstrate, to themselves and to the tradition, that their acts have contributed to the work that the work is. The metric is not the size of the visible result. The metric is the fidelity of the participation. This framing makes possible a sustained engagement with problems whose scale exceeds any individual's capacity to address — climate, injustice, suffering — that the more results-oriented frameworks have trouble maintaining over the long durations such problems require.

What resists in the popular contemporary deployment of tikkun olam, particularly in the American liberal-Jewish use that has dominated the phrase's circulation since the 1970s, is the temptation to flatten the cosmological depth of the older tradition into a generic call for progressive political activism. The flattening is not an accident; it has been useful, politically, to deploy a deep theological concept in support of contemporary social-justice projects, and the deployment has done real work in mobilising Jewish communities around causes that the strictly observant tradition might not have prioritised. But the flattening also empties the concept of much of what made it intellectually serious in its original setting, and the cost of the political utility has been a partial loss of the metaphysical seriousness that gave the phrase its weight in the first place.

THE TENSION

Tikkun olam offers a genuinely useful framework for engagement with problems whose scale exceeds any individual's capacity to solve: the participant is not asked to demonstrate that their acts have produced measurable improvement, but to demonstrate that their acts have contributed to a larger ongoing work. This framing makes possible a sustained engagement with climate, injustice, and suffering that more results-oriented ethics has difficulty maintaining over the long durations such problems require.

The cosmic framing of tikkun olam, particularly in its popularised contemporary form, can become a sophisticated permission to substitute the feeling of moral participation for the harder work of producing measurable change. The participant who believes themselves to be repairing the cosmos may find it easier to ignore the question of whether their particular acts are actually accomplishing anything in the local human terms in which most moral problems are eventually adjudicated.

THE NUMBER

613

Traditional count of mitzvot — the commandments enumerated in the Torah and elaborated through the rabbinic tradition — each of which is understood, in the Kabbalistic interpretation, as an opportunity to gather sparks of divine light scattered through the broken vessels of the world. The number is first given systematically by the eleventh-century Talmudist Simlai and remains the standard count across the subsequent twelve centuries of Jewish religious literature.

A FRAGMENT

The old man kept the small back garden as carefully as a temple. He had been doing it for forty years. He did not consider the work to be exceptional. He did consider it to be necessary. He had once tried to explain to a visitor that the wall he was repointing, the tomato vines he was tying, the small patch of lavender he was keeping clear of bindweed, were each a small act of repair in a world that he had come to think of as substantially in need of it. The visitor had nodded politely. He had not pressed the point. The work, in any case, did not require the visitor's agreement in order to continue.

THE WORD TO KNOW

tikkun olam

/tiˈkun oˈlam/ — תיקון עולם

Hebrew; literally 'repair of the world' or 'mending of the world.' The phrase appears first in the Mishnah, the early rabbinic legal compilation completed around 200 CE, where it is used in the more limited technical sense of ordinances enacted 'for the sake of the order of the world' — measures taken by the rabbinic court to address situations that the strict letter of the law had not anticipated. The phrase was substantially recast in the sixteenth century by Isaac Luria and his disciples in the Galilean town of Safed, who made it the central concept of a cosmological account of creation, shattering, and repair. The twentieth-century recasting, particularly within American Reform and Conservative Judaism since the 1970s, has made it the central organising phrase of a Jewish social-justice ethics whose relationship to the older Kabbalistic source has been contested by both traditionalist and progressive Jewish thinkers.

The work of repairing the broken aspects of the world — understood in the Kabbalistic tradition as the gathering of the scattered divine sparks released when the original vessels of creation shattered, and in the contemporary social-justice tradition as the active engagement with conditions of injustice and suffering as a religiously grounded obligation. Names both the cosmic project and the daily acts by which the project is incrementally pursued.

Rabbi Tarfon · Pirkei Avot (Sayings of the Fathers), Chapter 2, Mishnah 16, c. 200 CE

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks

1948 – 2020 · London, United Kingdom

Jonathan Sacks was the British rabbi, philosopher, and public intellectual who served as Chief Rabbi of the United Hebrew Congregations of the Commonwealth from 1991 to 2013 and who, across approximately four decades of writing and public lecturing, became one of the most internationally read Jewish theologians of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries. His books — 'The Dignity of Difference' (2002), 'To Heal a Fractured World' (2005), 'Morality' (2020) — extended the older Jewish ethical and theological tradition into engagement with contemporary questions of pluralism, political polarisation, religious extremism, and the conditions of liberal democracy. His prose was unusually accessible for a writer of his theological depth, and the books reached substantial non-Jewish readerships in addition to the Jewish audience for whom they were primarily written.

Sacks's complication is the tension he occupied for his entire public career between the orthodox tradition from which he came and the more pluralistic public role he chose to perform — a tension that produced, periodically, significant difficulties with both the strictly orthodox communities for whom his ecumenism was suspect and the more liberal Jewish communities for whom his theological orthodoxy was insufficient. He navigated the tension with substantial diplomatic and intellectual skill, but the cost of the navigation was visible in the substantial number of subjects he could not write about as fully as the underlying intellectual resources would have permitted. He died in November 2020 with several of his most ambitious projects unfinished, including the planned final volume of his five-volume Covenant and Conversation series of commentaries on the Torah, which other hands have since attempted to complete from the manuscripts and notes he left behind.

NOW HERE

The Old City of Safed (Tzfat)

Upper Galilee, Northern District, Israel

A small hilltop town in the upper Galilee, at approximately 900 metres elevation, with stone buildings arranged along the contours of a steep ridge and an old synagogue quarter — the Ari Synagogue, the Abuhav Synagogue, the Beit Yosef — preserving the architectural setting in which the sixteenth-century Kabbalistic revival took place. The town was, in the decades immediately following the Spanish expulsion of 1492, one of the principal destinations of the displaced Jewish scholarly community, and the concentration in Safed during the second half of the sixteenth century of Isaac Luria, Joseph Karo (the codifier of the Shulchan Arukh), Moses Cordovero, and Shlomo Alkabetz produced one of the most intellectually productive single decades in the entire history of Jewish thought. The narrow blue-painted alleys of the old quarter are still walked by visiting scholars, and the city remains a centre of Kabbalistic study and practice, with a continuous tradition extending from Luria's time to the present.

ON THIS DAY

1340

The Channel, off Sluys (present-day Zeeland, Netherlands)

On 24 June 1340, the English navy under Edward III destroyed the French fleet in the harbour at Sluys, in one of the largest naval engagements of the European Middle Ages and the opening major battle of what would become known as the Hundred Years' War. The English victory secured control of the Channel for the next several decades and made possible the long English military presence in northern France that would culminate, three generations later, in the campaigns of Henry V. The war that began effectively at Sluys would last, intermittently, for one hundred and sixteen years, and the world the war made was substantially the broken world that the contemporary Kabbalistic tradition in Safed, two centuries later, would describe in its account of the scattered sparks and the obligation to repair. The image of broken vessels and scattered light, while expressed in metaphysical language, was not unconnected to the actual condition of a continent that had been at intermittent war within itself for the previous several hundred years.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Where in your own life do you participate in work whose scale exceeds your individual capacity to complete it — and how do you maintain engagement with such work over the long durations it requires?

    Does the tikkun framing — that fidelity of participation rather than measurable result is the appropriate metric — describe how you actually relate to such work, or is your relation governed by a more results-oriented framework that struggles to maintain itself over time? And which framework, on reflection, has served you better?

    Identify one piece of work in your life whose completion you will not see. Spend an hour this week deliberately reframing your participation in it from the standpoint of completion to the standpoint of contribution. Notice whether the reframe makes the work easier to continue or makes the absence of visible result easier to bear, and decide on the basis of the experiment whether to maintain the reframe.

  • 2

    The essay distinguishes between the cosmological depth of the older tikkun tradition and the flattened contemporary deployment of the phrase as a generic call for progressive activism. Have you encountered concepts in your own field that have undergone a similar flattening — depth lost in the service of political utility?

    Is the flattening always a loss, or are there cases in which the popular use of a deep concept does work that the original depth could not have done? And where in your own thinking have you participated in either the flattening or the recovery?

    Pick one concept you regularly use whose popular contemporary form differs substantially from its original or technical sense. Spend an hour with a careful account of the original sense — a primary text, a careful scholarly treatment. Notice what you have been operating without, and whether the recovery changes your subsequent use of the term.

  • 3

    Rabbi Tarfon's saying — that it is not your obligation to complete the work but you are not at liberty to desist from it — proposes a particular relation to incompletable tasks. How does this proposal sit with your own approach to the large projects of your life?

    Have you ever desisted from work you knew you were not at liberty to abandon, and what was the cost of the desistance? And have you ever continued work you knew you would not complete, and what has the continuation revealed about what such work is actually for?

    Identify one task in your life that you have been treating as either completable (when it is not) or as desistable (when it is not). Take one concrete step this week toward an accurate relation to it — either by accepting the long incompleteness or by recommitting to the work — and notice what changes when the relation is honest.

TEST YOURSELF

The Kabbalistic doctrine of the shattering of the divine vessels and the scattering of the sparks, which provides the most fully developed cosmological setting for the concept of tikkun olam, is most closely associated with which sixteenth-century rabbi of Safed?

Isaac Luria (1534–1572) — known by the acronym 'the Ari' — was the Kabbalist who developed in Safed, during the few short years between his arrival there in 1569 and his early death in 1572, the system of mystical cosmology that became known as Lurianic Kabbalah. The doctrine of the contraction (tzimtzum), the breaking of the vessels (shevirat ha-kelim), and the gathering of the sparks (tikkun) is the most influential single articulation of the metaphysical framework in which tikkun olam acquired its contemporary depth. Luria himself wrote very little; the doctrine was transmitted and elaborated by his disciples, principally Hayyim Vital, whose 'Etz Chayim' is the standard exposition of the Lurianic system.

Letter by letter

The Japanese art of repairing broken pottery by mending the breaks with lacquer dusted or mixed with powdered gold, silver, or platinum — a practice that treats the breakage and its repair as part of the object's history, to be incorporated visibly into the restored piece rather than disguised. The aesthetic and the tikkun tradition are not historically connected but rhyme in their underlying conviction: that the broken object's wholeness is recoverable through visible, deliberate work that does not pretend the breakage did not happen.

MOVIE TIME

Ushpizin

Giddi Dar · 2004

A poor Hasidic couple in a working-class Jerusalem neighbourhood prepare for the festival of Sukkot, when traditional Jewish hospitality requires the welcoming of guests (ushpizin) into the temporary booths erected outside the home. When two visitors arrive — escaped convicts, as it turns out, posing as observant Jews — the couple's hospitality and their faith are tested across the week of the festival. The film, written by Shuli Rand (who plays the husband) and directed by Giddi Dar, is one of the few mainstream Israeli films to attempt a sympathetic portrait of the Haredi community from inside its own theological and practical assumptions, and the work the central couple does across the film — of patient, sometimes exasperated, hospitality toward guests who are not what they seem — is a small concrete instance of the tikkun the essay describes.
