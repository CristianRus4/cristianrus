The Truth a Household Agrees Not to Name

Every long-running household contains at least one fact that everyone in it knows and that no one in it says. The drinker who is no longer pretending to drink less. The marriage that has been over for years and continues as a logistical arrangement. The grown child whose ambitions everyone has privately written off. The grandparent whose memory is going faster than the family will admit. The shared agreement is not silence — silence would be too clean. The agreement is to talk around the fact in such a way that everyone present registers that everyone else has registered it, while no one allows the registration to become spoken.

The anthropologists who first studied a culture in which this practice was institutionalised gave it a word borrowed from a small language spoken in the Trobriand Islands. Mokita: the truth that everyone knows but nobody mentions. The Kivila speakers from whom the word was taken did not, as far as the record shows, regard the practice as pathological. It was simply the form social knowledge took when speaking it aloud would do more damage than its continued unsayability. The word names a positive technology rather than a failure of communication.

What the word makes visible — and what is harder to see without it — is that mokita requires considerable skill to maintain. A successful unsaid truth is not the same as ignorance. Everyone present must know the fact, must know that everyone else knows it, must know that everyone else knows that they know, and must conduct themselves accordingly through the surface of the conversation. The household with a mokita is operating on two layers simultaneously: the explicit topic, and the topic that organises which explicit topics are admissible. The work of maintaining the second layer is invisible and constant, and most family members would describe it as 'the way we are' rather than as the active social labour it is.

There are reasons mokita persists, and not all of them are pathological. Some truths, said aloud, do destroy what they describe. A mother who states plainly that her son has wasted his thirties forecloses the small narrow channel through which a change might still arrive. A husband who names the affair his wife has been conducting with a particular friend forecloses the possibility of its quiet exhaustion. The unsaid truth is sometimes the protection of a future that the spoken truth would amputate. The household that maintains certain things in mokita is sometimes wiser than the household that prides itself on its commitment to total honesty.

And yet the cost is also real. The energy required to maintain a mokita is not free. The longer a fact has been unsaid, the more elaborate the system of conversational redirection becomes, until eventually entire topics — entire rooms, entire holidays, entire decades — are organised primarily around the structural avoidance. The household begins to lose access to the topics adjacent to the mokita, because the avoidance bleeds outward, and the conversational space contracts. The truth that was protected from being said also stops being thought about, and what was a deliberate kindness becomes a slow narrowing of the family's ability to be present with itself.

What resists in all of this is the binary between honesty and dishonesty in which the modern therapeutic vocabulary tends to deal. Mokita is neither. It is a third category of social knowledge — knowledge that is shared and active and used, but not articulated — and the question is not whether to admit it or to deny it but whether the particular instance is the wise kind of unsaying or the slow corrosive kind. The household has to read its own weather. Some truths are best kept in the air between people. Others, kept there long enough, eventually become the air itself, and the air becomes hard to breathe.

THE TENSION

Mokita is often the wisest form a difficult truth can take, and the cult of radical honesty that has come to dominate therapeutic culture badly underestimates how much human relationship is held together by what is mutually known and mutually unsaid. A great deal of family functioning is built on the assumption that some things will be registered without being articulated, and the demand that every fact be made explicit treats relationships as if they were legal contracts rather than living ecologies.

Mokita becomes a slow violence when it is allowed to compound across years, and the appearance of wisdom in any particular instance of it should not be confused with the wisdom of the practice as a structural habit. The household that maintains too many unsaid truths loses access to whole categories of speech, and what looks like restraint is often the avoidance of a difficulty that, if faced, would have produced more honest connection than its endless circumnavigation has preserved.

A FRAGMENT

The dinner table held the empty place where she would have been sitting and no one looked at it. The conversation worked its way carefully around her name. The wine glass at her place had been removed in advance by someone, anonymously, before anyone arrived. They had been doing this for two years. They were going to do it for many more. What they were preserving was harder to name than her absence, and harder to say than what had caused it, and they had agreed without ever agreeing that the preservation was worth what it cost.

THE WORD TO KNOW

mokita

/moˈkita/

Kivila (also written Kilivila), the Austronesian language spoken in the Trobriand Islands of Papua New Guinea, first systematically studied by the anthropologist Bronisław Malinowski during his fieldwork there between 1915 and 1918. The word entered the broader anthropological literature in the mid-twentieth century as one of a small set of terms used to describe forms of social knowledge that English does not lexically isolate. It has since circulated in management theory, family-systems therapy, and political analysis, where it names something that the speakers of larger languages have not previously thought of as a discrete object.

The truth that every person in a group knows, that every person knows that every other person knows, and that no one mentions — sustained through coordinated conversational avoidance rather than through ignorance or denial. A form of shared social knowledge maintained in active circulation through what is not said, requiring continuous and often considerable skill from all participants to preserve.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Bronisław Malinowski

1884 – 1942 · Kraków, Poland / New Haven, USA

Bronisław Malinowski was the Polish-British anthropologist whose four years of fieldwork in the Trobriand Islands during the First World War established what became the dominant method of modern social anthropology: participant observation conducted in the language of the studied community, with the researcher living at length inside the social world being described rather than passing through it as a visitor. His major works — 'Argonauts of the Western Pacific' (1922), 'The Sexual Life of Savages' (1929), 'Coral Gardens and Their Magic' (1935) — established the Trobriand Islanders as one of the most fully documented small-scale societies in the ethnographic record, and the kula ring he described as the foundational case study in the anthropology of exchange.

Malinowski's complication is that the posthumous publication of his fieldwork diaries in 1967 revealed a man whose private writing contained casual racism and ethnographic frustration that contradicted the apparently sympathetic tone of his published work. The diaries provoked a long disciplinary reckoning that continues today: about the gap between the field researcher's recorded observations and the lived reality of the people observed, about the colonial conditions under which classical anthropology was conducted, and about whether the foundational texts of the discipline rest on a methodological honesty their author did not in fact practise. The work remains canonical; the man, complicated; and the discipline he helped found has spent the half-century since trying to understand what to do with both.

NOW HERE

Kiriwina Island

Trobriand Islands, Milne Bay Province, Papua New Guinea

The largest of the Trobriand Islands, a low coral-and-limestone island approximately forty kilometres long, surrounded by reef and lagoon, with a population that has continued to speak Kivila and to maintain versions of the kula ring exchange that Malinowski documented a century ago. The island has electricity in two villages and intermittent satellite internet at the small airstrip outside Losuia; otherwise the rhythm of the day is set by the tides, the gardens, and the obligations of the matrilineal kinship structure that organises domestic life. Anthropologists from Europe, Australia, and the United States still come — fewer than they did in the 1970s — and the islanders continue to receive them with the particular Trobriand mixture of generosity and amused observation that the longer-term visitors come eventually to recognise as itself a form of ethnographic intelligence.

ON THIS DAY

1989

Tiananmen Square, Beijing, China

On 4 June 1989, the People's Liberation Army cleared the protesters who had occupied Tiananmen Square for seven weeks, killing an undetermined number of civilians — estimates range from several hundred to several thousand — in the surrounding streets of central Beijing. The event has been the subject of one of the most successful state-organised mokitas in modern history: in mainland China, the date is not commemorated, the body count is not published, the iconic photograph of the man standing before the column of tanks is not available through standard search engines, and a generation of Chinese citizens has grown to adulthood inside a household where the truth of what happened is collectively known and collectively unsaid. The Trobriand word the essay uses, lifted from a small community in Melanesia, names what an industrial state has been able to maintain across a population of more than a billion for more than three decades.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Is there a mokita in your family of origin — a truth everyone there has known for years and that no one has named — that you can identify clearly without difficulty?

    Do you consider the unsaying of it to be a wisdom that protects something important, or a slow corrosion that has narrowed what your family can talk about? And on what basis are you making that judgment — yours, theirs, or some imagined neutral observer's?

    Without changing anything, simply notice this week how the conversation in your family of origin (in person, on the phone, in a text thread) routes itself around the mokita you have identified. Track the moves: which topics are veered away from, which words substitute, who maintains the redirection most actively.

  • 2

    Have you ever broken a mokita — said the unsaid thing in a room where everyone knew it — and watched the consequences play out?

    Did the breaking accomplish what you had imagined, or did it have effects you had not predicted? And looking back, would you make the same choice with the knowledge you have now?

    If you are currently considering breaking a mokita, write down two predictions: what you imagine happening immediately, and what you imagine happening a month after. Do not act on the basis of the writing alone. But hold the predictions where you can find them later if you do choose to act.

  • 3

    Where in your professional or institutional life — your team, your company, your field — does mokita do load-bearing work that nobody acknowledges?

    Is the institution more functional or less functional for the unsaid truth being maintained? And is anyone in the institution currently paying the cost of the maintenance disproportionately, in a way the unsaying itself helps to obscure?

    Identify one professional mokita you participate in. Notice over the next week whether your relationship to it is active maintenance or passive participation, and whether you would defend the unsaying if someone newer to the room asked you why no one mentions it.

TEST YOURSELF

Which anthropologist's fieldwork in the Trobriand Islands during the First World War established the methodology of long-term participant observation in the local language that became the standard for twentieth-century social anthropology?

Bronisław Malinowski conducted fieldwork in the Trobriand Islands of what is now Papua New Guinea between 1915 and 1918, having been stranded there during the First World War as a citizen of the Austro-Hungarian Empire in a British colony. His method — living in the village for years, learning Kivila, recording daily life rather than only ceremonial occasions — became the foundational template for modern ethnography. His major works on Trobriand exchange, kinship, and material culture remain among the most cited ethnographic texts of the twentieth century, and the word 'mokita' entered the broader anthropological vocabulary through the Trobriand material he and his successors documented.

MOVIE TIME

Festen (The Celebration)

Thomas Vinterberg · 1998

A sixtieth-birthday gathering of a wealthy Danish family interrupted when the eldest son, in a toast at dinner, names the abuse his father committed decades earlier against him and his twin sister. The first feature film made under the Dogme 95 manifesto, shot on handheld digital video with no artificial light. The film's entire formal apparatus is built around the breaking of a mokita and the long uneven aftermath of the breaking, in which the family does not collapse cleanly into denial or acceptance but spends a single night cycling through every available response while continuing, somehow, to remain in the room.

Game answer

The word 'mokita' is the standard Kivila term for political deception, used to describe statements made by chiefs that everyone privately disbelieves

The lie is the third statement. 'Mokita' does not specifically describe political deception or a chief's statements; it names the broader phenomenon of a shared truth maintained in collective non-mention. The word's entry into English-language anthropology occurred through its description of an ordinary social practice across many contexts of Trobriand life, not as a term of political analysis.
