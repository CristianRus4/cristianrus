The Gesture the Hand Makes When Memory Will Not Arrive

A name does not come. The face of the person whose name has slipped is visible to the rememberer in detail: the small scar above the left eyebrow, the particular way they laughed, the colour of the sweater they had been wearing on the afternoon in question. The name is not there. The rememberer waits. The name remains not there. And here, regardless of culture or training or self-consciousness, the rememberer's hand goes up to the head, and the fingers begin to do a small repetitive gesture — a light scratch, a circular rub at the temple, a tap on the crown — as if the hand had been delegated by the rest of the body to do something about the absence the mind cannot itself address.

Hawaiian has a noun for this gesture. Pana poʻo: literally to strike the head, used for the small repeated touch a person makes to their own scalp when trying to remember something that will not come. The word names the gesture rather than the underlying memory failure, which is an interesting choice. The Hawaiian vocabulary recognises that the failure of memory is one phenomenon and the body's response to that failure is another, and gives a name to the second.

What is interesting about the gesture, considered as a piece of human behaviour, is that it is essentially universal across cultures and ages without ever having been taught. No one has ever instructed a child to scratch their head when trying to remember something. Children begin to do it spontaneously, somewhere around the age at which they begin to be aware of effortful memory retrieval, and continue to do it for the rest of their lives. Anthropologists who have looked for the gesture in cultures geographically isolated from the cultures in which it is well-documented have found it in every population sampled. The gesture appears to be one of those small embodied behaviours — like the shrug, the headshake for negation in many cultures, the smile — that emerge from the human body without requiring cultural transmission, though their precise form may be culturally modulated.

Why the hand should go specifically to the head is a question to which the cognitive scientific literature has offered several partial answers. One is the embodied cognition account: the body knows, in some non-conceptual way, where the memory is supposed to be, and the gesture is the body addressing the seat of the failure. Another is the self-soothing account: the head-scratch is a low-grade displacement behaviour that discharges the tension of an unfinished cognitive task. A third is the social-signalling account: the gesture is a public sign to others in the room that the rememberer is working on something, that the silence has a cause, that the conversation should hold its place rather than move on. None of the three explanations is conclusive. The phenomenon is too small and too distributed to admit a clean experimental design.

What is striking, regardless of the explanation, is that the body responds to a problem of pure information with a physical intervention on its own surface. The piece of information being sought is not in the head; it is in some distributed pattern of activation across the brain that the head as a whole is not in a privileged position to access. The hand's address to the head is metaphorical at best, and yet the metaphor is performed by people who have never read a metaphor in their lives. The folk neuroscience embedded in the gesture — that the memory is in the head and that the head can be encouraged to release it — predates by millennia any scientific account of where memories are or how they are retrieved.

What this suggests, more broadly, is that the body has theories of the mind that it acts on without telling anyone. The vocabulary of cognitive science is largely a vocabulary of analysis from outside; the vocabulary the body uses is performed in millions of small daily gestures that the analytic tradition has not yet caught up to. Pana poʻo is one such gesture. There are many others — the way the eyes drift up and to the left when accessing autobiographical memory, the way the breath catches before the recall of a word, the way the hand traces the air when describing a remembered place. Each of them is a small theory of the mind in action, embodied by people who do not need to know the theory to perform it. The Hawaiian word names one of these gestures and, in naming it, makes the body's quiet expertise briefly visible.

THE TENSION

The pana poʻo gesture is a beautiful instance of embodied cognition: the body addressing a cognitive task through a physical intervention whose efficacy may be modest but whose ubiquity across cultures suggests something real about how human minds and bodies are integrated. To dismiss such gestures as mere displacement activity is to miss the larger pattern of how cognitive work is distributed across the whole organism.

The embodied cognition interpretation has been overextended in recent decades to the point of becoming a rhetorical move, applied to any human behaviour that occurs near the body. A head-scratch during a memory lapse may well be exactly what the displacement-behaviour literature suggests it is — a low-grade discharge of tension — and the move from observation to philosophical claim about the integration of mind and body is not as clean as the popularisers of the embodied turn have implied.

Approximate number of seconds, in the typical 'tip-of-the-tongue' study, that a participant will hold the experience of knowing a word without being able to produce it before either retrieving it or giving up — a duration during which most participants will, without being asked to, perform some version of pana poʻo at least once.

A FRAGMENT

The name had been at the front of her mouth all evening. She had said the man's whole face — the way he had stood at the back of the lecture hall, the umbrella he had carried even on dry days, the woman who had eventually married him whose name was different — and still the name itself had not come. Her hand kept finding her temple of its own accord. Across the table the listener was patient. He recognised the small recurring motion. He had done it himself an hour earlier, looking for a word he had known since he was eight. They sat together waiting for what the body knew and the mind for the moment did not.

THE WORD TO KNOW

pana poʻo

/ˈpana poˈʔo/

Hawaiian; a compound of the verb 'pana' (to strike, to tap, to spring, with a wide semantic range from physical impact to musical pulse) and the noun 'poʻo' (the head). The phrase appears in Hawaiian dictionaries from the nineteenth century onward and in everyday usage to name the specific small gesture of head-touching in moments of effortful recollection. The ʻokina between the o's is a phonemic glottal stop, marked in modern Hawaiian orthography; the kahakō (macron) over the o in 'poʻo' indicates a long vowel.

The small repeated gesture of scratching, tapping, or rubbing one's own head when trying to remember something that has slipped from immediate recall — the body's spontaneous physical response to a problem of pure information. Names the gesture itself rather than the underlying memory failure, recognising the two as distinct phenomena that happen to occur together.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Mary Kawena Pukui

1895 – 1986 · Kaʻū, Hawaiʻi, USA

Mary Kawena Pukui was the Native Hawaiian scholar, translator, composer, and cultural authority whose lifelong work in documenting Hawaiian language, oral tradition, music, and religion is the foundation on which substantially all subsequent Hawaiian-language scholarship rests. Born in 1895 in Kaʻū on the island of Hawaiʻi, raised bilingually, and educated both in the Hawaiian oral tradition by her grandmother and in the Anglo-American academic system that dominated the islands of her century, she worked for more than forty years at the Bishop Museum in Honolulu, where she co-authored, with Samuel Elbert, the Hawaiian Dictionary (1957) that remains the standard reference for the language. Her two-volume collection 'ʻŌlelo Noʻeau: Hawaiian Proverbs & Poetical Sayings' (1983) preserves approximately three thousand traditional sayings that would otherwise have been lost with their last fluent speakers.

Pukui's complication is that she conducted her career during the period of greatest active suppression of the Hawaiian language by the Territorial and State governments — a period during which the language was prohibited in public schools, its speakers actively discouraged from passing it to their children, and its institutional position systematically dismantled. She produced the dictionaries, the song collections, the proverb compilations, and the ethnographic interviews not as an academic in a thriving discipline but as a salvage operation conducted by a small number of native speakers against the active policy of the state in which they lived. The Hawaiian language revival of the past four decades — including the Hawaiian-medium school system that now exists across the islands — is built substantially on the foundation she and her collaborators put in place during a period when no one in power expected the language to survive at all.

NOW HERE

Hālau Hula at Hāʻena State Park

Kauaʻi, Hawaiʻi, USA

A traditional hālau hula — a hula school — operating at the edge of the dry cliffs at Hāʻena on the north shore of Kauaʻi, the easternmost site associated in Hawaiian oral tradition with the goddess Laka and the origin of the hula. The practice of hula in this place is a continuous tradition of embodied remembering: the chants are not memorised as texts but learned through the body, and the choreography itself is a mnemonic device for the genealogies, geographies, and ceremonial knowledge that the oral tradition has carried across centuries. The students, on a given afternoon at the foot of the cliffs, are doing through years of patient practice what the head-scratching of pana poʻo does in miniature — using the body to hold and to access what the mind alone could not preserve.

ON THIS DAY

1184 BCE

Troy (Hisarlık, modern Turkey)

On 11 June 1184 BCE, according to the calculation that Eratosthenes of Cyrene proposed in the third century BCE — and which served for centuries as the standard ancient chronology for the event — the city of Troy fell to the Achaean Greeks after a ten-year siege. The historical accuracy of the Trojan War as related in Homer remains contested, with archaeology suggesting a real Late Bronze Age destruction of the city at approximately this period but offering no evidence either way as to the names, characters, or specific events of the Homeric narrative. What the calculation by Eratosthenes records is something subtler than a fact: the ancient Greek attempt to fix in their own collective memory, by means of an exact date, an event whose precise details had long since become irrecoverable but whose centrality to who they were could not be allowed to fade into vagueness. The date is itself a kind of pana poʻo — a hand on the head of the culture, marking the place where the memory was supposed to be.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    What small embodied gestures do you perform regularly without having been taught them — and what do those gestures suggest about the theories of the mind your body has been operating on?

    Are there gestures you have noticed yourself doing that you cannot account for at all, and that you have never seen anyone else do? And are those gestures genuinely idiosyncratic, or merely local variations on a more widely shared embodied vocabulary you have not yet noticed in others?

    Watch your own hands and head for one day, with no agenda except to notice what they do during conversations, during silences, during effortful thinking. Make a brief list at the end of the day. Notice how much of your physical behaviour you had been unaware of.

  • 2

    When you encounter a memory gap — a name that will not come, a passage that will not surface — what is your habitual response, and is it productive of the memory you are looking for?

    Do you have strategies that you have developed deliberately for retrieving lost information, or do you rely on the body's spontaneous gestures and the gradual surfacing the mind will sometimes accomplish on its own? And how do you feel about the gaps that never close?

    The next time a name or word will not come, do not look it up. Sit with the gap for at least ten minutes. Notice what your body does, what kinds of associations begin to drift past, and whether the gap eventually closes or remains. Notice also what the willingness to leave the gap open allows that the immediate lookup forecloses.

  • 3

    The Hawaiian word names the gesture rather than the underlying memory failure. Are there other small everyday phenomena in your life that you would benefit from naming the embodied response to rather than the cause?

    What changes when you name the body's response rather than the underlying problem? And what is gained or lost by attending to the response first?

    Pick one recurring small distress in your life — boredom, mild anxiety, restlessness, frustration with a particular task. Spend a day attending not to the cause but to the small physical things you do in response, and see whether the attention to the bodily response changes the experience of the underlying state.

TEST YOURSELF

The Hawaiian language belongs to which broader language family, whose other members include Māori, Samoan, Tahitian, and the languages of the Marquesas, Tonga, and Easter Island?

Hawaiian belongs to the Polynesian branch of the Austronesian language family, one of the most geographically widespread language families in the world, stretching from Madagascar across maritime Southeast Asia to the eastern edge of the Pacific. The Polynesian subfamily includes the closely related languages of Hawaiʻi, Aotearoa (Māori), Samoa, Tahiti, Tonga, the Marquesas, and Rapa Nui (Easter Island), among others. The shared core vocabulary — words for fundamental concepts such as 'sky,' 'water,' 'fish,' and 'house' — remains recognisably cognate across these languages despite the enormous oceanic distances separating them.

MOVIE TIME

Amour

Michael Haneke · 2012

An elderly retired music teacher in Paris cares for his wife as she declines after a series of strokes that progressively dismantle her memory and her ability to speak. The film holds, with characteristic patience, on the small gestures her body continues to make even as the named content of her memory recedes: the hand reaching toward a familiar object, the head turning at a remembered voice. Haneke does not sentimentalise. The film offers no recovery and no consolation, only the long observation of what a body retains when the mind has begun to release what the body once knew with it.
