The Place an Animal Returns to Under Pressure

In the bullring, a torero in the second quarter of the corrida watches the bull, very carefully, for a specific small detail of behaviour that the rest of the audience does not yet have the eyes to see. The bull, on its own without provocation, will choose one particular position in the ring and will keep returning to it. The position has nothing distinctive about it visually. It is simply a point on the sand. But the bull has, over the early minutes of the fight, identified it as the location to which the bull's own body, under the pressure of the engagement, instinctively orients. The point is the bull's querencia. To fight the bull intelligently from this point onward, the torero has to know which point it is and to manage the bull's movements relative to it.

Querencia is the Spanish noun for this. The word derives from the verb 'querer,' which in modern Spanish means to want or to love but in older Spanish carried the broader sense of inner attachment or affinity. The bullfighting use is the most technically precise, but the word has a substantial life outside the bullring. A querencia is the place an animal returns to under pressure; by extension, the place a person returns to when their own resources have been depleted; by further extension, the inner sense of where one is most fully oneself, which one carries with one through any external displacement. The word names, in its full range, the location at which the bearer's nervous system can rest into something it does not have to actively maintain.

What is interesting about the bullfighting sense, considered carefully, is that the querencia is not chosen by the bull through any process resembling deliberation. The bull does not select the point as one prefers a particular bench in a park. The querencia emerges, in the early minutes of the corrida, through the bull's instinctive response to the conditions of the ring — the angle of the light, the proximity to the chute through which the bull entered, the local micro-conditions of the sand, the position from which the bull first felt itself unobserved by the torero. The point selects itself, in a sense, through the bull's body. The bull does not know it has chosen a querencia. The torero, who has trained for years to read the small behavioural signs, knows.

The metaphorical extension into human psychology has been worked out most thoroughly by the American naturalist Barry Lopez, whose late writing returned several times to the term as a name for the inner place a person locates themselves through under conditions of difficulty. The querencia, in Lopez's usage, is not a literal geographic place — though it can be, for some people — but a kind of psychological coordinate, a felt sense of where the bearer's nervous system can return to in order to be unguarded. A person who has identified their own querencia can locate it under conditions of stress and can use the location as a baseline from which to assess the surrounding pressures. A person who has not identified theirs can spend a great deal of energy under stress without knowing what they are looking for.

What this opens up is a different framing of the question of where one belongs. The Anglo-Saxon vocabulary tends to organise belonging around external categories — nationality, profession, family role, residence — and to treat any tension between these external belongings and the bearer's inner sense as a problem requiring resolution. The Spanish noun proposes a different unit of belonging: the place, internal or external, to which the body involuntarily returns under pressure. This unit is not always congruent with the external belongings. A person can hold passports from one country and a profession from another and still have, as their querencia, a single childhood kitchen they have not entered in thirty years. The querencia is not chosen and cannot be argued with. It is the body's own answer to where it is willing to rest.

What remains is the practical question of how to find one's querencia, particularly for people whose lives have been displaced from the conditions in which the natural one might have formed. The bull in the ring is a useful model only up to a point: the bull's querencia emerges over minutes within a single bounded space, and the bull does not have to find it deliberately because the bull's nervous system finds it on the bull's behalf. The human seeker, particularly the displaced one, often has to do conscious work to identify what the body would have located unconsciously if the conditions had permitted. The work is not glamorous. It usually involves long attention to which places and conditions and people produce in the bearer the small involuntary relaxation that the querencia is. The work is worth doing, because the querencia, once located, is one of the few things the surrounding world cannot easily take away.

THE TENSION

Identifying one's querencia is a substantial piece of psychological self-knowledge that the dominant external categories of belonging tend to obscure. The body's involuntary baseline is more reliable than the social categories it is supposed to fit, and a person who has located theirs has access to a form of inner stability that the contested external belongings of contemporary life cannot supply.

The valorisation of the querencia as a private inner location risks providing a sophisticated alibi for withdrawal from the genuine political and relational work of contested belonging. The bearer can retreat into their identified querencia rather than engage with the imperfect external belongings that actually require their continued attention, and the language of self-care has at times been deployed in exactly this way.

A FRAGMENT

She had moved seven times in the previous decade and the apartment she was packing was indistinguishable, room for room, from the apartments she had inhabited before it. What she could not pack, and what kept her sitting on the floor of the half-empty kitchen at three in the morning, was the absence of the corner of her grandmother's living room — a corner she had not sat in for twenty-six years, in a house that had been sold and demolished. She had spent the previous decade trying to recreate the corner in a series of new cities and had not succeeded. She understood, sitting on the floor, that this was what she had been doing. She understood that the corner was not a place she would arrive at by moving. She had begun to suspect she would have to find it without it.

THE WORD TO KNOW

querencia

/keˈɾenθja/

Spanish; from the verb 'querer' (to want, to love, to wish, with the older and broader sense of inner attachment or affinity). The noun's specific use in bullfighting terminology is documented from at least the eighteenth century in the Spanish taurine treatises that codified the conventions of the modern corrida. The metaphorical extension of the word into human psychology, particularly through the writings of the American naturalist Barry Lopez and through several twentieth-century Spanish writers, has produced a contemporary use considerably broader than the technical bullfighting sense, encompassing any felt place — internal or external — to which the bearer's nervous system involuntarily returns under pressure.

The place an animal returns to under pressure; by extension, the inner location — physical or psychological — to which a person involuntarily orients when their own resources have been depleted, where the nervous system can rest into something it does not have to actively maintain. Names not a chosen home but the body's own answer to where it is willing to be unguarded, often discoverable only through attentive self-observation under conditions of difficulty.

THE NOT SO KNOWN

Barry Lopez

1945 – 2020 · Port Chester, New York, USA / Finn Rock, Oregon, USA

Barry Lopez was the American naturalist, essayist, and short-story writer whose 1986 book 'Arctic Dreams' won the National Book Award and substantially redefined the possibilities of long-form American nature writing for the generation that came of age after Annie Dillard and Wendell Berry. Lopez worked across approximately five decades — books, essays, fiction, photographic collaborations — at a quality of sustained moral seriousness about the human relation to the non-human world that very few other American writers of his generation achieved. His later work, particularly the 2019 essay collection 'Horizon,' returned repeatedly to the concept of querencia as one of the central organising terms of his thinking about belonging in the natural world.

Lopez's complication, in the last several years of his life, was the public revelation that he had been sexually abused as a child by a family acquaintance — an experience he addressed openly in a 2013 essay in Harper's Magazine and that gave a particular weight to the lifelong work he had done on the relationship between humans and the larger natural world they inhabit. The connection between the early trauma and the later vocation was something Lopez himself was prepared to discuss publicly toward the end of his life, and the body of work appears differently in the light of that knowledge — not diminished but more accurately situated as the long achievement of a person who had been required, very early, to do the difficult work of locating an inner querencia capable of holding what the outer world had not been safe enough to hold.

NOW HERE

Mas de la Ferme

Camargue, Bouches-du-Rhône, France

A working ranch of approximately three hundred hectares of marshland on the central plain of the Camargue, in the river delta of the Rhône, where the herds of black bulls used in the courses camarguaises — the bloodless Provençal variant of the bullfight — are bred and kept. The Camargue is one of the most distinctive landscapes in southern Europe: a flat low expanse of saltmarsh, lagoon, and reedbed, with the white horses, the pink flamingos, and the small black bulls that the region has produced for centuries inhabiting an environment that is neither fully terrestrial nor fully aquatic and that has resisted the agricultural intensification that has reorganised most of comparable European landscapes. The bulls, on the open pasture, demonstrate continuously the behaviour from which the bullfighting term querencia was abstracted: each animal has its own preferred location within the wider herd's territory, returns to it across the days, and orients its movements relative to it without the structure of the ring imposing the pattern from outside.

ON THIS DAY

1633

Rome, Italy

On 22 June 1633, the day after his trial before the Roman Inquisition, Galileo Galilei formally recanted his support for the Copernican model of the solar system in a public ceremony at the convent of Santa Maria sopra Minerva in Rome, kneeling before the assembled cardinals to read the prepared text. The sentence imposed required him to remain under house arrest for the rest of his life, initially at the Tuscan embassy in Rome and subsequently at his villa in Arcetri near Florence. The villa at Arcetri became the querencia of his last nine years: the small enclosed space within which the work he could no longer publish openly continued to be conducted, the manuscripts of the 'Discorsi' assembled, the visits from his student Vincenzo Viviani that would carry forward his thinking after his death. The geography of intellectual life under conditions of external pressure has tended to consist of such small places — the prison cell, the country house, the convent retreat — within which the bearer's mind could continue what the public world had foreclosed.

THREE QUESTIONS FOR YOU

  • 1

    Can you identify your own querencia — the place, internal or external, to which you involuntarily orient when your resources have been depleted?

    If you can, when did you first notice it, and how reliable has it been across the changes of your adult life? And if you cannot, do you suspect the absence is because no such place has formed for you, or because you have not yet attended carefully enough to where your body actually returns?

    Over the next week, notice without judging where your body and mind go when you are tired or stressed — not where you wish you went, but where you actually go. Make a brief note each evening. At the end of the week, look for the pattern, and consider whether the location that emerges is the querencia your body has been quietly maintaining.

  • 2

    The essay distinguishes querencia from the external categories of belonging — nationality, profession, family role. Are these aligned or misaligned in your own case, and what has the alignment or misalignment cost you?

    If the inner querencia and the external belongings are misaligned, have you tried to bring them into alignment, and what has the attempt revealed about what is and is not under your control? And which of the two, on reflection, do you trust more as a guide to where you actually belong?

    Write down, in two short lists, the external categories of belonging you would offer if asked, and the inner places (people, conditions, locations) to which you actually return under pressure. Notice the gaps between the two lists, and consider what the gaps suggest about the work of the next decade of your life.

  • 3

    For people whose lives have been displaced from the conditions in which a natural querencia might have formed, the essay suggests the location has to be sought deliberately. Are you among the displaced in this sense, and if so, what has the search looked like?

    Has the search produced a querencia, a substitute for one, or only a clearer understanding of what was lost? And if you have not undertaken the search, would the work be worth doing now even though the natural conditions for its emergence have passed?

    If you suspect you are among the displaced, undertake one small piece of the search this week: a visit to a place that mattered to an earlier version of you, a conversation with a person who remembers an earlier you, a deliberate hour spent in a condition (a kind of light, a kind of food, a kind of music) that produces the involuntary relaxation the querencia carries. Notice what the experiment teaches you.

TEST YOURSELF

The Spanish noun 'querencia' derives from the verb 'querer,' which in modern Spanish has which primary meaning?

The modern Spanish verb 'querer' principally means 'to want' or 'to love' — used both for desires ('quiero un café') and for personal affection ('te quiero'). The noun 'querencia' derives from this verb and carries forward, in its broader semantic field, the older sense of inner attachment or affinity that 'querer' encoded in earlier Spanish. The other options name concepts the noun is sometimes loosely associated with in English translations but that are not the actual etymological root: 'saber' would be 'to know,' 'volver' would be 'to return,' and 'descansar' would be 'to rest.'

MOVIE TIME

Zama

Lucrecia Martel · 2017

An adaptation of Antonio di Benedetto's 1956 novel about a colonial Spanish magistrate stationed in a small Paraguayan settlement at the end of the eighteenth century, waiting for a transfer that never arrives. Martel's film follows the protagonist across years of displacement in which his nominal external identity — the king's officer, the man with the title — has come entirely loose from any inner place to which his depleted body can return. The film is one of the most precise cinematic studies of what it looks like to live without a querencia, in conditions that do not permit one to form, and to attempt nonetheless to construct an inner stability from the wrong materials.

Game answer

tornillo

Tornillo means 'screw' — a piece of hardware. The other four are all Iberian-Peninsula nouns naming aspects of longing or attachment: querencia (the place one returns to), añoranza (the mainstream Spanish word for nostalgic longing), morriña (a Galician term for melancholic homesickness, particularly associated with Galician emigrants), and saudade (the Portuguese and Galician term that has crossed into broader Iberian usage). The semantic field of longing-and-place is unusually well-developed in the Iberian languages, in part because of the long history of migration and exile that has shaped those cultures.
